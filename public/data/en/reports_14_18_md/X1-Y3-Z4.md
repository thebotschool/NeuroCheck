# Cognitive development report
## Analysis of the ability of a student (age: 14–18 years)

This report offers parents and teachers a deep understanding of unique strengths and areas for the development of a student. It contains practical recommendations for creating the most favorable educational environment.

## 1. The main results

Each test reveals the unique aspects of cognitive functions.

*   **Working memory test and strategy (CPT)**
    High ability to concentrate and effective memorization of information with clearly defined tasks. This indicates the significant potential of memory and thinking.

*   **Braking and coordination test (Go/No-Go)**
    The need to improve coordination between the hemispheres of the brain was revealed. This can affect the performance of tasks that require the simultaneous analysis and visualization of information, for example, when working with text and graphs.

*   **Attention test (TCP)**
    Difficulties with long -term retention of the focus were noted. Fast fatigue and distraction can be associated with overload or the influence of the difficulties that have been manifested in the previous test.

## 2. The guide for the success of your student

Understanding an individual learning style is the key to the disclosure of potential.

### Personal approach

*   **The pace of training.** Unevenness is manifested. It takes time to adapt to a new task. High speed of work is possible with the interest and clarity of the task, but monotony or multitasking reduces the pace.
*   **Perception of information.** Visual information is most effectively absorbed: schemes, tables, illustrations. Solid text or oral speech is less optimal. It is difficult for simultaneous listening and abstract.
*   **Focus of attention.** Attention is selectively and unstable. Deep immersion is possible in interesting tasks, but routine work or constant switching leads to rapid depletion.

### Optimal conditions

*   Clear step -by -step instructions, breaking major tasks into controlled stages.
*   Visual supports: diagrams, checklists, mental cards, color coding of key information.
*   Unified Information Channel: Focusing at the hearing, viewing or writing separately.
*   Predictability and rhythm: a structured plan of a lesson or day, work with intervals with short breaks (for example, a method of aid).
*   Silence and lack of distracting factors for maximum concentration.

### Which reduces effectiveness

*   Multivativity: for example, simultaneous listening, viewing the board and recording.
*   Frequent change of tasks without enough time to complete the previous thought.
*   Abstract or vague formulations of tasks without clear criteria.
*   Background noise and visual distracting factors.
*   Hurry and pressure related over time.

## 3. Recommendations

An individual approach in the home and school environment.

### Home environment

*   **Homework.**
    *   Break work into short blocks for 15–20 minutes. Turn on 5-minute breaks for physical activity.
    *   Make clear plans for the evening in a checklist format: "1. Follow 5 examples on algebra. 2. Read 3 pages of history. 3. Find 2 images for the report."
    *   Use a timer for complex objects. Install the rule: "20 minutes of work, then rest, regardless of progress."
*   **Support and promotion.**
    *   Mark the efforts, not just the result. Appreciate the ability to take a break and seek help.
    *   Discuss difficulties without condemnation. The phrase "I see that it is difficult, let's think about how to make it easier" more effective than "gather" calls.
*   **Organization of space.**
    *   Provide a quiet, stable workplace. All necessary items should be at hand. Take distracting gadgets from the field of view during the class.

### School environment

*   **The formulation of tasks.**
    *   Provide the tasks step by step, preferably in writing (checklist or pattern on the board).
    *   When explaining the new material, use visual supports: presentations, infographics, schemes. Bind new information to the already known.
*   **Lesson structure and placement.**
    *   The optimal place for the student is behind the first or second desk in the center. This minimizes distraction and maintains visual contact with the teacher.
    *   Report the lesson plan at the beginning of the lesson to create a sense of control and predictability.
*   **Elements of an individual route.**
    *   It is recommended to discuss with the school psychologist the possibility of providing additional time at control work and exams.
    *   Allow the use of drafts and schemes for oral answers to structuring thoughts.
    *   If possible, offer a choice of the format for passing the assignment (for example, project presentation or mental card instead of essay).

## 4. Recommended training

These exercises are aimed at strengthening attention and coordination, based on existing strengths.

*   **Attention**
    *   Board games that require observation of the actions of others: "Uno", "jenga", chess, checkers.
    *   Applications or books with the tasks "Find the differences", labyrinths, puzzles of Sudoku.

*   **Coordination and interhemispheric interaction** (key area for development)
    *   **Exercise "Lokot-Kolono".** Standing, alternately touch the right elbow of the left knee and the left elbow of the right. Perform slowly, 1-2 minutes daily.
    *   **Juggling.** Start with two objects (balls, tangerines), throwing them from hand to hand.
    *   **Playing a musical instrument,** involving both hands (guitar, piano, drums).

*   **Memory**
    *   Games to remember sequences: Electronic game "Simon says", repetition of movements.
    *   Learning of short texts: poems, quotes, excerpts of songs.

*   **Rhythm and bodily control**
    *   Dancing, especially those where it is required to repeat movements for the instructor.
    *   Fitting simple rhythms to the music of the palms on the table or knees.
    *   Balance exercises: stand on one leg (with open/closed eyes), walking along the curb, using a balance sheet.

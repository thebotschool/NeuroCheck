# Cognitive assessment report: Working memory + brake control + self -regulation (14–18 years)

---

## 1. Cognitive profile

The results of the assessment of key cognitive functions.

*   **Visual working memory (CPT):** The confident ability to memorize and maintain a large amount of complex information. This is a strong side in training.
*   **Brake control (Go/No-Go):** High level of pulse control. Actions are accurate and follow the rules when the task is clear and structured.
*   **Attention and impulsiveness (TCP):** In conditions of quick decision -making or emotional stress, there is a tendency to hasty, impulsive actions.

## 2. The guide for the success of your student

A detailed analysis of features and recommendations for optimal learning.

### Features of training

*   **Pace:** Able to work quickly and absorb information. However, impulsiveness can lead to errors in carelessness, especially when the task is completed.
*   **Perception:** The most effectly perceives structured visual information: schemes, tables, checklists, infographics. Oral or continuous text instructions require more effort.
*   **Focus of attention:** Capable of deep concentration on complex and interesting tasks. With uncertainty, boredom or emotional pressure, attention becomes unstable, and behavior becomes impulsive.

### Supports for success

*   **Visualization:** Schemes, algorithms, Mayind cards, marker isolation.
*   **Clear steps:** Great tasks, divided into small, understandable stages with clear criteria.
*   **Predictability:** Clear rules, a stable schedule, pre -known requirements.
*   **Pause for verification:** The time for a second glance at the work performed to fix the hasty mistakes.
*   **External framework:** The use of a timer for concentration and rest (for example, the method of "helping").

### Overpers in the way

*   **Multitasking:** The simultaneous implementation of several cases or quick switching between different tasks leads to a decrease in quality.
*   **Blurry instructions:** Tasks without a clear plan and evaluation criteria cause frustration and stupor.
*   **Pressure:** Squeezing and emotional pressure (criticism, comparison) enhance impulsiveness.
*   **Distracting factors:** Noise and extra incentives require additional efforts to self -control, distracting from study.

## 3. Support and development

Practical steps to support a teenager at home and at school.

### Home environment

*   **Organization of work:**
    *   Miss large tasks into small steps (for example, “collect the material”, “make a plan”, “write a draft”). Use stickers or online planners.
    *   Apply **timer**: 25-30 minutes of work, then 5-7 minutes of the break (physical activity). This maintains concentration and reduces stress.
    *   Select time at the end of work on **self -test** On the checklist.
*   **Communication and support:**
    *   Discuss concrete behavior and result, not a person (“What prevented it from doing it neatly?”, “How to avoid this mistake next time?”).
    *   Praise not only for the result, but also for efforts in self -control: “I see how you tried not to rush, it's great!”
    *   Help recognize impulsive impulses. Enter the code word (“pause”, “think”) for the signal to stop.

### School environment

*   **The wording of tasks:**
    *   Provide instructions in the form **checklists or algorithms**. For example, instead of “Write an essay”, give a plan: 1. Thesis. 2. Three arguments. 3. An example to everyone. 4. Conclusion.
    *   Use visual anchors: schemes on the board, key formulas on cards, dates on the timeline.
*   **Lesson organization:**
    *   The optimal place - **first or second desk** To minimize distracting factors.
    *   When submitting new material, follow the principle **"First, rules and criteria - then the task"**. This creates a framework for control of impulsiveness.
    *   In the test work, remind: “There are 10 minutes left. Time to double -check the work. ”
*   **Individual route:**
    *   Officially allow **Using a plan or checklist** For creative and design work.
    *   Give **Additional 5-10 minutes** On important controls exclusively for verification, not for solution.

## 4. Developing practices

Exercises for the development of self -control, using the strengths of a teenager.

*   **Attention and self -control:**
    *   **Board strategic games:** Chess, GO, checkers, "Carcasson". They require planning, patience, the ability to wait for the move, training brake control.
    *   **Game "Find 10 differences":** Develops concentration on details, reduces the tendency to surface scanning.

*   **Coordination and rhythm:**
    *   **Juggling:** Start with two goals, go to three. Synchronizes the work of the hemispheres, develops a bunch of “eye-hand”, improves motor control.
    *   **Game of musical instruments:** Especially drums, percussion, rhythm gitar-pace and stability are important.

*   **Bodily control and awareness:**
    *   **Balance exercises:** Standing on one leg (with eyes open/closed), walking along the line, using a balance board. He teaches to feel and control the body.
    *   **Respiratory practices:** Master “square breathing” (4 inhalation - 4 delay - 4 exhale - 4 delay) for stressful situations. Start from 1-2 minutes a day.

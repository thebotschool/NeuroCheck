# Cognitive assessment

This report reveals the cognitive profile of the student in three key areas: attention that inhibits control and visual memory. The goal is to identify strengths, determine growth zones and offer practical recommendations for optimizing training and everyday life.

---

## 1. Analysis. Key skills

*   **Sustainability of attention (CPT):** Demonstrates a high ability to prolonged concentration. This is a strong basis for a deep study of material and project activities.
*   **Inhibitory control (Go/No-Go):** Difficulties with control of impulsive reactions and quick switching were noted. In situations requiring restraining the first impulse or operational change of actions, errors are possible.
*   **Visual working memory:** He remembers objects well (what?), But he experiences difficulties with their sequence (in what order?). It is difficult for him to hold step -by -step plans in his mind without external support.

## 2. Guide. The success of the student

### Peculiarities. Individual profile

*   **Pace:** Prefers a calm, measured pace. Hurry and temporary pressure significantly increase the number of errors due to reduced control.
*   **Perception:** Better assimilates structured, visually organized information. Abstract ideas or continuous text without schemes, lists and plans are difficult.
*   **Focus:** Attention is "deep, but sensitive." It can concentrate on one task for a long time. However, it is easily distracted with external stimuli or the need for sharp switching.

### Which helps

*   **Step -by -step instructions:** Clear, short algorithms (for example, “do it once, do two”) - his main tool for success.
*   **Visual supports:** Check lists, diagrams, intelligent cards, color coding, comics-everything that structures the information.
*   **Predictable environment:** It feels confidence and acts more effective when the rules are understandable and stable.
*   **Rhythm:** Rhythmic actions (for example, tapping) help to concentrate and synchronize processes.

### What interferes

*   **Multitasking:** Attempts to perform several cases simultaneously cause stress and errors.
*   **Fast change of tasks:** Sharp transitions between topics without a pause for "rebooting" deplete the control resource.
*   **Information overload:** A large amount of unstructured information or strong background noise (loud conversations, music) reduce productivity.
*   **Lack of sample:** Tasks without a clear plan or support structure (for example, “write an essay on a free topic”) cause significant difficulties.

## 3. Recommendations

### House

*   **Homework:**
    *   Break big tasks into small, understandable steps. Example: instead of "Prepare for the control" - "1. Read paragraph 2. Full 5 exercises. 3. Check yourself out of answers."
    *   Use checklists. Refusing the points executed enhances the sense of control.
    *   Provide a quiet workplace. Remove distracting factors, including the phone.
*   **Support:**
    *   Help draw up a plan *before* The start of work. Ask: "Where will we start? What will be the second step?"
    *   Praise not only the result, but also compliance with the plan, as well as the moments of self -control.
    *   Calmly analyze difficulties. If something failed, discuss the trigger: "Was it noisy? The task was incomprehensible?"
*   **Attention:** The schoolboy can maintain concentration on an interesting business for up to 40-50 minutes. After that, a short break (5-10 minutes) is needed, especially before changing the type of task.

### School

*   **The wording of tasks:** The tasks should be specific, with a clear algorithm. Example: instead of “analyze the text” - “1. Find the three main arguments. 2. Give one example. 3. Formulate the conclusion.”
*   **Structuring the material:** Use schemes, numbered lists, supporting notes. For complex topics (essays, projects), provide a template or structure.
*   **Accommodation:** The optimal place in the class, remote from the door and a window, where there are fewer distracting factors.
*   **Innings:** The style is calm, consistent. Warn the change of activity in advance: "After 5 minutes we will finish and move on to a new topic."
*   **Individual route:** Given the features of inhibitory control, it can be useful:
    *   Additional time on control and exams to reduce stress.
    *   The possibility of using a pre -drawn up plan for an oral answer.
    *   Attracting a tutor or psychologist for a short -term program for the development of self -regulation and planning.

## 4. Training. Development of skills

The proposed exercises and games are aimed at supporting and developing the necessary cognitive functions.

*   **Attention and braking:**
    *   **"Yes, no say":** Develops control of speech reactions.
    *   **Correct samples:** On a sheet of text, reproach one letter, circle another. He trains selective attention and control.
    *   **Board games:** "Jenga", "balancing chairs" - require concentration and accurate control of movements.
*   **Consistent memory and planning:**
    *   **Assembly according to the instructions:** Designers (Lego Technic), models, recipes cooking. It is important to strictly follow the steps.
    *   **Creation of comics/recitroaches:** Retelling the plot of a film or book in 4–6 consecutive pictures.
    *   **Card games:** To remember the sequence, for example, "Uno".
*   **Rhythm and bodily control:**
    *   **Musical instruments:** Drums, percussion. Develop a sense of rhythm and coordination "action-renovation".
    *   **Juggling:** Start with two balls. Great training, concentration and a sense of time.
    *   **Dancing/Sports:** Martial arts (karate, judo) with clumsy rituals, dancing with the need to follow rhythm and choreography.

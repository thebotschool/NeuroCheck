# Cognitive development report: a schoolchild neuroprofil

This report provides key data on the cognitive functions of the student, including attention, self -control and memory. The goal is to identify the strengths and areas for development by proposing practical recommendations for home and school.

---

### 1. Analysis of effectiveness

*   **Attention and memory (CPT):** There are own strategies for memorizing visual information. Disclosures can reduce the accuracy of recognition of similar parts.
*   **Self-control (Go/No-Go):** There are difficulties with the suppression of impulsive reactions, especially when using an unknown hand. This indicates the need to improve the consistency of the work of the cerebral hemispheres.
*   **General attention and performance (TCP):** Expressed difficulties with concentration and self -control are noted. This may be due to mental overload, increased anxiety or educational challenges. This aspect requires priority attention.

### 2. A unique approach to learning

#### Key features

*   **Pace:** Uneven. High productivity in simple tasks. The pace slows down with a complication, the need to switch or the appearance of distracting factors.
*   **Perception:** Optimally assimilates visually and structured information. Effective schemes, brief lists, color discharge. Continuous text or complex graphs cause difficulties.
*   **Concentration:** Unstable. It is easily distracted by external stimuli or inner thoughts. Productively holds attention on one task for no more than 10-15 minutes.

#### Support factors

*   **Step -by -step instructions:** Tasks, divided into small, specific actions.
*   **Visual funds:** Using color, stickers, mental cards for organizing thoughts.
*   **Predictability:** A clear daily routine, the use of a timer for working intervals (for example, 15 minutes of work, 5 minutes of rest).
*   **Calm atmosphere:** A workplace with a minimum of distracting objects and sounds.

#### Obstacles

*   **Multitasking:** The simultaneous implementation of several cases often leads to errors and loss of information.
*   **A sharp change in activity:** Fast transitions between objects or activities without a short pause.
*   **Distracting factors:** Loud talk, working TV, mess, overloaded with information presentations.

### 3. Recommendations

#### For the house

*   **Homework: Effective approaches**
    *   Separation: divide the tasks into 15–20-minute intervals. Turn on 5-minute breaks with physical activity.
    *   Planning: draw up a simple plan before starting work (for example, “first mathematics, then a break, then reading”).
    *   Flexibility: Use drafts for schematic drawings and free samples without fear of errors.
*   **Support and encouragement: motivation through the process**
    *   Recognition of efforts: note the effort and compliance with the plan, and not just the final result. For example: "Great, that you first painted the scheme!"
    *   Involvement: instead of "you did?" Ask: “How is it progressing? What are you doing now? Need help? "
*   **Optimal concentration**
    *   A realistic period of continuous attention on a difficult task is 15 minutes. Do not demand more.

#### For school

*   **Claus formulation: clarity and structure**
    *   Maximum specificity: instead of “analyze the text”, use: “1. Read. 2. Find the three main arguments. 3. Write them in a notebook. "
*   **Material structure: visualization and brevity**
    *   Visibility: on the board and in presentations, use lists, diagrams, discharge. Avoid long paragraphs.
    *   Priority: on slides, display only key information.
*   **Organization of the workplace**
    *   Location: optimally-the first-second desk in the central row to minimize abstracts and maintain contact with the teacher.
*   **Individual support (IOM)**
    *   Time: Give additional time for control and independent work.
    *   Flexibility: allow the use of sketches, plans or schemes during an oral response.
    *   Accessibility of instructions: Duplicate oral instructions in writing (on the board, on the card).

### 4. Developing exercises

These exercises develop key skills. Regularity is important: 5-10 minutes are enough daily.

*   **For attention**
    *   **Correct samples:** Crossing the set letters on the text page (for example, “a”) for 5 minutes.
    *   **"Find 10 differences":** Classical game for training concentration on details.
*   **For coordination and interhemispheric interaction**
    *   **Cross -steps:** The alternate touch of the left knee with the right hand, and with the left hand - the right, slowly, in the rhythm.
    *   **"Lazy eights":** Drawing an infinity sign in the air or on a large sheet with each hand in turn, then with both hands together.
*   **For visual memory**
    *   **Memory game:** Training of memorization of location and visual images.
    *   **"What has changed?":** Removing the location of 5-7 items, then determining the changes after their rearrangement or removal.
*   **For rhythm and bodily control**
    *   **Cooling rhythm:** Repetition of a simple rhythmic pattern with cotton or pencil on the table.
    *   **Balance exercises:** Standing on one leg (with open, then with eyes closed), walking along the line. Develops a sense of body and control.

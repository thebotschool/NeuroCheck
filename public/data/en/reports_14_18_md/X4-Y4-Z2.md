# Cognitive assessment report

This report gives an idea of ​​the cognitive features of the student, helping to determine the strengths and areas for development. It contains practical recommendations for supporting home and at school.

---

## 1. Assessment review

The test results revealed key points in the cognitive profile of the student.

*   **Test for attention and self -regulation (CPT):** Difficulties with keeping attention and planning are noted. Fast fatigue and an increase in the number of errors with prolonged concentration are possible.
*   **Braking and control test (Go/No-Go):** Difficulties with the control of impulsive reactions and a quick switch between tasks, especially in conditions requiring composure, were identified.
*   **Test of visual working memory (TCP):** Difficulties with the memorization of visual information and its sequence are shown. This can affect the assimilation of the material and the implementation of multi -step instructions.

## 2. The guide for the success of the student

Understanding individual characteristics helps to create an optimal environment for development and training.

### Features of perception

*   **Pace:** Moderate, thoughtful. An additional time is required to comprehend and fulfill. A fast pace can cause overload and stress.
*   **Perception format:** The most effective visual channel is: diagrams, tables, step -by -step instructions, selected text. Oral information without visual support is more complicated.
*   **Focus of attention:** Attention is unstable, easily exhausted. The effective concentration lasts 15–20 minutes, after which a short break is needed.

### Effective approaches

*   **Visual supports:** Check Listers of tasks, Mayland cards for structuring, highlighting keywords.
*   **Step -by -step instructions:** Clear, short sequences of actions (for example: 1. Read. 2. Write out. 3. Answer).
*   **Structure and predictability:** Breaking large tasks into small, controlled stages. A clear plan reduces anxiety.
*   **Silence:** A calm workplace without distracting factors (music, TV, extra objects).
*   **Using a timer:** Short working sessions (method of prying) improve concentration and reduce the feeling of work.

### Obstacles in learning

*   **Multitasking:** Attempts to listen to, write down, look at the board and follow the time lead to the loss of information.
*   **Fast change of tasks:** High pace of lesson without pauses for understanding.
*   **Noise and chaos:** Background conversations, disorder at the workplace or in the classroom significantly reduce productivity.
*   **Abstract and long instructions:** Long oral explanations without visual support or a clear structure are practically not absorbed.

## 3. Recommendations

The application of these recommendations will help create a supporting environment for the development of a student.

### Home environment

*   **Organization of work:**
    *   Classes of short blocks for 20-25 minutes. Breaks of 5 minutes with physical activity.
    *   Start with complex or less beloved items, while cognitive resources are maximum.
    *   Make a simple written plan for the evening. Mark the executed points for a sensation of progress.
*   **Support and encouragement:**
    *   Praise for efforts and process, not only for the final result. Phrases like “I see how you tried” more important than the assessment.
    *   Avoid criticism for slowness. Instead of "Why so long?" Ask: “What interferes? Let's figure it out. ”
*   **Day of the day:**
    *   A stable dream (at least 8–9 hours) is critical. Ned -sleeping worsens attention and self -control.

### School environment

*   **The wording of tasks:**
    *   Instructions to give both verbally and in writing (on the board, card, in the chat).
    *   Divide tasks into clear, numbered stages. Use the imperative verbs: “Emphasize”, “Write out”.
    *   Provide templates or samples for complex tasks.
*   **Lesson structure and workplace:**
    *   Optimal place: 1-2 desks in the central row. Minimizes distraction, improves eye contact with the teacher.
    *   Micropausa before a new topic, so that the student manages to comprehend the previous one.
    *   Periodically handle short, direct questions (“Is everything clear?”, “What is the next step?”) To return attention.
*   **Individual educational route (IOM):**
    *   It is recommended to discuss the possibility of creating an IOM with a school psychologist. This may include:
        *   Additional time on control (up to 25%).
        *   Using a draft or a checklist in the answer.
        *   Adaptation of homework.

## 4. Recommended training

Daily short training (10-15 minutes) in a playful way will help to develop the necessary skills.

### For attention

*   **Correct samples:** In any printed text, delete all the letters “A” or circle “O”. You can complicate, looking for two letters at the same time.
*   **Schultte tables:** Search for numbers from 1 to 25 in order in a random table. He trains concentration and peripheral vision perfectly.

### For coordination and body control

*   **Balance exercises:** Standing on one leg with open, then with eyes closed (30 seconds on each leg).
*   **Juggling:** Start with two small balls. Develops the coordination of "hands-eyed" and concentration.

### For memory

*   **Memory game:** Search for paired cards.
*   **"Chain of words":** To call the words in turn on a certain topic, each next begins in the last letter of the previous one.

### For rhythm and self -control

*   **Rhythmic drawings:** Back or cool the specified rhythm (for example: two short, one long). You can use a metronome.

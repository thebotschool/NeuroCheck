## Cognitive assessment: Your child

This report offers a detailed view of the cognitive abilities of your student. We highlight the strengths and denote key areas for development to optimize training and growth.

---

## 1. Key conclusions

### Consistent memory
The student demonstrates the high potential in memorizing facts and images. The main area for development is structuring and organization of information.

### Impuls control
Difficulties with a quick stop of actions and switching attention are noted. This can be manifested in impulsiveness, hasty decisions and needs in enhanced self -control.

### Sustainability of attention
The student's attention is subject to quick fatigue. This makes it difficult to maintain a stable pace and concentration, especially in long or monotonous tasks.

## 2. The guide for the success of your student

### Features of training

**The working pace.** The pace of the student is individual and can be uneven. With fatigue or loss of interest, the concentration is reduced. A high external pace, requiring constant self -control, can be complicated.

**Perception of information.** The optimal perception is through structured and visual information. Oral instructions without written support are less effective. Schemes, lists and algorithms contribute to better assimilation.

**Concentration.** Attention is impulsive and subject to exhaustion. Distraction and haste can lead to errors. For a stable focus, external supports are needed: plans, checklists, visual tips.

### Supporting factors

*   **Step -by -step instructions.** Separation of complex tasks into small, understandable steps.
*   **Visual supports.** Schemes, intelligence cards, timline, brief lists. Highlighting keywords.
*   **A clear plan of action.** Preliminary planning organizes thoughts and actions. It is commendable that the student is already using his own strategies.
*   **Templates and samples.** The finished example reduces the cognitive load.
*   **Predictable rhythm.** Alternation of work (15–20 minutes) and short breaks (3-5 minutes).

### Interviewing factors

*   **Multitasking.** The simultaneous performance of several tasks provokes errors and frustration.
*   **Long oral instructions.** Audial information without visual support is quickly forgotten.
*   **High pace and temporary pressure.** Hurry enhances impulsiveness and reduces self -control.
*   **Lack of clear structure.** Faring tasks, for example, "make an essay" without a plan, cause confusion.
*   **Monotonous activity.** Long -term work of the same type quickly causes fatigue and loss of attention.

## 3. Practical recommendations

### For the house

**Organization.**
*   Use the timer: 20-25 minutes of work, 5 minutes of break (movement, water).
*   Together, create an evening check list. Example: 1. Solve 5 problems. 2. Read 10 pages. 3. Collect a backpack.
*   Before a difficult task, help make a plan: what to do first, what later, what is the expected result.

**Support.**
*   Encourage the process, and not just the result: "Great plan!", "I see your efforts in concentration."
*   Together, look for effective strategies for memorization: not just “teach”, but “let's draw a scheme” or “come up with a story”.

**Holding attention.**
*   Effective concentration lasts 20–25 minutes. Avoid the requirements of prolonged continuous work.

### For school

**The formulation of tasks.**
*   Use short, clear instructions. Duplicate oral tasks in writing (on a board, in a chat, on a card).
*   Miss large projects into stages with clear terms.

**The structure of the lesson.**
*   At the beginning of the lesson, announce the plan: "We will check your homework, study the new topic, do the exercise."
*   Apply visual supports: presentations with a minimum of text, diagram, tables.
*   The recommended place is the first-second desk (minimization of abstracts).

**Individual approach.**
*   Provide extra time in the test work.
*   Allow me to use a finished plan or a template for an essay/complex questions.
*   The results indicate the need to consult a school psychologist or neuropsychologist to develop a cognitive correction program.

## 4. Training of skills

These exercises will help to develop key skills. Perform them in a playful way, without coercion.

### Attention and self -control

*   **Board games.** “Jenga” (control, concentration), “set” (search for patterns), “uno” (switching of the rules).
*   **Corrective tests.** Printed text: cross everything 'A' in 1 minute. Complicate: 'A' circle, 'o' cross out.
*   **The game "Sea is worried once."** Classical game: the ability to freeze and hold the pose trains brake control.

### Memory and planning

*   **Assembly according to the instructions.** Designers (Lego Technic), models, assembly of furniture according to the scheme. Teach to follow the algorithm.
*   **Cooking on recipes.** Preparation of dishes by steps: perfect planning and sequence training.
*   **Creation of comics.** Draw a story of 4-6 frames. Develops a logical sequence.

### Rhythm and bodily control

*   **Rhythmic exercises.** Repeat the simple rhythms (claps, knock) behind an adult or audio recording.
*   **Games with the ball.** Throwing the ball with fishing by signal.
*   **Dance games.** Dance games (for example, Just Dance) develop coordination, rhythm, sequence of movements.

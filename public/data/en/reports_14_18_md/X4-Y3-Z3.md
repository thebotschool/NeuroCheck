# Cognitive assessment report
### Attention, control, coordination
Age group: 14–18 years old

This report provides key information about the cognitive abilities of the student. It is designed to help in understanding the strengths, identifying areas for development and a sentence of practical recommendations for support in training and everyday life.

## 1. Overview of the results

*   **Test for attention and memory (CPT):** Difficulties with a long concentration, holding the sequence of actions and planning are noticeable. Volumetric or unstructured tasks can cause difficulties.
*   **Self-control test (Go/No-Go):** Asymmetry is noted in speed and accuracy between the leading and unknown hand. This indicates the need to strengthen interhemispheric interaction, important for tasks requiring the synthesis of analysis and visualization.
*   **Impulsive test (TCP):** There is a tendency to hasty actions. This can be manifested in errors on inattention in study and the difficulties of self -control in social interactions.

## 2. The guide for the success of the student

### Individual characteristics

*   **Pace:** The progress is non -linear. Initial development requires time that hasty execution can follow. This can lead to a passage of details.
*   **Perception:** Optimally assimilates information through visual and structural formats. Schemes, checklists and step-by-step algorithms are as effective as possible.
*   **Attention:** The focus is unstable. Easily distracted. Monotonous or complex tasks, as well as multitasking, quickly deplete concentration.

### Which contributes

*   **Visual support:** Schemes, tables, mental cards, markers for keywords. Everything that structures information.
*   **Clear instructions:** Step -by -step algorithms for each task. This ensures clarity of actions.
*   **Regulations and rhythm:** Short intervals of intensive work (15–25 min) with small breaks (3-5 minutes). Supports energy level.
*   **Focus on one:** The maximum concentration on the current task is without abstracts.

### What prevents

*   **Multitasking:** The simultaneous performance of several actions (listen, write, think) leads to overload and data loss.
*   **Rush and oral instructions:** Long oral instructions without visual support are poorly absorbed. The pressure of time enhances impulsiveness.
*   **Excessive incentives:** Noise, disorder (visual, audial) reduce attention productivity.
*   **Abstract tasks:** General or voluminous instructions without specific steps can cause a stupor and procrastination.

## 3. Recommendations

### For the house

*   **Organization of work:**
    *   Divide big tasks into small steps. For example, instead of “Make mathematics” - “Solve the problem 1, then rest, then task 3”.
    *   Use the timer. The "Prydor" method (25 minutes of work, 5 minutes break) contributes to concentration.
    *   Provide a quiet, ordered working space without unnecessary items.
*   **Support and motivation:**
    *   Mark the efforts made: "I see, you perfectly planned this task!" Focus on the process, and not just on the result.
    *   Together plan: use stickers, glider or digital applications to draw up a schedule.
    *   Accustom to pauses. With stress, offer breathing exercises to restore calm.
*   **Realistic expectations:**
    *   Productive attention is limited to 15-20 minutes for complex tasks. Do not expect a long continuous work.

### For school

*   **The wording of tasks:**
    *   The instructions should be chat, step -by -step, with numbering.
    *   Duplicate oral instructions in writing: on the board, in the card, in an electronic diary.
*   **Lesson structure:**
    *   Use visual materials: schemes, tables, infographics.
    *   Start a lesson with the voicing of the plan. This creates predictability.
*   **Class placement:**
    *   Optimally, the first or second place removed from the sources of distraction (window, door).
*   **Individual route:**
    *   Give additional time for control and independent work.
    *   Allow the use of drafts and pre -drawn up plans for answers.
    *   Consider the accompaniment of a tutor or school psychologist for the development of self -regulation and planning.

## 4. Training practices

The exercises and games presented below are aimed at developing key cognitive functions. Regularity and a positive approach are the key to success.

*   **For attention and memory:**
    *   **Board games:** "Cortex", "Dobbl", "drum" for the speed of reaction and focus. Classic "memorial".
    *   **Schultte tables:** Purposeful search for numbers for a while. Develops peripheral vision and concentration.
*   **For coordination and interhemispheric interaction:**
    *   **Juggling:** Start with two items. A powerful brain activity stimulator.
    *   **Synchronous drawing:** Draw symmetrical figures (circles, squares) with two hands at the same time.
    *   **Crossroads:** Standing or in motion, touch the right hand of the left knee, the left - the right.
*   **To control impulsiveness:**
    *   **The game "Stop-signal":** During active movement (for example, dance), instantly freeze on command. Develops the speed of inhibition of the reaction.
    *   **Balance exercises:** Standing on one leg, walking along the line, using a balance board.
    *   **Respiratory practices:** Exercise "Squarey breathing" (inspiration-carving-outline of 4 counts). Helps to calm down and concentrate.

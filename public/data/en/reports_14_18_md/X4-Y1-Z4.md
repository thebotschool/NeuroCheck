## Cognitive assessment report: CPT + GO/No-Go + Test of visual working memory (14–18 years)

The report is intended for parents and teachers. Purpose: to reveal the strengths and growth zones of the student, to provide recommendations for creating an optimal environment of learning and development.

## 1. Review of tests

*   **Visual memory test (Z):** Difficulties with maintaining the sequence of images and planning were noted. Indicates calls in working memory and organization of attention in volumetric tasks.

*   **Go/No-Go Test (Y):** High self -control and quick decision -making in tasks with clockwork are demonstrated. Talks about the mature braking of impulses in simple situations.

*   **CPT (x):** Long cognitive load causes overload and anxiety. This reduces attention and self -regulation.

**Key conclusion:** The high reaction rate and impulse control are strengths. Complex, long or multi -stage tasks can lead to fatigue and focus loss.

## 2. The guide for the success of your student

### Individual characteristics

*   **Pace:** Leisurely. It takes time to comprehend the task. Hurry provokes errors and frustration.
*   **Perception:** Step -by -step. Information is better absorbed if it is represented by short, logical blocks. Long texts and multi -stage instructions overload.
*   **Focus of attention:** Targeted. High concentration on one simple task. Multivativity, memory requirements or noise scatter the focus.

### Success factors

*   **Step -by -step instructions:** Check lists, algorithms, numbered lists. Separation of volumetric tasks into small steps.
*   **Visual supports:** Setting the keys, stickers, diagrams, Mayland cards. Unload work memory.
*   **Quiet environment:** A minimum of visual and sound distractions. Promotes concentration.
*   **Timer:** For short, intensive sessions (15–20 minutes work, 5 min rest). He trains attention.

### What prevents

*   **Multitasking:** Simultaneous perception, memorization and recording leads to failure.
*   **Long oral instructions:** More than two consistent teams overload the memory.
*   **Rush and pressure:** Strong terms and calls to speed cause anxiety, blocking thoughts.
*   **Unstructured information:** Continuous text, obscure tasks, a mess on the table.

## 3. Recommendations

### Home environment

1.  **Organization of work:**
    *   Together draw up an evening plan: an object, time, expected result. Fix in a prominent place (board, leaf).
    *   Break volumetric tasks (for example, preparation for the control) into small subtasibles. Example: Today - paragraph 1, tomorrow - paragraph 2.
    *   The “15-minute sprints” method: set the timer for 15–20 minutes for one task. After the signal - a short break. He trains concentration.

2.  **Support and motivation:**
    *   Praise for the process, not just the result: "You sat for 15 minutes without abstracts, excellent!" Or "I see, you followed the plan, well done!"
    *   Recognize difficulties: “I understand that the task is great. Let's think about how to simplify him. ”

3.  **Working space:**
    *   Provide a quiet workplace without distracting items. It is better to clean the phone in another room.

### School environment

1.  **The wording of tasks:**
    *   The instructions should be chat, brief, step -by -step. It is advisable to duplicate orally and in writing (board, card).
    *   Example: instead of “Read the paragraph, answer questions and write out the terms” Use the algorithm:
        1.  Read with. 45–47.
        2.  Answer the question No. 3 in writing.
        3.  Find and write out 3 terms highlighted by bold font.

2.  **The structure of the lesson and tests:**
    *   The first-second desk is recommended to minimize abstracts.
    *   On control: tasks can be divided into blocks or provided extra time.
    *   Elements of an individual route: allow a draft-plan for an essay. Provide visual schemes for complex topics.

3.  **Material feed:**
    *   Use visibility: diagrams, tables, infographics are more effective than continuous text or lectures.
    *   Enjoy short, specific questions. This maintains activity in the lesson.

## 4. Recommended training

These exercises develop key skills. In a game, affordable form.

### Attention and planning

*   **Board games:** “Corridor”, “Katamin”, complex labyrinths. Develops the passage of moves, retention of the target.
*   **"Find 10 differences":** He trains concentration and system comparison.

### Working memory

*   **The game "Memory" (find a couple):** Increases the load on visual working memory with an increase in the number of cards.
*   **Exercise "Repeat the sequence":** The teenager repeats the series of movements (cotton, top, click). Gradually increase the length of the sequence.

### Coordination and rhythm (interhemispheric interaction)

*   **Juggling:** Start with two items (balls, scarves). Synchronizes the brain and body, improves concentration.
*   **Backing the ball:** Fight the ball from the floor or walls in a given rhythm (music, score).

### Bodily control and self -regulation

*   **Obstacle course:** Create and go through a simple strip (step over, crawl, get around) for a while. Teaches spatial planning.
*   **Respiratory exercises:** Simple techniques, for example, “square breath” (4 inhalation, 4 delay, 4 exhale, 4 delay). They help to cope with anxiety and overload.

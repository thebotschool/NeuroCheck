# Cognitive assessment report

This report provides a comprehensive analysis of the cognitive functions of the student, based on the test results CPT, Go/No-Go and TCP. The goal is to identify individual characteristics and offer practical recommendations for support in training and development.

---

## 1. Brief conclusions on tests

### Test for attention and working memory (CPT)
Difficulties with planning and holding the sequence of actions were noted. Mistakes are possible when performing tasks requiring organization and a phased approach.

### Braking Test (Go/No-Go)
Difficulties with self -control have been identified. The tendency to impulsive actions, especially with fatigue or high load.

### Attention test (TCP)
General impulsiveness is confirmed. This can affect the quality of decisions and behavior, causing actions before thinking.

## 2. Profile of a student: Features of learning

This section will help to understand how the student perceives information and learns most effectively.

### Individual characteristics

*   **Pace:** Uneven. The tendency to impulsive start, then rapid exhaustion. An individual, measured rhythm of work is required.
*   **Perception:** Optimally - visually and step -by -step. Abstract oral instructions are ineffective and quickly forgotten.
*   **Focus of attention:** Unstable. High distraction from external stimuli and cognitive overload. Multivature reduces concentration.

### Optimal training conditions

*   **Step -by -step instructions:** Separation of large tasks into 2-3 small, clear steps.
*   **Visual support:** Check lists, diagrams, Mayind cards, color release of key information.
*   **Clear structure:** Clear rules, predictable algorithm, a pre -known plan of the lesson or assignment.
*   **Own pace:** The lack of pressure and haste reduces the number of errors.
*   **Short breaks:** Pause (3-5 minutes) for physical discharge help to restore attention.

### Obstacles in learning

*   **Multitasking:** The requirement of the simultaneous implementation of several cases or quick switching between them.
*   **Long oral instructions:** Volumetric explanations without visual support are perceived as a background noise.
*   **Rush and high pace:** Activates impulsiveness leading to chaos and errors.
*   **Distracting factors:** Noise, conversations, visual disorder reduce concentration.

## 3. Recommendations

### For the house

1.  **Homework organization:**
    *   **Decomposition of tasks:** Separate large tasks into small, controlled stages.
    *   **Check lists:** Create joint work plans. Mark the executed points for clarity.
    *   **Timer:** Use the “Prydor” method (20 minutes of work, 5 minutes of rest) to structuring time.
    *   **Working space:** Organize the workplace by minimizing distracting elements.

2.  **Support and encouragement:**
    *   **The emphasis on the process:** Mark efforts and efforts, and not just the final result.
    *   **Calm:** Support a calm and objective reaction to errors. Constructive discussion is more effective than criticism.
    *   **Realistic expectations:** Keep in mind that effective attention to a complex task is limited to 15-20 minutes.

### For school

1.  **The wording and structure of tasks:**
    *   **Maximum specificity:** Provide clear algorithms for completing tasks.
    *   **Visual templates:** Use finished schemes, plans, algorithms to remove the load from working memory.
    *   **Duplication of instructions:** Duplicate important tasks in writing (on the board, in handouts).

2.  **Organization of the educational process:**
    *   **Optimal place:** Placing at the first or second desk, in the center, far from the sources of distraction (window, door).
    *   **Clear lesson planning:** Briefly voiced the lesson plan at the beginning to create a predictable structure.
    *   **Individual educational route (IOM):** Consider the possibility of connecting a school psychologist to develop an IOM, including:
        *   Additional time in control work.
        *   Alternative formats for passing tasks (projects, diagrams).

## 4. Targeted training

These exercises will help develop skills detected as scarce. Regularity and a positive approach are the key to progress.

### For attention and self -control

*   **Correct samples:** Search and deletion of certain letters or characters in the text (start from 3-5 minutes).
*   **Graphic dictations:** Following the drawing according to oral instructions (“one cage to the right, two cells up”).
*   **The game "Forbidden movement":** Repetition of movements behind the host, excluding one in advance.

### For working memory

*   **Memory game:** Classical game for the search for paired cards.
*   **"What has changed?":** Memorization of several objects on the table, then identifying changes (removal, movement).
*   **Repetition of sequences:** Memorization and reproduction of unrelated words or numbers in a given order, gradually increasing their number.

### For bodily control and rhythm

*   **Balance exercises:** Walking along the line, a stand on one leg (you can with eyes closed).
*   **RIGHTS RIGHTS:** The exact reproduction of the specified rhythmic sequences (cotton, knock).
*   **Juggling:** Start with two balls for the development of visual-motor coordination and concentration.

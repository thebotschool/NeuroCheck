# Neurocheck: Cognitive profile report (14–18 years old)

This report is developed for parents and teachers. Its goal is to identify the strengths and areas of growth of the student. Recommendations are aimed at creating an optimal environment for training and development.

---

## 1. Overview of the results

*   **CPT (attention and working memory):** Difficulties have been identified with the simultaneous retention of information and planning, which leads to errors and slowdown.
*   **Go/No-Go (braking and control):** Difficulties with the suppression of impulsive reactions were noted, especially if necessary switching.
*   **TCP (stability of attention):** The stronger side is the ability to long and deep concentration on one task under understandable conditions.

**Key conclusion:** The attention of the student is not “bad” as a whole. It is capable of deep concentration, but experiences difficulties in case of simultaneous self -control, holding several conditions and flexible switching between tasks.

## 2. The guide for the success of your student

### Individual profile

*   **Pace:** He prefers his pace, more often moderate. It takes time to comprehend the task. Hurry provokes errors.
*   **Perception:** Optimally perceives the information visually and step by step. Abstract or long oral instructions are difficult to absorb.
*   **Focus of attention:** “Tunnel” attention was noted. The strengths are deep concentration on one task. However, this can lead to ignoring other important details or conditions.

### Which contributes to training

*   **Step -by -step instructions:** Clear, short algorithms (checklists).
*   **Visual supports:** Schemes, tables, highlighting keywords, cards with rules.
*   **A single channel of information:** Instructions in one format (in writing or orally, but not at the same time).
*   **Predictability and structure:** Clear rules, a pre -known plan.
*   **Adaptation time:** 1-2 minutes to read and understand the assignment before starting work.

### Which prevents training

*   **Multitasking:** Simultaneous requirements for listening, recording and controlling time.
*   **Frequent change of tasks:** Sharp transitions between activities without a pause.
*   **Oral instructions:** Lack of visual support, especially for long and multicomponent instructions.
*   **Noise and distraction:** Extraneous conversations or visual mess.
*   **Time pressure:** Tough time frame for the performance of complex tasks.

## 3. Recommendations

### For the house

*   **Organization of work:**
    *   Divide big tasks into small, understandable steps. For example, a project on history: 1. Find 5 facts. 2. Write 2 sentences for each. 3. Pick up 3 pictures.
    *   Use gliders or checklists to track progress. This creates a feeling of control and completeness.
    *   The optimal duration of work: 20-25 minutes, then a short (5 minutes) break without gadgets.
*   **Support:**
    *   Praise for the process and efforts: “It's great that you followed the plan!”, “I see how you tried to concentrate.”
    *   Create visual tips for complex topics (cards with formulas).
*   **Working space:** Organize a quiet, ordered place. On the table there should be only necessary for the current task.

### For school

*   **Instructions:**
    *   Formulate clearly, step by step, with verbs of action (“emphasize”, “write down”).
    *   Duplicate oral tasks in writing (on the board, on the card).
*   **Lesson structure:**
    *   Place the student in the front of the class, away from distracting factors (windows, doors).
    *   Before starting difficult work, make sure that the task is understood. Ask the student to repeat the action plan.
    *   Use a timer for individual stages, and not for the entire assignment. This will reduce anxiety.
*   **Individual plan (IOM):**
    *   The test results can serve as the basis for the development of IOM with a school psychologist and class teacher.
    *   IOM can officially fix adapted tasks (with checklists) and additional time on control. Consultation with specialists for contacting the PMPK is recommended.

## 4. Recommended training

These exercises contribute to the development of skills detected as growth zones. The key to success is regularity, not duration.

### Attention and self -control

*   **The game "Delay":** Dance to the music, freeze with the end of the melody. He trains a quick stop.
*   **Graphic dictations:** Drawing on cells under dictation. Develops auditory attention and following instructions.

### Working memory and planning

*   **Board strategic games:** Chess, checkers, "set". They require holding the rules and planning moves.
*   **Game "Find a couple" (Memory):** Search for steam cards. Perfectly develops visual working memory.

### Coordination and control

*   **Juggling:** Start with two balls or scarves. Improves the coordination of the “eye-hand” and concentration.
*   **Rhythmic exercises:** Cooling or polling the specified rhythmic pattern. Helps synchronize attention and movement.

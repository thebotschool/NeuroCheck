**Assessment of cognitive functions: memory, control, attention (14–18 years)**

This report is designed to reveal the strengths and growth zones of your student. Recommendations are aimed at creating a favorable environment for development and training.

---

### **1. Key tests of tests**

*   **Memory:** The information is absorbed well, but the sequence can be broken. Support for structuring and planning is required.
*   **Control:** High level of self -control. The decision -making is quick, impulsive is minimized. This is a key learning skill.
*   **Attention:** Long -term concentration on one task is difficult. Fatigue and decrease in accuracy in monotonous work are possible.

<!-- page-break -->

### **2. The guide for the success of your student**

#### **Individual characteristics**

*   **Pace:** Unstable. The beginning is actively, but fatigue quickly occurs with long, monotonous tasks. An intermittent rhythm is recommended.
*   **Perception:** Effectively assimilates visual, structured information: schemes, tables, lists, mental cards. Long oral explanations without visual support are more difficult.
*   **Focus of attention:** The combination of strong self -control for short tasks and low stability with prolonged concentration. Effective concentration lasts 5-10 minutes.

#### **Which contributes to success**

*   Step -by -step instructions.
*   Visual supports.
*   Tiemer planning.
*   Active involvement.

#### **Which prevents success**

*   Multitasking.
*   Monotonous lectures.
*   Abstract tasks.
*   Lack of structure.

<!-- page-break -->

### **3. Practical recommendations**

#### **In the home environment**

*   **How to do homework:**
    *   Break work into blocks for 20-30 minutes. Provide 5-minute breaks for physical activity.
    *   Make a written plan before starting work. Mark the executed points for a sensation of progress.
    *   When memorizing sequences, use visual methods: comics, schemes, timeage.
*   **How to support:**
    *   Focus on structuring, not a direct solution. Questions: “What is the plan? What is the next step? " Help.
    *   Support his self -control. Mark successful following the plan, timely breaks and return to tasks.
*   **Concentration:** Effective concentration on one task is 20–25 minutes. After this, a short break is required to restore productivity.

#### **In the school environment**

*   **Instructions:** Offer ducal algorithms or checklists. Example: for an essay - to structure according to the items: main thought, arguments, examples, introduction/conclusion.
*   **Lesson structure:** Duplicate key oral moments on the board/presentation (schemes, lists). Alternate activities: lecture, work with text, paired discussions.
*   **Place:** The first desks, closer to the center. This minimizes distraction and provides visual contact with the board.
*   **Style style:** Interactive, involving. To include questions, requests to give examples, short tasks for self -testing.
*   **Individual route:**
    *   Allow the use of a timer for self -organization.
    *   Provide written instructions for complex oral tasks.
    *   Involve in design work. This will allow the use of high self -control and planning (with support at the structuring stage).

### **4. Training practices**

The proposed practices are aimed at strengthening cognitive functions.

*   **Attention and control:**
    *   Schultte tables: search for numbers in order. Improves concentration and peripheral vision.
    *   Games "Find the differences" or searching for objects: develop visual perception.
*   **Memory and planning:**
    *   Table strategies (chess, checkers, Ticket to Ride): stimulate multi -way planning.
    *   History of wordless comics: develop a logical and temporary sequence.
*   **Rhythm and bodily control:**
    *   Neurohymnastics (for example, “Kulak-Robric Ladon”): improves interhemispheric interaction and reduces fatigue.
    *   Juggling: develops visual-motor coordination and concentration.

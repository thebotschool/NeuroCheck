# Cognitive profile report: CPT+Go/No-GO+TCP (14–18 years)

This report is designed to reveal the unique cognitive profile of your student. Data analysis reveals significant potential for self -control and processing of information. At the same time, current indicators indicate a possible overload that affects attention and planning.

## 1. Review of tests

### Memory and planning (CPT)
Information is absorbed effectively. However, its consistent organization and use cause difficulties. The skill of reproduction of information is ahead of the skill of its structuring.

### Self-control (Go/No-Go)
A high level of ability to contain impulses and compliance with the rules. This indicates the maturity of regulatory functions.

### Attention (TCP)
Difficulties with maintenance of prolonged concentration are noted. There is a slight distractiveness. These indicators may indicate the current overload of the nervous system.

### Key conclusion
Your schoolboy has significant potential for self -regulation. The current results reflect the state of cognitive overload. The goal is to create an environment where its strengths will be fully disclosed.

## 2. The guide for the success of your student

### Individual profile

#### Pace
The dynamics of tasks are unstable. A quick start in simple cases is replaced by fatigue at long distances. The optimal rhythmic, predictable pace with mandatory breaks.

#### Perception
The visual-logical channel prevails. Abstracts and volumetric texts make it difficult to assimilate. Schemes, lists, intelligence cards, step-by-step instructions are effectively perceived.

#### Focus of attention
Attention is unstable, the resource is quickly depleted. Active concentration is possible up to 15-20 minutes, then a short break is required.

### Supporting factors

#### Decomposition of tasks
Separation of complex tasks into small, controlled steps. Example: instead of 'prepare for history' - 'Read the paragraph, write down the date, answer questions.'

#### Visual supports
Effectively, the use of checklists, stickers, text secretions, diagrams. These tools compensate for the temporary weakening of planning function.

#### The rhythm of work
Application of the method of “prying” or similar techniques: 20 minutes of work, 5 minutes of rest (movement, hydration, distraction without gadgets).

#### Organized environment
A quiet, ordered working space with a constant location of objects.

### Distracting factors

#### Multitasking
Parallel performance of tasks, for example, listening to information and recording, or study against the background of distracting devices, reduces efficiency. Focus on one task.

#### Pressure
Improving the pace or emotional pressure (for example, 'faster', 'you do not have time ”) enhance the alarm and prevent the work of cognitive functions.

#### Unstructured tasks
Large, unstructured tasks (for example, “write an essay ') are perceived as insurmountable. A clear plan is required: entry, arguments, conclusion.

#### External distraction
Any external stimuli - noise, disorder, movement - deplete a limited resource of attention.

## 3. Recommendations

### Home environment

#### Organization of training
Joint planning of tasks: determine the volume, sequence, time. Fix the plan. Use a timer (20-25 minutes of work, 5-7 minutes break). Priority - support, not control. Questions: 'What is in terms?', 'Why do we start?', 'Need help with the decomposition?'.

#### Motivation and support
Mark efforts and process, and not just the result ('focused work within 20 minutes - excellent progress'). Open dialogue about difficulties with concentration. Joint search for causes (fatigue, anxiety, lack of sleep).

#### Reduction in pressure
Provide time for free vacation and hobbies without digital devices. The brain needs regular rebooting.

### School environment

#### Briefing
Task formulation: step by step, in numbered lists. Providing samples or examples for complex tasks. In oral answers - specific questions, avoiding common ones.

#### Educational process
Optimal place: 1-2 desks, remotely from sources of distraction (window, door). Make sure of the understanding of the assignment (the request to briefly repeat). Provide micro-pauses (warm-up, access to water) after intense blocks.

#### Individual route
A temporary reduction in the training load is recommended. Providing additional time in control and independent work. Consultation with a school psychologist to analyze the reasons for overload (anxiety, social aspects) and the development of supporting strategies.

## 4. Practical training

The following exercises contribute to the development of key cognitive skills in interactive form. Regularity is more important than the duration: 10 minutes daily more efficiently hours once a week.

### Attention and self -control

#### Graphic dictations
Exercises on the given coordinates ('two cells to the right, one up "). Develop auditory attention and following the algorithm.

#### Schultte tables
Search for numbers in order by time. Improves concentration and peripheral vision.

#### Search for differences
Classic exercise for the development of focus and detailed attention.

### Consistency and planning

#### Design according to the instructions
Assembly of designers (for example, Lego) strictly according to the instructions. Walking planning training.

#### Creation of comics
Retelling of stories (film, book, day) in the form of 4-6 pictures with short signatures. Develops the selection of the main and chronological thinking.

#### Strategic games
Board games with planning a few moves in advance ('jackal', 'karcasson', chess).

### Bodily control and relaxation

#### Juggling
Start with two balls in one hand, then in the other. Synchronizes the hemisphere, reduces stress.

#### Balance
Standing on one leg, walking in a straight line. Promotes grounding, improves proprioscope and indirectly affects attention.

#### Rhythmic exercises
Reproduction of the specified rhythms (tapping, popping). Supports the stability of the nervous system.

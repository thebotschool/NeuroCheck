# Cognitive Profile Assessment: Report

This report offers a detailed understanding of the cognitive features of your student. The goal is to provide clear recommendations to support its development and training.

---

### 1. Key assessments

*   **Working memory (CPT):** The ability to preserve individual facts is high, but the ordering of information by the sequence causes difficulties.
*   **Control and reaction (Go/No-Go):** The high reaction rate was noted by the leading hand. However, when the second hand is used or in unusual conditions, the speed and accuracy are reduced.
*   **Sustainability of attention (TCP):** There are signs of mental fatigue and cognitive overload. Attention is unstable, self -control is weakened.

### 2. Training profile: approach to learning

#### Features of assimilation

*   **Pace:** The student needs extra time for concentration, especially on multi -stage tasks. There is a quick fatigue that reduces productivity after 15–20 minutes of work.
*   **Perception:** Information is better absorbed if there is a clear visual structure. Oral instructions without support for text or images can be difficult to process.
*   **Attention:** Attention is intermittently and unstable. Distracting factors, or the complexity of the task, can lead to loss of concentration. This is a consequence of resource depletion.

#### Supporting factors

*   **Step -by -step instructions:** Check lists, numbered lists, algorithms. Format "Step 1, then Step 2."
*   **Visual supports:** Mental cards, diagrams, tables, color release of key points.
*   **Predictable rhythm:** The alternation of short working intervals (15–20 minutes) with small breaks (3-5 minutes).
*   **One task at a time:** A clear, focused target, without switching between different objects or tasks.
*   **Physical activity:** Short warm -ups, especially with the use of both hands, contribute to “rebooting” and improving coordination.

#### Interviewing factors

*   **Multitasking:** The simultaneous performance of several actions reduces effectiveness.
*   **Long oral instructions:** Details can be missed.
*   **Rush and pressure:** Enhance errors associated with sequence and asymmetry.
*   **Information noise:** Distracting factors in the environment deplete the reserve of attention.
*   **Unstructured material:** Continuous text without logical organization complicates perception.

### 3. Practical recommendations

#### Home environment

*   **Organization of work:**
    *   Joint planning of homework: “First mathematics (tasks 1, 2), then 5 minutes of break, then reading (2 pages).” The plan can be fixed.
    *   Using the timer: 20 minutes of work, 5 minutes break.
    *   The workplace should be organized: on the table only necessary for the current task.
*   **Support and encouragement:**
    *   Mark efforts, not just the result: “You follow the plan!”, “Well, 20 minutes of concentration is great!”
    *   In case of difficulties, help return to the structure: "What is the next step according to the plan?"
*   **Mercing of attention:** Expect 15–20 minutes of focused work, after which a short break is needed.

#### School environment

*   **The wording of tasks:**
    *   Miss large tasks into small, numb steps.
    *   Provide instructions both verbally and in writing (on the board, in a printout).
    *   Use clear, directed verbs: “1. Read. 2. Emphasize. 3. Write out. "
*   **Lesson structure:**
    *   Place the student away from the sources of distraction (doors, windows), closer to the teacher.
    *   Provide templates to complete tasks (for example, a plan for writing, a table for filling out).
    *   At the beginning of the lesson, voice and fix the plan on the board: “Today we: 1. We repeat ... 2. We will find out ... 3. Fill ...”.
*   **Individual approach:**
    *   Permission to use a line to track line when reading.
    *   Providing additional time for the performance of tests.
    *   Assessment of the quality of performed step -by -step tasks, and not just their number.

### 4. Development of abilities: exercises

These exercises will help strengthen key cognitive functions. Offer them in a playful way, without coercion.

*   **Memory and attention:**
    *   **The game "Story on the pictures":** Lay out 4-6 plot images in random order, ask to restore the sequence and make a story.
    *   **Graphic dictations:** Drawing on cells for dictation ("Two cells to the right, one up ...").
    *   **Assembly according to the instructions:** Designers, models, culinary recipes where the procedure is important.
*   **Coordination and interhemispheric interaction:**
    *   **Juggling:** Start with two light objects (balls, scarves).
    *   **Game on the piano or synthesizer:** Develops independent work of both hands.
    *   **Exercise "Fist-Robric Ladon":** Alternate change in the position of the hand on the table, first one, then the other, then both at the same time.
    *   **Mirror drawing:** Simultaneous drawing of symmetrical patterns with both hands.
*   **Body control and rhythm:**
    *   **Rhythmic tapping:** Reproduction or creation of simple rhythms, taping them with palms on the table.
    *   **Dancing:** Any dances requiring repetition of movements.
    *   **Games with the ball:** Throwing the ball from hand to hand, blows against the wall and fishing.

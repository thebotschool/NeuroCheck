### The cognitive profile of the student

This report offers a deep analysis of the cognitive abilities of your student. He is designed to help parents and teachers create optimal conditions for learning and development, revealing strengths and offering support in growth zones.

### 1. Test results: review

*   **Long -term attention (CPT):** Difficulties with sustainable retention of concentration and memorization of sequences were noted. This can be a sign of fatigue or temporary cognitive load.
*   **Self-control (Go/No-Go):** A strong ability to contain impulsive reactions has been revealed. The child demonstrates a high level of deliberation of actions and resistance to distracting factors.
*   **Visual working memory (TCP):** The results indicate the current cognitive overload. Attention can be unstable, which affects information processing.

**Key conclusion:** In the presence of pronounced potential for self -regulation, the cognitive system of the student is currently experiencing overload. This does not indicate a deficiency of abilities, but rather of temporary depletion of resources. The goal is to reduce the load and implement effective support strategies.

### 2. The guide for the success of your student

#### Individual characteristics

*   **Pace:** Thoughtful and leisurely. It takes time to comprehend the task and planning actions. Hasting often leads to errors.
*   **Perception:** It more effectively assimilates information presented in the form of small, logical blocks with visual elements (diagrams, lists, color accents).
*   **Focus of attention:** It has developed self -control. However, the current overload can cause distraction, especially when working with significant volumes of new or complex information.

#### Supporting factors

*   **Step -by -step instructions:** One task, one step. The next action is voiced only after the end of the previous one.
*   **Visualization:** The use of diagrams, Mind Cart, stickers with keywords helps to structure information.
*   **The clearness of the formulations:** Short and specific instructions. For example: "Collect toys in the box" Instead of "put the order."
*   **Predictability:** It is important to report a plan of action in advance and warn about a change in activity in 5-10 minutes.
*   **Silence:** Minimization of distracting sounds (music, TV, conversations) is critical during classes.

#### Factors that make the process difficult

*   **Multitasking:** The simultaneous performance of several tasks or frequent switching reduce productivity and cause fatigue.
*   **Long oral instructions:** Only the initial and final parts are remembered, the middle is lost.
*   **High pace:** The requirement to act quickly provokes stress and prevents thoughtfulness.
*   **Visual and audial noise:** Infant and loud sounds exhaust the limited resource of attention.

### 3. Recommendations to support

#### For the house

*   **Organization of work:**
    *   Divide homework into blocks for 15–20 minutes. Take short breaks (5 minutes) using a timer.
    *   Start with the most complex items while the concentration is maximum.
    *   Together, make a simple written plan: 1. Mathematics (No. 5, No. 6). 2. Rest. 3. Russian (ex. 10).
    *   Concentrated attention remains for about 15–20 minutes, after which a short rest is required.
*   **Support and encouragement:**
    *   Do not blame for moments of distraction. Delicately return to the task with the question: "What did we stop at? What is the next step?"
    *   Praise not for speed, but for the diligence and ability to deliberate actions.
    *   Discuss the condition of the child: "I see you are tired. We will take a break, we will breathe or drink water." This contributes to the development of introspection.

#### For school

*   **The wording of tasks:**
    *   Duplicate oral instructions in writing on a board or card.
    *   Select the keywords in the tasks (for example, verbs: “emphasize”, “write down”, “compare”).
*   **Lesson structure and environment:**
    *   The optimal place is in the center of the class, away from distracting factors (windows, doors).
    *   At the beginning of the lesson, voice a short plan: "Today we will check the DZ, study the new topic and complete the exercise."
    *   Warn the time left until the end of the task: "You have 5 minutes left."
*   **Individual approach (IOM):**
    *   Allow me to use the line for following the lines when reading.
    *   Give the opportunity to use a draft or plan before the final answer.
    *   In the test work, provide for additional time, given its pace of work.

### 4. Recommended training

These exercises will strengthen attention and memory, and also help to use strengths (self -control).

*   **For attention:**
    *   **Correct samples:** On the text page in 3-5 minutes, cross out all the letters "A" or circle the prepositions "B".
    *   **"Find 10 differences":** Classic exercise for training concentration and visual search.
*   **For coordination and self -regulation:**
    *   **Balance:** Stand on one leg with open, then with your eyes closed.
    *   **Juggling:** Start with two balls. Develops the coordination of the "eye-hand" and concentration.
*   **For working memory:**
    *   **"What has changed?":** Lay out 5-7 items. Give 30 seconds to remember. Then ask to turn away and remove/change one item.
    *   **Repetition of sequences:** Start with a simple one: "Remember and repeat: 5-8-2-4." Gradually increase the length of the row (with words).
*   **For rhythm:**
    *   **RIGHT RIGHTS:** Back the simple rhythm on the table. Ask to repeat exactly. Gradually complicate.

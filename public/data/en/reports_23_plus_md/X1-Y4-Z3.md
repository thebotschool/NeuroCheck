# Cognitive assessment
## Report on test results (23+ years)

This report is intended for parents and teachers. It offers a detailed analysis of the student’s cognitive profile, identifying key strengths and areas for further development. The goal is to provide practical recommendations for creating an optimal environment of training and support.

<!-- page-break -->

# 1. Review of tests
## Assessment of cognitive functions

*   **Attention (CPT):** There is a tendency to quick, hasty answers. Actions are ahead of the complete comprehension of information.
*   **Braking (Go/No-Go):** Difficulties with maintaining concentration and control of impulsive reactions were noted, especially in conditions of fatigue or stress.
*   **Visual working memory:** Highly developed. The ability to effectively memorize, preserve and structure significant volumes of visual information.

## Key conclusion

High cognitive potential is supported by excellent visual memory. However, the impulsiveness and features of self -control can complicate its complete disclosure. The probable reason is overwork or stress.

<!-- page-break -->

# 2. Profile of learning
## Individual characteristics

*   **Pace:** Fast, impulsive. Actions often precede the complete understanding of the task, which leads to errors in carelessness.
*   **Perception:** Optimally assimilates clear visual information. Schemes, tables, intelligence cards, checklists are effective perception tools. Long oral monologues or continuous text are more complicated.
*   **Attention:** Unstable. Deep concentration is possible thanks to developed memory, but is easily disturbed due to impulsiveness. External "anchors" are required to hold the focus.

## Success factors

*   **Visual tips:** Step-by-step instructions, checklists, highlighting keywords help organize thoughts.
*   **Planning:** A brief discussion of steps before starting work ("Where do we start? What is the second step? How will we check?").
*   **Pause:** Slowdown techniques. For example, the rule "Read the task twice" or "Count to three before the answer."
*   **Clear rhythm:** Work in short intervals with mandatory breaks (for example, a method of prying: 25 minutes of work, 5 minutes of rest).
*   **Working environment:** A quiet, organized atmosphere with a minimum of distracting factors.

## Obstacles

*   **Multitasking:** A rapid switching between different types of tasks depletes the resource of self -control and enhances impulsiveness.
*   **Faring instructions:** Long oral explanations without visual support are quickly lost.
*   **Time pressure:** Hard terms provoke automatic reactions, bypassing analytical thinking.
*   **Distracting factors:** The mess on the table, open tabs on the computer, extraneous sounds compete for attention and reduce concentration.

<!-- page-break -->

# 3. Recommendations
## For the house

*   **Homework:**
    *   Miss large tasks into 2-3 small subtasibles. Fix them with a list.
    *   On the table should only be materials for the current subtasibility.
    *   Introduce the ritual: "First we remove the phone, then we start."
*   **Support and encouragement:**
    *   Praise for attentiveness and following the plan, and not for speed. Questions: "Have you checked yourself? I did everything according to the instructions (a)?"
    *   Replace the phrase "Think faster" with "not in a hurry, I will wait. The main thing is the correctness."
*   **Concentration:**
    *   Realistic time of continuous concentration is 20–25 minutes. Then a short physical break is needed (walk, water, look out the window).

## For school

*   **The wording of tasks:**
    *   Duplicate oral instructions in writing. Use numbering, lists, text highlighting.
    *   Add reminders: "Check: are all points made?"
*   **Lesson structure:**
    *   Serve new material with a visual support: presentations with diagrams, diagrams, supporting abstracts. This activates a strong visual memory.
    *   Place the student closer to the teacher and the board, away from the window or door to minimize distraction.
*   **Style style:**
    *   Speak calmly, measured. Make short pauses after key thoughts.
    *   With an oral survey, give time to think. You can offer a reception: inhale-out of the answer.
*   **Individual route:**
    *   In control and independent work, the provision of 5-10 minutes of additional time is useful. This will reduce stress from haste and will allow the use of memory to verify and correct errors.

# 4. Training of skills
The proposed exercises develop self -control, attention and coordination "thought - action."

## Attention and control

*   **Graphic dictations:** They train to follow the instructions and timely stop. Example: "Two cells to the right, one cell up, stop, one cage to the left."
*   **The game "Yes and no, do not say":** Teaches to control automatic speech reactions.

## Coordination and balance

*   **Juggling:** You can start with two balls. Develops concentration, peripheral vision and interhemispheric ties.
*   **Balance exercises:** Walking along the line, stand on one leg (with open, then closed eyes).

## Memory and strategy

*   **Board games-strategies:** Chess, checkers, "Karcasson", "Colonialists". They teach to plan actions for several moves ahead and evaluate the consequences of decisions.
*   **Game "Find a couple" (Memory):** Complicated versions train working memory in a playful way.

## Rhythm and awareness

*   **RIGHT RIGHTS:** The exact repetition of the proposed rhythm. Gradual complication.
*   **Breathing "on the square":** Inhale at 4 counts, a delay of 4 accounts, exhale at 4 counts, a delay of 4 accounts. This simple exercise helps to "ground", reduce stress and impulsiveness before the task.

# Neurocheck. Cognitive assessment report.
**Age: 23+ years**

This report reveals the cognitive profile of the student. It analyzes the strengths and areas for development, offering practical strategies to create an optimal environment of training and growth.

<!-- page-break -->

## 1. Analysis of tests

### Attention and memory (CPT)
Difficulties with distinguishing between visual parts with a high information load have been noticed. This indicates the need for data filtering, and not a decrease in memory.

### Self-control (Go/No-Go)
The tendency to impulsiveness has been revealed. The inhibition of the reaction is difficult, possibly due to fatigue or stress.

### Decision -making speed (TCP)
A fast pace of action has been noted. The student is inclined to proceed with the tasks until complete instructions, which increases the risk of inattentive errors.

<!-- page-break -->

## 2. Profile of a student

### Individual characteristics
*   **Pace.** Tall, swift. The student is inclined to start the tasks without listening to the instructions. Switching quickly.
*   **Perception.** Preference: simple, structured visual information (diagrams, images, lists). Long texts or saturated pages cause overload and decrease in concentration.
*   **Attention.** Selective, but unstable. It is able to deeply concentrate on an interesting one. It is easily distracted by external stimuli (noise, movement). It is quickly tired of monotony.

### What supports
*   Step -by -step instructions.
*   Visual anchors: backlight, stickers, short checklists.
*   Clear rhythm: work on the timer (for example, 20 minutes of work, 5 minutes of rest).
*   Preliminary planning: pronouncing steps before starting.
*   A quiet, organized environment without unnecessary noise.

### What prevents
*   Multitasking.
*   Fast oral change of tasks without visual support.
*   Long, monotonous instructions.
*   Hurry and excessive pressure.
*   Overloaded workspace.

<!-- page-break -->

## 3. Practical recommendations

### At home
*   **Organization of tasks.** Divide the work into blocks for 15–20 minutes. Provide 5-minute breaks for physical activity. Use the timer. Make a simple plan (for example, 1. Mathematics, 2. Reading) with the possibility of crossing off points.
*   **Support and promotion.** Mark the process and self -control, not just the result. Example: “I noticed how you double -check” or “Great that you listened.” Organize the workplace: only the necessary items on the table.
*   **Holding attention.** The optimal concentration time on one task: 15–20 minutes. Exceeding this limit is counterproductive.

### At school
*   **The formulation of tasks.** Principle: One sentence is one action. After the instructions, pause, ask the student to repeat the task. This helps to concentrate.
*   **The structure of the lesson.** Duplicate oral instructions visually (plan, room on the board). Place key information uniformly. Use an interactive approach with a frequent change of activity (explanation, practice, work in pairs, independent work).
*   **Individual route.**
    *   **Placement.** The first/second desk in the center to minimize abstracts.
    *   **Support.** Allow the use of a line for reading, a sheet for closing tasks.
    *   **Examination.** Give additional time to control the work performed.

## 4. Training and games

These classes are integrated into everyday life. They develop key functions that require support, not feeling like routine exercises.

### Attention and visual perception
*   **Corrective test.** Crossing out a given letter in the text for a while.
*   **Find the differences.** Classical game for concentration on details.
*   **The board game "Dobble" (Dobble/Spot it!).** Develops reaction speed and visual search.

### Self -control and braking
*   **Edible-universal.** He trains a quick solution and inhibition of the reaction.
*   **Traffic light / sea is worried once.** Develops the ability to stop in time at the signal.
*   **Rhythm tap.** The exact repetition of the proposed rhythm.

### Working memory and coordination
*   **Memory (find a couple).** Classical game of memory development.
*   **What has changed?** Removing and searching for changes among objects.
*   **Graphic dictations.** Drawing on cells under dictation develops coordination and spatial attention.

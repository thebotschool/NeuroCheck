### Cognitive profile: Age 23+

This report reveals the cognitive features of your student. It is formed on the basis of three tests that evaluate attention, self -control and working memory. The goal is to create an optimal environment for development.

### 1. Evaluation of cognitive functions

*   **Attention for stability (CPT):** The difficulty in distinguishing between visually similar details is noted, especially with a rapid stream of information. The schoolboy is experiencing difficulties with cutting off visually similar, but incorrect elements.
*   **Braking / self-control test (Go / No-Go):** The tendency to quick, impulsive actions has been identified. The student often begins to perform the task without fully comprehending the instructions, which leads to hasty errors.
*   **Working memory test (TCP):** Difficulties are observed with keeping attention on one task for more than 15-20 minutes. Distribution and loss of the thread of the instructions with a long explanation is noted.

### 2. Your schoolboy: Features of success

This is a student’s profile, who needs to need a structure, predictability and support in the development of a deliberate approach to achieve success.

#### Key features

*   **Pace:** Impulsive, fast. The action is characteristic "first I do, then I think." Errors arise due to haste, not ignorance.
*   **Perception:** Optimally perceives simple, clear visual information. Overloaded images, small font or excess parts cause fatigue and provoke errors.
*   **Focus of attention:** Dynamic. The student is able to concentrate on short periods (15-20 minutes). After this, a short break or a change of activity to restore concentration is required.

#### Supporting factors

*   **Step -by -step instructions:** Separation of large tasks into 2-3 sequential stages.
*   **Visual anchors:** The use of short checklist, highlighting keywords, the use of a line to preserve the line during reading.
*   **Clear rhythm:** The use of a timer for working intervals. Speaking of the rules. Using rhythmic setting exercises.
*   **Pause before action:** The formation of the habit of "look, think, do." Please take a breath-output and say the task before starting.

#### Calls in training

*   **Multitasking:** The simultaneous performance of several actions (for example, listen and write down) leads to a loss of information.
*   **Long oral instructions:** Only the beginning or end of a long monologue is perceived.
*   **Visual and audial noise:** Loud sounds, a mess on the desk, a complex design of materials distract attention.
*   **Rush and pressure on time:** Phrases of the type “Come on faster” enhance impulsiveness and increase the number of errors.

### 3. Support and development

#### Home environment

*   **Homework organization:**
    *   Divide the tasks into blocks for 15-20 minutes. Provide short breaks (2-5 minutes) for physical activity.
    *   Use a kitchen timer as an external focus signal.
    *   Start with the most complex or least favorite objects while the energy level is high.
    *   The workplace should be as free from extra items.
*   **Support and encouragement:**
    *   Praise not for the result, but for **effort and process**: "I see how you tried not to rush." "Well done, that I checked the work." This forms self -control.
    *   Instead of “you are distracted again,” say: “I see you are tired. Let's take a break for 3 minutes. ”

#### School environment

*   **The wording of tasks:**
    *   Let's briefly, clearly, one at a time. Pause between stages. Example: “Open the textbook on page 25. (Pause). Find exercise 5. (Pause). Read the first task. "
    *   Duplicate oral instructions on the board in the form of a simple plan or diagram.
*   **Lesson structuring:**
    *   The perfect place for such a student is the first desk, in the center of the class. This minimizes distracting factors.
    *   Integrate the elements of the interactive: ask to repeat the rule, go to the board. This activates attention.
*   **Elements of an individual educational route:**
    *   Give the opportunity to use a ruler or a clean sheet to close part of the text.
    *   In control work, use a small checklist for self-testing: “Check all examples?”, “Wrote your name?”.
    *   Allow the short "physical" at the desk if the student loses concentration.

### 4. Practical exercises

The presented exercises and games are aimed at developing key cognitive skills. Integrate them into everyday life.

*   **For attention and visual memory:**
    *   **"Find 10 differences":** Develops attentiveness to details.
    *   **"Corrective test":** In 1 minute, cross out all the letters "A" in the text. Gradually complicate, adding the second letter.
    *   **Board games:** “Memory”, “Dobble”, “Drum” - train the reaction, attention and control in the framework of clear rules.
*   **For self -control and inhibition (work with impulsiveness):**
    *   **"Edible - inedible":** A game with a ball requiring a quick decision on the reaction.
    *   **"The sea is worried once":** He teaches the ability to freeze in time by signal, controlling the body.
    *   **“No, don't say”:** Develops an attentive hearing and control over a speech response.
*   **For rhythm and bodily control:**
    *   **"Rhythmic echo":** Reproduction of a given rhythm (cotton, knocks). Synchronizes the nervous system.
    *   **Walking along the line:** Walking along the drawn line (forward, backward, socks) develops coordination and concentration.

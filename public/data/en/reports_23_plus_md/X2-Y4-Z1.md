# Individual report
## Cognitive profile of a student (23+ years old)

This report contains an analysis of cognitive features and practical recommendations. Its goal is to support the development of the student, creating optimal conditions for learning and growth.

## 1. Assessment of competencies

*   **Attention (CPT):** With a high load or fast stream of information, the student can confuse similar visual details. This indicates a need for more accurate data processing in conditions of intensive effects.
*   **Self-regulation (Go/No-Go):** The tendency to impulsive actions or complexity with a timely stop is noted. Often this is a consequence of fatigue or overload of the nervous system.
*   **Working memory (TCP):** In optimal conditions, a high ability to concentrate and self -control is manifested. This is a strong side that should be based on development.

## 2. The guide for the success of your student

This section explains how the schoolboy assimilates information, and what contributes to his success in learning.

### Individual characteristics

*   **Pace:** The tendency to a quick, impulsive pace is manifested. An external structure is required to slow down and prevent errors due to haste.
*   **Perception:** Optimally perceives visual information if it is clearly structured. Excess or chaos in visual representation reduces effectiveness.
*   **Focus of attention:** Attention is excited in nature. Deep concentration is possible on interesting tasks, but is quickly exhausted from monotony or complexity. Fatigue is the main factor in the reduction of control.

### Support factors

*   **Step -by -step instructions:** Miss large tasks into specific, measurable steps.
*   **Visual supports:** Use checklists, markers for isolation, short schemes instead of lengthy texts.
*   **Estimated rhythm:** Adjust the working intervals with a timer (for example, 15 minutes of work, 5 minutes of rest).
*   **Predictability:** The stable daily routine and understandable rules reduce anxiety, releasing energy for study.
*   **Focus on one task:** Avoid multitasking. The student’s brain at this stage copes better with the consistent processing of information.

### Obstacles

*   **Multitasking:** Simultaneous implementation of several tasks (for example, listen and write down).
*   **Visual overload:** Overloaded with illustrations or small print materials.
*   **Rush:** The pressure of time or calls to acceleration increase the number of errors.
*   **Multi -stage oral instructions:** More than two successive teams are difficult to keep in memory.
*   **Distracting factors:** Background noise, a mess at the workplace significantly reduce concentration.

## 3. Practical recommendations

### For the house

*   **Educational process:**
    *   Miss large tasks into small stages. For example, instead of "make all mathematics" - "solve three problems."
    *   Use the timer: 15–20 minutes of work, then a short break (water, movement). This is the optimal concentration time.
    *   Create an evening check list. The mark of the completed cases strengthens the sense of control and achievement.
*   **Motivation and support:**
    *   Encourage efforts, and not just the result: “I see how you tried and tested yourself. Great work!”, “You could not rush, and this reduced the mistakes.”
    *   Instead of criticism of impulsiveness, calmly state the fact and offer the decision: "It seems you are tired and hurried. We will take a break."
*   **Workplace:**
    *   On the table there should be only objects necessary for the current task. Remove the rest.

### For school

*   **Tasks and instructions:**
    *   Formulate the instructions briefly, clearly, one at a time. Duplicate oral instructions in writing (on the board).
    *   Highlight keywords in tasks (for example, *Write out* only *nouns*).
*   **Dynamics of the lesson:**
    *   Alternate activities every 15–20 minutes (letter, oral answers, work with visual materials, short warm -ups).
    *   Warn about changing activities in advance: "In five minutes, we will finish writing and move on to work in pairs."
*   **Location in the class:**
    *   Optimally-the first-second desk, away from distracting factors (window, door).
*   **Individual route:**
    *   It is recommended to provide additional time for control and independent work.
    *   Allow the use of simple visual organizers (for example, a reading line, a clean sheet for closing part of the page).

## 4. Developing exercises

The presented games and exercises are easily integrated into everyday life, contributing to the development of key skills.

*   **Development of attention:**
    *   **"Find the differences"** And **"Corrective test"** (deleting a given letter in the text).
    *   **The board game "Dobble" (spot it!):** He trains reaction speed and visual scanning.
*   **Coordination and control:**
    *   **Games with the ball:** Throwing with a given rhythm, fishing after cotton.
    *   **Balancing board (Balanceboard):** Daily short classes improve bodily perception and control.
*   **Memory and rhythm:**
    *   **Memory game:** Search for paired cards.
    *   **Rhythmic exercises:** Repetition of the rhythm (clap, tapping). Start slowly, gradually accelerating.
*   **Impulse control:**
    *   **Games "Delay" / "The sea is worried once":** They teach instant stopping at the signal.
    *   **Board games with alternate strokes** (for example, "Jenga", "Uno", checkers): develop patience and follow the rules.

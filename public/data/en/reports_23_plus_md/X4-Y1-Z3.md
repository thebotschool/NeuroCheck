## Cognitive profile of a student (23+ years old)

This account reveals the unique cognitive profile of the student, based on the test results. It is designed to help parents and teachers identify strengths, determine the development zones and create an optimal environment for training.

---

## 1. Analysis of cognitive functions

*   **Sustainability of attention (CPT):** The ability to long -term concentration on the same tasks is limited, especially with fatigue or stress. Removing sequences requires effort.
*   **Impulse control (Go/No-Go):** High level of control over impulsive actions. The ability to effectively “slow down” in the presence of clear rules is a key strong side.
*   **Visual working memory (TCP):** A tendency to quick, hasty decisions is noted. The full amount of available information is not always used before taking an answer.

**General profile:** The profile of the "sprinter" is presented. High efficiency in short, dynamic tasks requiring a quick reaction and control. On the contrary, long and monotonous tasks quickly deplete the reserve of attention, which can lead to hasty conclusions.

## 2. Understanding your student

### Features of perception

*   **Pace:** Fast, but with the need for pauses. Capable of intensive intellectual effort, requiring short -term "rebooting".
*   **Information format:** The best assimilation is achieved with a clear, structured and visualized feed (diagrams, lists, color selection). Volumetric verbal instructions without visual support lose their effectiveness.
*   **Focus of attention:** Intensive, but short. Deep concentration is possible for 15–20 minutes, after which the attention begins to dissipate.

### Supporting environment

*   **Step -by -step instructions:** The principle of "one task is one step." Decomposition of complex tasks for simple, consistent stages.
*   **Visual timers:** The use of sand clocks or electronic timers for a visual demonstration of the remaining time to complete the task.
*   **Check lists and schemes:** Presenting tasks in the form of lists for the mark of the executed points. This increases the sense of control and completeness.
*   **Short working intervals:** Effective method "Prydor" (20 minutes of work / 5 minutes of break).
*   **Physical activity:** The possibility of motor warm -up in breaks helps to relieve stress and restore concentration.

### Factors distracting from study

*   **Multitasking:** The requirement of simultaneous retention of several instructions or switching between different types of tasks.
*   **Monotone:** Long lectures or the same type of exercises that cause depletion of the resource of attention.
*   **Pressure and rush:** History increases the likelihood of impulsive, rash answers.
*   **Unstructured information:** A continuous text without paragraphs, lists and discharge is created by a barrier for perception.
*   **Noise and distracting factors:** Due to the features of focus retention, external stimuli can easily break the workflow.

## 3. Practical recommendations

### For the home environment

*   **Homework:**
    *   Separate the tasks into short blocks for 15–20 minutes. Between blocks-a 5-minute break (for example, drink water, move).
    *   Use the checklist: Write down all tasks for the day, celebrating the completed. This forms a sense of control.
    *   Start with the most difficult object while the level of attention is maximum.
    *   Organize the workplace by minimizing distracting factors.
*   **Support and development:**
    *   Noting the speed, but the process: “You carefully checked all the details, it's great!”, “You have planned the solution of the problem well.”
    *   Form the habit of a pause before the answer. Ask a question and suggest: "Think 10 seconds, and then answer."
    *   Discuss the consequences of hasty decisions on household examples, without condemnation.
*   **Optimal concentration:** The realistic period of continuous concentrated work is **15–20 minutes**.

### For the school environment

*   **The wording of tasks:**
    *   The most clear, short, step -by -step instructions. Optimal - provide one instruction at a time.
    *   Written instructions (on the board, card) are preferable to oral ones.
*   **Lesson structure:**
    *   Alternate activities every 15–20 minutes (explanation, work at the board, independent practice, discussion).
    *   Apply visual materials: diagrams, intelligence cards, color selection of keywords, short videos.
*   **Organization of space:** The optimal place is the first-second desk, in the center. This minimizes distraction and provides constant visual contact with the teacher.
*   **Dynamics of learning:** Use an interactive, dynamic feed style. Ask questions, actively involve in the discussion. Instead of "Who knows the answer?" You can suggest: "A minute for thinking, then I will ask ..."
*   **Individual approach (IOOM elements):**
    *   Permission to use a simple timer for self -control of time.
    *   Providing tasks in the format of printed checklists.
    *   An additional minute for checking the test work before delivery.

## 4. Developing exercises

These exercises will help strengthen attention and patience, as well as develop strengths, such as control and reaction.

*   ### attention and memory
    *   **"Graphic dictations":** Drawing on cells for dictation (for example, "one cage to the right, two up"). Develops auditory attention and retention of the sequence.
    *   **"Find 10 differences":** A classic game for training concentration and visual attention.
    *   **The game "Remember and repeat":** Show a sequence of 3-5 items, then cover them and ask for a reproduce order. Gradually complicate.

*   ### Control and Rhythm
    *   **The game "Sea is worried once" or "Frozen!":** Develops a rapid reaction to the signal and bodily control.
    *   **Tapping rhythms:** You tapping a simple rhythm, the child exactly repeats. He trains attention and coordination.

*   ### Impulsion Management
    *   **Board games:** Chess, chess, UNO, Jenga and similar games requiring the expectation of their course, strategic thinking and suppression of immediate action.
    *   **Labyrinths:** Passing the labyrinths with a pencil, without touching the walls, develops planning and accuracy, slowing down the pace.

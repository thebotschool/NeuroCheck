# Cognitive development report

This report provides an in -depth look at the cognitive features of a student aged 23+ years. It is designed to highlight the strengths and identify areas for development, offering practical recommendations for parents and teachers to create an optimal educational environment.

---

### 1. Key indicators

**Memory on the sequence**
Effective memorization of individual elements. However, the retention of their proper order or sequence is difficult.

**Inhibition and reaction**
Asymmetry in the actions of the hands when performing tasks is noted. This indicates the need to strengthen interhemispheric interaction to improve the reaction rate in dynamic situations.

**Sustainability of attention**
Long -term retention of concentration on monotonous tasks is difficult. Attention is manifested by bursts of activity, not a uniform stream.

### 2. Individual learning profile

#### Key features

*   **The pace of assimilation:** Thoughtful, requiring time for processing and structuring information. Haster can violate the sequence of perception.
*   **Perception format:** Visual-structural. Effective assimilation occurs through schemes, lists, mental cards and step -by -step instructions. Oral explanations require visual support.
*   **Focus of attention:** Periodic. A high concentration on an interesting task is possible up to 15–20 minutes, after which a short break or a change in activity is required.

#### Effective approaches

*   **Step -by -step instructions:** Decomposition of tasks on 3-5 numb steps.
*   **Visual supports:** Check lists, schemes, highlighting key elements, short videos.
*   **Rhythm and movement:** The use of rhythmic and physical exercises to memorize sequences (for example, cross steps).
*   **Time management:** Using a timer for work and rest cycles (for example, 20/5 minutes).
*   **Predictability:** A clear schedule and plan reduce the cognitive load.

#### Factors that reduce efficiency

*   **Multitasking:** The simultaneous implementation of several tasks reduces the assimilation of information.
*   **Long oral instructions:** The absence of pauses and visual clues overloads the memory.
*   **Rush and pressure:** Worsen accuracy and enhance the asymmetry of actions.
*   **Monotone:** Quickly exhausts attention. Tasks for more than 20 minutes without a break are ineffective.
*   **Background noise:** Significantly distracts, interfering with concentration.

### 3. Practical recommendations

#### Home environment

*   **Organization of the workplace:** Limit items on the table to the current task.
*   **Plan of execution:** Make a checklist of the tasks, noting the completed points.
*   **Method "Prydor":** Use the timer (for example, 20 minutes of work, 5 minutes of rest).

*   **Promotion:** Mark efforts and process, and not just the result.
*   **Structuring information:** Ask questions that help build a chronology or structure of events (beginning, development, end).

*   **Expectations for attention:** 15-20 minutes of focused work, then a short active break is required.

#### School environment

*   **Clarity of tasks:** Decomposition of tasks for short, numbered points.
*   **Duplication of instructions:** Written duplication of oral instructions (board, cards).

*   **Lesson plan:** Sound and write down a short plan of the lesson on the board.
*   **Place in the class:** The first-second desk to minimize distraction and the best review.

*   **Visualization:** Use schemes, infographics, tables. Visualization of algorithms increases assimilation.
*   **Coordination activity:** Enter the actions that require bilateral coordination (for example, erasing the boards with two hands).

*   **Additional materials:** Providing written copies of tasks from the board.
*   **Tools:** The ability to use a timer in independent work.
*   **Grade:** Division of tests into short, controlled blocks.

### 4. Developing exercises

The following exercises contribute to the development of key skills. Regularity is more important than the duration: it is preferable to 10 minutes daily than long sessions once a week.

#### Attention and consistent memory

*   **Graphic dictations:** Develop auditory attention and following instructions.
*   **The game "Remember and repeat":** Reproduction of sequences (sounds, movements, objects).
*   **Board games:** “Memory” (visual memory), “set” (attention), “insidious fox” (logic, planning).

#### Coordination and interhemispheric interaction

*   **"Crossroads":** Alternated touches of opposite knees with hands. Slowly, you can do with musical accompaniment.
*   **"Mirror":** Synchronous repetition of the partner’s movements as a reflection.
*   **Juggling:** Start with two items. Effective for coordination and interhemispheric ties.

#### Rhythm and bodily control

*   **Fighting rhythm:** Repetition of rhythmic drawings (hands, tools).
*   **Dancing:** Dance games or ligaments requiring memorization and reproduction of movements.

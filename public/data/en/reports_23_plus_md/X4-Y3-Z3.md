# Cognitive assessment report
### Attention, self -control, coordination (23+ years)

This report is your guide to understand the unique abilities of the student. It contains practical recommendations for more effective education, both at home and at school.

## 1. Test results
A brief overview of the cognitive profile.

*   **Attention and memory:** It can be difficult for a student to hold concentration and sequentially remember information, especially with fatigue or excitement.
*   **Self -control:** There is a tendency to impulsive actions. The schoolboy can start completing the task until complete instructions, which leads to hasty errors.
*   **Coordination:** The difference in the work of the hands is noted, which indicates the ongoing development of interhemispheric connections. Work on coordination of movements will be useful.

## 2. Success guide
Understanding an individual approach to learning.

### Features of perception

*   **Pace:** Fast, impulsive. The tendency to act on the principle of "I do first, then I think."
*   **The format of instructions:** Short, clear, visually supported instructions are more effective. Long oral explanations can be less digestible.
*   **Focus of attention:** Unstable. Periods of intensive concentration are about **10-15 minutes**, after which a short break or change of activity is needed.

### Which helps training

*   **Visual supports:** Check lists, schemes, cards with a plan of action ("First ... then ... at the end ...").
*   **Step -by -step instructions:** Tasks, divided into small, specific stages. Give no more than one instruction at a time.
*   **Timer:** The use of sand clocks or a visual timer helps the student feel the time and work in a given rhythm.
*   **Rhythm and movement:** Short physical pauses, the ability to tap the rhythm or speak an action plan aloud.
*   **Predictability:** A clear daily routine and a lesson plan. When the student knows what will happen next, his level of anxiety is reduced, and attention is growing.

### What creates difficulties

*   **Multitasking:** The request to do several things at the same time.
*   **Rush and pressure:** Phrases that cause stress (“Come on faster”, “it's easy”).
*   **Long oral explanations:** Monotonous speech without support for a picture or diagram.
*   **Distracting factors:** Visual and audial noise (many objects on the table, loud sounds).
*   **Unstructured tasks:** Open tasks without a clear plan and support issues.

## 3. Recommendations
Practical approaches for home and school.

### For the house

#### Organization of homework

*   Work intervals: **15 minutes of work / 5 minutes of break**. Breaks should be active.
*   Perform one task at a time. Remove everything superfluous from the table.
*   Use a checklist for tasks. Distribution of points gives a feeling of control and completeness.

#### Support and promotion

*   Praise effort and process, and not just the result: “I see how you tried!”, “It's great that you followed the plan!”
*   Before a difficult task together, make a plan: “What will we do first? What then? "

#### Reducing impulsiveness

*   Introduce the “Stop-signal”: before the answer or action, ask the student to count up to three to himself or take a deep breath.

### For school

#### The formulation of tasks

*   Miss large assignments into 2-3 subparagraphs.
*   The instructions should be short, clear, preferably with visual support (picture, a pattern on the board).

#### The structure of the lesson

*   At the beginning of the lesson, voice a short plan: "Today we will repeat it first, then we will write a dictation, and at the end we will play."
*   Place the schoolboy **Closer to the teacher (first-second desk), away from the window or door**to minimize distracting factors.

#### Material feed style

*   Speak calmly, use gestures. Continue the schoolchild with a simple issue for returning attention.
*   After the instructions, it is useful to ask the student to repeat it: “Repeat, please, what needs to be done now?”

#### Elements of an individual route

*   Allow the use of a visual timer on the desk.
*   Provide subscription cards with algorithms of standard tasks (for example, analysis of a word in composition).
*   Provide the opportunity for a “legal” movement - ask to distribute notebooks, erase from the board.

## 4. Games and training
The development of skills through fascinating activity.

### Attention and self -control

*   **Graphic dictations:** "One cage to the right, two cells up ...". They train auditory attention and ability to follow the instructions.
*   **Correct samples:** Discarding certain letters in the text (for example, all letters “A” or “O”).
*   **The game "Delay!":** The schoolboy moves to the music, and when it is stopped, it must immediately freeze.

### Coordination and interhemispheric interaction

*   **Synchronous drawing:** Drawing in the air or on a large sheet of paper with both hands at the same time (circles, waves, symmetrical patterns).
*   **Balance exercises:** Walking along the line, a stand on one leg (you can with eyes closed).
*   **Finger gymnastics:** Simple exercises with fingers (“rings”, “locks”), games with the ball (throwing, beaten from the wall).

### Working memory and sequence

*   **"Remember and repeat":** Repetition of sequences of 4-5 words, numbers or movements.
*   **The board game "Memory" ("Find a couple"):** He trains visual memory and concentration perfectly.
*   **"Cook":** Memorization and bringing of 3-4 items from another room (“Please bring the Red Book, blue pencil and eraser”).

# Cognitive assessment report

Assessment of the test results of your student. This report will help to understand the strengths and growth zones, forming the basis for creating an effective environment for development and training.

---

### 1. The main indicators

A brief overview of the results of key cognitive tests.

*   **Attention: visual filtering.**
    Difficulties are noted with distinguishing similar elements, especially with high information load or fatigue. This indicates the need for support for filtering visual information.

*   **Self -control: High level.**
    Strong control of pulses has been demonstrated. The accuracy and deliberation of solutions increase with clarity of the problem and the absence of overload.

*   **General resource: exhaustion.**
    The current state is characterized by mental overload. Unstable attention and uneven reactions are a consequence of cognitive fatigue.

**Bottom line:**
The child has a significant potential for self -control, but at the moment is exhausted. Errors are interpreted as an indicator of cognitive overload, and not the lack of abilities.

### 2. The profile of the student

The guide for the success of your student. Understanding individual characteristics for effective learning.

#### Key features

*   **Pace.**
    Prefers a measured approach. It takes time to immerse in a task and detailed analysis. Hurry provokes errors.

*   **Perception.**
    It more efficiently assimilates structured, step -by -step information. Visual noise (a lot of text, images) complicates concentration.

*   **Concentration.**
    Capable of deep concentration, but for a limited period (15-20 minutes). The resource is quickly depleted, attention becomes scattered.

#### Support: What contributes to progress

*   **Singlety.** Focusing on one action.
*   **Visual cleanliness.** Clear instructions, allocation of key points.
*   **Step -by -step algorithms.** Separation of tasks into small, consistent steps.
*   **Predictable rhythm.** A clear schedule of classes and rest.
*   **Pauses.** Short breaks (3-5 minutes) for "rebooting".

#### Calls: What makes the process complicate

*   **Multitasking.** Fulfillment of several cases at the same time.
*   **Fast shift.** Lack of pauses when switching between objects.
*   **Rush.** Tough time frame for tasks.
*   **Noise.** Distracting visual or auditory factors.

### 3. Recommendations

Practical tips for creating a supporting and developing environment.

#### Home environment

*   **Organization of tasks.**
    *   Divide homework into blocks for 15-20 minutes.
    *   Provide a 5-minute break between blocks (drinking water, look out the window, light movements).
    *   Start with the most difficult tasks while the resource of attention is maximum.
    *   Use a draft to relieve fear of error.

*   **Motivation and support.**
    *   Praise not for the result, but for efforts and the process: "I see how you tried and was attentive!"
    *   Recognize fatigue: "I see you are tired. Let's rest." This teaches the child to recognize his condition.
    *   Extract his self -control: "It is great that you did not give up and ended the matter!"

*   **Working space.**
    *   Organize the table so that it is only necessary for the current task.

#### School environment

*   **Tasks: clearness and structure.**
    *   The instructions should be short and step -by -step, duplicated orally and in writing (on the board, card).
    *   Visually highlight key points: **fatty** Text, emphasizing.

*   **Lesson: optimal atmosphere.**
    *   The recommended place is the first-second desk, in the center, to minimize abstracts.
    *   Before an important task, give a minute to concentration.

*   **Material feed: teacher style.**
    *   Calm, measured pace of speech.
    *   Using visual supports: a line for reading, cards with algorithms.

*   **Individual route: potential adaptations.**
    *   Providing additional time for testing.
    *   Permission to use printed copies of tasks from the board.
    *   Minimization of tasks focused on speed.

### 4. Development of skills

Effective games and exercises to strengthen attention and self -control in a playful way.

*   **For attention and accuracy:**
    *   **"Corrective test."** On the text page, reproach certain letters (for example, all “A” or to circle “O”). Start with 3-5 minutes.
    *   **"Find 10 differences."** Classic exercise for training visual attention to details.
    *   **Graphic dictations.** Drawing on cells for dictation (for example, "one cage to the right, two up").

*   **For memory and structuring:**
    *   **The game "Memory."** Search for paired cards. Complication: pronouncing images on open cards.
    *   **"A chain of words."** Completing and memorizing chains of 5-7 words related in history.

*   **For rhythm and bodily control:**
    *   **Rhythm tap.** Repetition of simple and complex rhythmic patterns (cotton, on the table).
    *   **Balance exercises.** Holding equilibrium (standing on one leg), walking along the line. Develops the connection between the brain and the body, strengthening self -control.

**Assessment of cognitive abilities**
**Memory. Control. Attention. (10–13 years)**

This report offers a deep understanding of individual cognitive features. The goal is to reveal the strengths and designate zones for development, creating an optimal environment for training.

### **1. Analysis by tests**

*   **Visual working memory:** The student confidently remembers visual information. The sequence of elements sometimes requires additional attention, which indicates an active search for data organization strategies.
*   **Impulse control (Go/No-Go):** The highest level of self -control. The ability to effectively suppress impulsive reactions and optimal coordination of interhemispheric relationships.
*   **Attention:** Attention is focused and effective on short, stimulating tasks. With prolonged or monotonous work, fatigue and a decrease in concentration are observed.

### **2. The guide for the success of your student**

These are key features of thinking and perception.

**Individual features:**

*   **Pace:** Variant. High productivity on fascinating tasks, slowdown in monotonous or irrelevant work.
*   **Perception:** Visual-structural format. Effective assimilation through schemes, plans, lists. Solid text and long oral instructions are less effective.
*   **Focus:** Selective. Deep concentration in an active mental process. Passive perception (for example, lectures) quickly exhausts the resource of attention.

**Which helps:**

*   Skid -controlled stages (15–25 minutes).
*   Visual support: schemes, plans, checklists.
*   Step -by -step instructions for structuring.
*   Timely feedback for motivation.
*   Active involvement: discussions, projects, practical tasks.

**What interferes:**

*   Simultaneous implementation of several tasks.
*   Multiple oral teams (more than three).
*   Long -term tasks or lectures.
*   Incomplete instructions (for example, “be more attentive”).

### **3. Recommendations**

**For home:**

*   **Homework organization:**
    *   **Principle 20/5:** 20–25 minutes of work, 5 minutes of active break. Use the timer.
    *   Start with the most difficult tasks while the concentration is maximum.
    *   Joint planning: Create a visual plan for completing tasks, dividing them into stages. This increases control and structuredness.
*   **Support and motivation:**
    *   Focus on the process, and not just on the result. Mark efforts: concentration, following the plan, application of strategies.
    *   Avoid direct criticism distracting. Delicately redirect attention by offering short breaks.

**For school:**

*   **The wording of tasks:**
    *   Use specific verbs of action and a step -by -step structure of tasks. Example: “1. Read. 2. Write out. 3. Make a diagram. "
    *   Offer tasks with a measurable result: projects, laboratory work, presentations, cases.
*   **Organization of the educational process:**
    *   Location in the class: closer to the teacher and the board, far from distracting factors (window, door).
    *   Active visualization: use a board for plans, terms, schemes. This supports attention and memory.
    *   Individual route: offer a choice of work format (for example, infographics instead of an essay). Involve the design activity for the development of self -regulation and solving problems.

### **4. Recommended training**

Exercises and games for the development of cognitive functions in interactive form.

*   **For attention and self -control:**
    *   Strategic Games: Chess, GO, Jackal. Develop planning and control.
    *   “Find differences”: improves concentration on the details.
*   **For consistent memory and structuring:**
    *   Redections, comics: retelling of events or plots through the sequence of images.
    *   Games for the sequence: “snowball”, repetition of dance movements.
    *   Preparation according to recipes: training of step -by -step instructions.
*   **For bodily control and rhythm:**
    *   Juggling: develops coordination, attention, interhemispheric ties.
    *   Balance exercises: balance board, walking on narrow surfaces, stand on one leg.
    *   Musical instruments, dancing: develop rhythm and consistent memory.

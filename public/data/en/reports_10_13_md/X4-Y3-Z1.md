# Cognitive report
## Disclosure of potential

This report provides key data on the cognitive features of your student. The goal is to identify the strengths and determine the directions for growth, creating optimal conditions for learning and development.

# 1. Evaluation results
## Key conclusions on tests

*   **Attention and working memory (CPT):**
    Tasks that require the simultaneous retention of many parts and their implementation cause difficulties. Long or complex tasks can lead to a decrease in concentration and loss of sequence.
*   **Self-control and braking (Go/No-Go):**
    Movement control is more effective with the leading hand. Switching to another hand or the need for a quick reaction and deterrence can lead to errors, pointing to the possibilities to improve intermollash coordination.
*   **Visual working memory:**
    High accuracy and concentration appear when working with simple, visual and clearly structured visual tasks. This is a confirmed strong side that promotes training.

# 2. The profile of the student
## Understanding the needs for training

### Features of perception
*   **Pace:** A moderate, uniform pace is preferred. Hurry often leads to chaotic actions and errors. The ability to work in your own rhythm to think about each step is important.
*   **Perception:** Optimally perceives information presented clearly and step by step. Visual schemes, plans and brief written instructions are more effective than long oral explanations.
*   **Focus of attention:** The ability to concentrate is effective in performing one simple action. Attention becomes scattered when the task requires simultaneous perception by ear, memorization and performing actions.

### Efficiency support
*   **Step -by -step instructions:** Separate large tasks into 2-3 small, clear steps. For example: instead of “solving the problem” - “1. Read the condition, highlight the main thing. 2. Record briefly. 3. Choose action. "
*   **Visual supports:** Apply stickers, checklists, highlight key information with color. A written plan of action is the most effective tool.
*   **Rhythm and predictability:** A clear daily routine and a structured organization of classes reduce anxiety, contributing to the optimal tuning of the brain to work.
*   **Physical discharge:** Short breaks with moderate physical activity (stretching, squats) contribute to the restoration of concentration.

### Factors that reduce productivity
*   **Multitasking:** The simultaneous implementation of several tasks (listening, recording, analyzing) is extremely difficult. At each point in time, the focus of attention can only be aimed at one action.
*   **Long oral instructions:** Large volumes of information transmitted by ear quickly overloaded working memory. The child is inclined to remember only the beginning or end of the message, missing the main part.
*   **Hurry and background noise:** Time restriction and distracting factors (conversations, external sounds) significantly reduce productivity and can provoke errors.

# 3. Practical recommendations
## Creating an optimal environment

### Home environment
*   **Homework organization:**
    *   Divide the tasks into blocks for 15–20 minutes. Turn on short (3-5 minutes) active breaks.
    *   Use a timer to visualize the time of work and rest. This contributes to the development of self -control.
    *   Make a simple written plan before starting work: “1. Mathematics (No. 1, 2). 2. Break. 3. Russian (ex. 5). " The mark on execution gives a feeling of completeness and satisfaction.
*   **Support and encouragement:**
    *   Encourage the process and efforts, not just the result. Examples: “You have focused on these 15 minutes!”, “A wonderful plan for the task!”
    *   Allow mistakes. They are an opportunity to search for new, more effective solutions.
*   **Duration of attention:** Effective concentration on a complex mental task remains up to 15–20 minutes. Further, productivity is reduced, requiring a short rest.

### School environment
*   **The wording of tasks:**
    *   The instructions should be brief, clockwork and step -by -step. Duplicate oral tasks with a written entry on the board or card.
    *   Place the student so as to minimize distracting factors (for example, closer to the teacher, away from windows or doors).
*   **Material feed structure:**
    *   Offer visual algorithms to complete complex tasks (for example, memos for solving equations).
    *   Apply the model: “Explanation → Demonstration → Joint Work → Independent Practice”.
*   **Individual approach:**
    *   Provide additional time to perform control and independent work.
    *   Allow the use of drafts or lines to maintain concentration when reading or writing.
    *   In written works, the emphasis is shifted to accuracy and correctness, and not by speed. The requirements for the pace can be reduced.

# 4. Developing training
## Strengthening cognitive skills

These exercises and games are aimed at developing cognitive skills in interactive form.

### Attention and working memory
*   **Correct samples:** Use any text. The task is to cross certain letters (for example, all “a”) and circle others (for example, all “o”). Start with one paragraph.
*   **Remember and name:** Show 4-5 items, then cover them. Ask to name them from memory. Gradually increase the number of objects.

### Coordination and interhemispheric interaction
*   **Mirror drawing:** Simultaneous drawing of symmetrical figures (circles, squares, waves) with both hands in the air or on paper.
*   **Kinesiological exercises:** Exercise “Ring” is a alternate connection of the thumb with the rest of the fingers (index, middle, etc.) first with one hand, then with the other, then both at the same time.

### Bodily control and rhythm
*   **Rhythmic dictation:** Cut or fit a simple rhythm (for example: Ta-Ta-Ta). The task of the student is to repeat it exactly.
*   **Games with the ball:** Throwing the ball out of the hand to the hand, beat off the floor or wall with alternating hands. Develops the coordination of the "eye-hand" and a sense of rhythm.

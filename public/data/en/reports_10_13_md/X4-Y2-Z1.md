# Cognitive development report
### Analysis: CPT+Go/No-Go+visual memory (age 10–13 years)

This report is intended for parents and teachers. It provides an overview of the strengths and areas of the growth of your student, offering recommendations for more effective and comfortable learning.

# 1. Test results

Assessment of cognitive functions:

*   **Attention and working memory (CPT):** Difficulties with the retention of many details or consistent steps without a clear strategy.
*   **Impulsive control (Go/No-Go):** Tendency to quick, rash reactions; Complexity with timely stop of action.
*   **Visual working memory:** Quick forgetting visual information, especially with a large volume or high feed speed.

# 2. Optimization of training

Understanding how the schoolboy assimilates the information helps to create the best conditions for its development.

### Key features

*   **Pace:** Impulsive, fast. It can act or respond until completely comprehending or completion of instructions.
*   **Perception:** Effective mostly assimilates information presented visually, briefly and consistently. Long monologues or texts overload working memory.
*   **Attention:** Unstable. Significant efforts are required for concentration, easily distracted by external stimuli.

### Optimal conditions

*   **Visual support:** Schemes, reference pictures, highlighting key points, simple checklists.
*   **Step -by -step instructions:** The principle of "one step at a time." The next action is only after the end of the previous one.
*   **Rhythm and structure:** Clear schedule, predictability. Rhythmic shift exercises.
*   **Pause for understanding:** Time for thinking before the answer or the beginning of action.
*   **Physical activity:** Short breaks to relieve voltage.

### Factors that reduce efficiency

*   **Multitasking:** Simultaneous requests to memory and actions lead to errors.
*   **Sharp change in activity:** The transition without preparation and pauses causes fatigue and disorientation.
*   **Sensory overload:** Excess noise, visual disorder, a large number of people exhaust attention.
*   **Verbal overload:** Long oral explanations without a visual support.

# 3. Recommendations

### Home environment

*   **Organization of work:**
    *   Break the tasks into blocks for 10-15 minutes with breaks of 3-5 minutes (water, short exercise).
    *   Use visual timers (sand clock, phone timer) for clarity.
    *   Make a simple, visual plan for the evening: Mathematics (15 min) → Rest (5 min) → Reading (10 min).
*   **Support and motivation:**
    *   Praise effort and process, and not just the result: "I see how you tried to concentrate!" "Great that I first checked the work."
    *   Instead of criticism of errors, offer a search for a solution: "What do you think, why did it happen? Let's fix it."
*   **Self -control development:**
    *   Before starting the assignment, ask: "What is our plan? What do we do first, and then what?" This forms strategic thinking.

### School environment

*   **Instructions:**
    *   Give the instructions in stages. For example, instead of one long phrase, break into short teams with pauses: "They took textbooks. They opened page 20. Found exercise 5. They prepared notebooks. They began to perform."
    *   Use simple, unequivocal verbs: “Draw”, “Clutch”, “Cross out”.
*   **Lesson structure:**
    *   Provide algorithms or checklists for complex tasks.
    *   Select key information on the board with color or frame.
    *   It is recommended that landing in the first desk in the center to minimize abstracts and better contact with the teacher.
*   **Material and IOOM:**
    *   Give the time to comprehend. Before the question, pause: "Now the question. Think. Raise your hand, who is ready."
    *   Turn on short kinesiological exercises or physical education, especially before complex tasks.
    *   Elements of an individual route, such as a personal-algorithm or the use of a visual timer in independent work, will be effective.

# 4. Recommended training

These exercises and games contribute to the development of key skills in interactive form.

### Attention and working memory

*   **The game "Memory" ("Find a couple"):** Start with 4-6 pairs, gradually increasing the number of cards.
*   **"Corrective test":** In the printed text, ask to cross out everything “A” or circle all “O”.
*   **"Photographer":** The child looks at 3-4 items 5 seconds, then turns away and calls them.

### Coordination, rhythm and body control

*   **The game is "edible, unauthorized":** Develops the speed of reaction and the ability to inhibit the action.
*   **Tapping rhythms:** Repetition of the rhythm you specified (for example, tuk-tuk-tuk).
*   **The game "Sea is worried once":** He trains the ability to freeze by signal and bodily control.
*   **Exercises on a balancing board/pillow:** Improve coordination and awareness of the body.

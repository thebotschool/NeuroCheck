# Report on the cognitive profile of the student
Age: 10-13 years

This report is designed to help parents and teachers identify the strengths and development zones of the student. The recommendations presented are aimed at creating an optimal environment for training.

## 1. Key indicators

### Conservative memory test (CPT)
The student remembers specific information, but experiences difficulties with holding it in the correct sequence. He actively applies strategies to compensate for this aspect.

### Braking and control test (Go/No-Go)
There is a decrease in the concentration and control of impulsive reactions, especially with fatigue or performing complex tasks. This indicates rapid fatigue and insufficient cognitive endurance.

### Attention test (TCP)
Attention is easily distracted. Monotonous tasks and external incentives significantly reduce the ability to concentrate.

## 2. Learning Guide

This is a brief instruction on effective interaction with the student in the educational process, based on his cognitive features.

### Features of perception

*   **Pace:** Optimal pace with regular pauses is optimal. Fast supply of material causes overload, not acceleration of work.
*   **Perception:** Preferably assimilates information presented visually and structurally. Long oral explanations are less effective.
*   **Focus:** Attention is short and unstable. External support is required to keep on the task. Monotony quickly exhausts the resource of attention.

### Effective approaches

*   **Visual supports:** Schemes, intelligence cards, algorithms, supporting notes. All that can be seen.
*   **Step -by -step instructions:** The division of large tasks into 2-3 clear, small steps.
*   **Short intervals of work:** Using a timer for work (for example, 15–20 minutes) with short breaks.
*   **Clarity and predictability:** Clear, short formulations of tasks and an understandable plan of the lesson or homework.
*   **Work in pairs:** Interaction with a partner can maintain attention and overall pace.

### Factors that reduce productivity

*   **Multitasking:** The requirement to do several things at the same time.
*   **Long oral instructions:** More than two consistent teams are probably not remembered.
*   **Monotonous work:** Long -term execution of the same type of exercise quickly depleted the resource of attention.
*   **Background interference:** Extra items on the table or loud conversations significantly reduce productivity.

## 3. Practical recommendations

### For the house

*   **Homework organization:**
    *   Separate the tasks: instead of a general indication, crush at small stages with breaks.
    *   Use the timer: interval work (15–20 minutes of focus, 5 minutes break).
    *   Plan: together make a short plan of 2-3 points, mark the completed.
*   **Support and motivation:**
    *   Praise effort: focus on the process, and not just on the result.
    *   Teach the right rest: during the break it is better to do physical exercises or look out the window, avoiding gadgets.
*   **Mercing of attention:**
    *   Evaluate realistically: a student can productively hold attention to the educational task of about **15–20 minutes**. The requirement of more provokes errors and fatigue.

### For school

*   **Making tasks:**
    *   Let's briefly, step -by -step (no more than 2 steps at a time). Duplicate oral tasks in writing (on the board, card).
    *   Use visual markers: emphasize keywords, highlight important parts of the rule.
*   **Organization of space:**
    *   The optimal place: the first-second desk in the central row, where distracting factors are minimized and there is direct visual contact with the teacher.
    *   Active involvement: more often attract to interactive activity (work at the board, response from a place, pair work) for “rebooting” attention.
*   **Individual approach:**
    *   A temporary decrease in the volume of the same type of tasks is recommended.
    *   Providing the opportunity to use support schemes or algorithms when performing tasks.
    *   Developing support is necessary with an emphasis on the formation of regulatory functions (planning, self -control, evaluation of the result).

## 4. Developing exercises

The following exercises are integrated into everyday life, supporting the development of key cognitive functions.

### Attention and self -control

*   **Correct test:** Offer to cross out all the letters “A” on the page of the old magazine in 5 minutes.
*   **Board games:** “Uno”, “Dobbl”, “Drum” - require a quick reaction, following the rules and inhibition.
*   **The game "Delay!":** During the active game (run, dance) on the command "Frozen!" It is necessary to freeze instantly.

### Consistent memory and rhythm

*   **"Repeat me":** Back or cool the simple rhythm and ask the student to repeat it for sure. Gradually complicate.
*   **Retelling of the day:** In the evening, ask to tell what he did during the day, focusing on the chronology: “What was *at first*? A *what later*? ".
*   **Graphic dictations:** Drawing on cells under dictation (“Two cells to the right, one upward ...”) trains auditory attention and sequence of actions.

### Coordination and bodily control

*   **"Crossroads":** Ask the student to touch the left knee in turn and with the left hand - the right. This contributes to the establishment of the connection between the hemispheres of the brain.
*   **Games with the ball:** Bowing the ball from the floor or wall in a given rhythm.
*   **Balance:** Walking along the curb, stand on one leg.

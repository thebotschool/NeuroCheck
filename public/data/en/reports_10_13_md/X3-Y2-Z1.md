# Cognitive assessment report
### Pupil profile (10-13 years)

This report is prepared to help parents and teachers better understand the unique abilities and potential of the student. Recommendations are based on the results of cognitive tests and are designed to create optimal conditions for successful training and development.

<!-- page-break -->

## 1. A brief report for each test

*   **Test for attention (CPT):** The student demonstrates an excellent amount of memory, easily assimilating information. However, structuring and maintaining the data sequence require additional practice.
*   **Self-control test (Go/No-Go):** The developing ability to self -regulation is noted. It can be difficult for a student to “brake” impulsive actions, especially with enthusiasm or hurry.
*   **Test for stability of attention (TCP):** Sustainable and deep attention to one task is the strength of the student. This ability is manifested when the task is understandable, interesting and does not require frequent switching.

## 2. The guide for the success of your student

This leadership will help to understand how the student perceives educational tasks and what contributes to his success.

### Individual characteristics

*   **Pace:** Shows a tendency to a quick, impulsive pace. This can lead to errors due to inattention, not ignorance. It needs external signals to think about actions.
*   **Perception:** Most effectively assimilates the information presented visually and step by step. Abstract or long oral instructions can be less effective.
*   **Focus of attention:** Capable of deep immersion in the same task. However, the transition between different activities or multi -step planning require development.

### Support: What helps

*   **Visual supports:** Check lists, visual schemes, color selection of key points in tasks.
*   **Clear algorithm:** The rule "Stop-think-make." A small pause before the answer or the start of the task.
*   **Predictable environment:** Knowing the upcoming sequence of actions reduces anxiety and increases concentration.
*   **Rhythm:** The use of rhythmic cotton, account or short rhymes to remember sequences.
*   **Focusing:** One task at one point in time. Full completion of the action before the transition to the next.

### Obstacles: what is interfering

*   **Multitasking:** Simultaneous requests (listen and record, search in a textbook).
*   **Long oral instructions:** Tasks containing three or more consistent commands.
*   **Sharp change in activity:** A quick transition between tasks without a short pause for "rebooting".
*   **Rush and pressure:** Phrases like "Come on faster!" They can increase impulsiveness and number of errors.
*   **Unstracting noise:** Distracting background sounds.

## 3. Recommendations

### At home

*   **Homework organization:**
    *   **Crushing of tasks:** Separate large tasks into small stages. For example, "first we will solve only the first column of examples, then we will rest for 5 minutes."
    *   **Regular breaks:** The productive concentration time on a difficult task is 15–20 minutes. After that, a short break is required (water, stretching).
    *   **Visual timer:** Use a sand clock or a timer for visual control of working hours.
*   **Support and encouragement:**
    *   **Focus on diligence:** Praise for attentiveness and self -control, and not just for speed. Example: “It's great that you first checked, and then handed over a job!”
    *   **Planning:** Before starting work, pronounce the sequence of actions: “What will you do first? And then what? ”
*   **Household tasks:**
    *   **Check lists:** Place in a prominent place simple lists of morning and evening affairs. It trains the sequence.

### At school

*   **The wording of tasks:**
    *   **Clear instructions:** Let’s the instructions briefly, clearly, on one team. Check the understanding: "Repeat what needs to be done now?"
    *   **Visualization:** Duplicate the tasks on the board or card, highlighting the key steps (1. Read. 2. Emphasize. 3. Resolve).
*   **Lesson structure:**
    *   **Action plan:** When performing multi -stage tasks (solving a complex problem, writing a presentation), provide a ready -made plan or template.
    *   **Separation of steps:** In control and independent work, share the steps of the assignment with numbering.
*   **Class placement:**
    *   **Optimal place:** The first-second desk, in the center, far from windows and doors to minimize distraction.
*   **Individual route:**
    *   **Helping cards:** Provide cards with algorithms of action (for example, a word analysis plan).
    *   **Signal for the answer:** Agree on a special non -verbal signal for situations when you need to hold back the pulse (for example, a nod of a teacher allowing to answer).

## 4. Recommended training

Simple game exercises for the development of the necessary skills in everyday life.

### For attention and self -control

*   **The game "Delay!":** A quick reaction to the signal and stopping the movement.
*   **Graphic dictations:** Following the instructions (“one cage to the right, two cells up ...”), auditory attention and self -control are trained.
*   **The game is "edible-universal":** Develops the speed of reaction and the ability to restrain the wrong answer.

### For memory and sequence

*   **The game "What is first, then what?":** Restoration of about 3-5 cards with a simple plot.
*   **"Repeat the pattern":** Repetition of a sequence of 3-4 colored cubes or beads from memory with a gradual complication.
*   **"I put in a bag ...":** Classical game of consistent memorization of objects.

### For rhythm and bodily control

*   **RIGHTS RIGHTS:** Repetition of simple rhythms torn off with a palm helps to establish an internal pace.
*   **Games with the ball:** The throwing of the ball with the performance of the action before fishing (for example, cotton) develops coordination and planning.
*   **Walking along the line:** He teaches to control the body, maintain balance and focus on movement.

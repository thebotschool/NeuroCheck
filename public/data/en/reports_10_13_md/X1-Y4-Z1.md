## Cognitive profile: Assessment and support

This report was created for parents and teachers. Its goal is to identify the strengths of the student and determine the zones for development. The proposed recommendations will help create a supporting environment to disclose its potential.

---

## 1. Test results

### Visual memory

Great ability to remember and withhold complex visual information. The student demonstrates effective data assimilation strategies.

### Impuls control

Difficulties with timely stop and prevention of impulsive actions are noted. This is manifested especially with fatigue or the need to adapt to new rules.

### Sustainability of attention

High and high -quality concentration on one task. The student demonstrates self -control in predictable conditions.

### Key conclusion

Problems are not associated with concentration, but with the function of "braking". The brain is effectively included in the work, but experiences challenges with self -regulation, double -checking or fast change of tactics.

## 2. Profile of a student

### Features of cognition

*   **Pace:** Able to work at a high pace on one task. However, the transition or the need to stop can cause slowdown and error. Included to act until interpretation.
*   **Perception:** Optimally assimilates structured, logical and visual information. Schemes, algorithms, step-by-step instructions and checklists are supported by excellent memory.
*   **Focus:** Attention is characterized as "tunnel". The student is deeply immersed in interesting tasks. The main difficulty is not holding attention, but an interruption of an impulsive answer.

### Efficiency support

*   **Step -by -step instructions:** Separation of large tasks into small, understandable stages.
*   **Visual supports:** Check lists, timers, highlighting keywords. The timer is used to structure time, not for acceleration.
*   **The rule "Stop-think-make":** Direct training of micropauze before action or answer.
*   **Clear "stop-signals":** Clear criteria for completing the problem and the following steps.
*   **Preliminary planning:** Joint discussion of the action plan before starting work.

### Calls in training

*   **Multitasking:** The simultaneous performance of several tasks or quick switching often leads to errors and frustration.
*   **Long oral instructions:** The information flow, transmitted only by ear, overloads the control system.
*   **Fast change of activity:** A sharp transition between objects without a short pause.
*   **Lack of pauses:** Continuous work depletes cognitive endurance, enhancing impulsiveness.
*   **Uncertainty:** The vague formulations ("be more attentive") are ineffective. Specifics are required.

## 3. Recommendations to support

### Home environment

*   **Performing tasks:**
    *   Divide homework into blocks for 20–25 minutes. Turn on 5-minute breaks (Methods).
    *   Create a simple written plan: "1. Mathematics (3 tasks), 2. Break, 3. English (learn words)."
    *   Focus on one subject at a time. Remove everything that relates to other lessons from the table.
*   **Promotion:**
    *   Praise not for speed, but for accuracy and following the plan. Mark successful moments of self -test: "It is great that you noticed a mistake and corrected!"
    *   Instead of "did you do lessons?" Ask: "What are you under plan today? Let's see together."
*   **Mercing of attention:** Remember that deep immersion in one thing (for example, the collection of a model) can last hours. At the same time, tasks requiring constant self -control (for example, solution of examples with verification) can quickly cause fatigue. This is fine.

### School environment

*   **The wording of tasks:**
    *   Provide tasks one at a time, not a list.
    *   In control work, highlight keywords: *"NO", "except"*, or *"Find the perimeter, not the area"*.
*   **Structure and feed:**
    *   Before starting a lesson or a new topic, place a short plan on the board: "Today we: 1. Repeat ... 2. We study ... 3. We train ..."
    *   Combine oral instructions with mandatory visual support (recording on the board, printouts).
    *   Place a student where there are fewer distracting factors (for example, closer to the teacher, further from the window).
*   **IOM elements:**
    *   In control work, provide extra time specifically to verify and correct impulsive errors.
    *   Use sheets with exercises where the tasks are visually separated by blocks to prevent "jumping".

## 4. A set of exercises

The purpose of these exercises is not a correction of attention, but the development of self -control skills and "braking". This will turn potential weakness into a strong, consciously used tool.

### The development of self -control

*   **Game "Delay":** Effectively trains the ability to instantly stop motor activity by signal.
*   **Graphic dictations with the Stop teams:** For example: "Two cells to the right, one up, stop, one cage to the left." The Stop team forms a pause skill.
*   **Board games to reaction with deterrence elements:** "Dobbl" or "drum", where sometimes you need to name an object, and not grab it.

### The use of strengths

*   **Complex "memories":** Games to search for steam with an increased number of cards or additional conditions (for example, find three identical ones).
*   **Strategic board games:** "Chess", "GO", "checkers". These games require planning and suppressing impulsive desire to make a move immediately.

### Brain synchronization

*   **RIGHTS RIGHTS:** The parent or teacher taps the rhythm, the student exactly repeats him after a short pause. Gradually, rhythms become more complicated.
*   **Juggling:** Even with two goals, it perfectly develops coordination, a sense of time and the ability to concentrate on several simultaneous processes.

### Physical self -regulation

*   **"Square breath":** Simple technique for reducing impulsiveness. 4 seconds inhale - 4 delay - 4 exhale - 4 delay. Practiles before the start of a complex task.

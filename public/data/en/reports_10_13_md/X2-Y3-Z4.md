### Cognitive profile of a student: Age 10–13

This report is an analysis of the results of cognitive tests. It is designed to determine the strengths and areas for development, as well as to provide practical recommendations for creating an optimal environment for learning and growth of a student.

### 1. Test results

#### Visual memory
Fixation of visual information occurs. However, when distracting or the need for accurate reproduction of parts, difficulties are possible. The memory operates, but an increase in concentration in accurate images is required.

#### Self -control
Asymmetry is observed in the performance of tasks requiring coordination of both hands. This can affect the concentration and performance of actions where the coherence of movements is important, for example, when writing.

#### Sustainability of attention
Difficulties are noted in long -term attention, control of impulsiveness and quick switching between tasks. Focusing is required extra time and external support.

### 2. Understanding the stylistics of learning

#### Individual profile

*   **The pace of work**
    Uneven. The beginning can be impulsive, then fatigue occurs, especially with tasks that require accuracy. Maintaining stable speed is difficult.

*   **Perception of information**
    The most effective through simple and clear visual images. Complex schemes or volumetric texts cause difficulties. The rhythmic organization of activities contributes to assimilation.

*   **Focus of attention**
    Unstable. It is difficult to filter out external stimuli (noise, movement) and internal impulses. The ability to concentrate on one task is up to 15–20 minutes.

#### What supports training

*   **Step -by -step instructions:** Miss tasks into 2-3 small, specific steps. Let's the next instruction after the previous one.
*   **Visual supports:** Use checklists, diagrams, timers, color release. The plan of a day or lesson creates predictability.
*   **Rhythm and rituals:** A clear rhythm (score, claps) and predictable rituals (for example, preparation of the workplace) help to tune in.
*   **Two -handed work:** The activity that involves both hands (drawing, modeling, games with the ball) contributes to the balance of brain activity.

#### What complicates training

*   **Multitasking:** The execution of several requests is also difficult. One task at a time.
*   **Noise and chaos:** Distracting factors (extra objects, loud sounds) significantly reduce productivity.
*   **Sharp change of tasks:** The transition without a pause and warning causes disorientation. It takes time for mental switching.
*   **Long oral instructions:** More than one sentence is lost at a time. Information is perceived fragmented.

### 3. Recommendations

#### Home environment

*   **Homework structure**
    *   Break work into blocks for 15–20 minutes. Provide 5-minute breaks for physical activity.
    *   Use a visual timer for a visual reference of time.
    *   Start with the most difficult tasks while the energy level is high.
    *   Make a simple written plan of work (for example, "1. Mathematics: Task 5"). Cross out the executed points.

*   **Support and promotion**
    *   Mark not only the result, but also the efforts made: "I see how you tried to concentrate," "excellent that you corrected your mistake."
    *   Provide a calm and predictable workplace free from extra items.

*   **Focus of attention**
    Remember, arbitrary attention on the uninteresting task is held for up to 15 minutes. Avoid the requirements of a greater continuous concentration.

#### School environment

*   **The formulation of tasks**
    *   Let's briefly, clearly, one at a time. For example: "Open the notebook. Find number 3. Decide the first example."
    *   Use the verbs of the action: “Close”, “Emphasize”, “Connect”.

*   **Organization of the lesson**
    *   At the beginning of the lesson, voice and write a short plan on the board. This reduces anxiety and helps the student navigate.
    *   Place the child in the center of the class, closer to the board to minimize distracting factors.

*   **Material feed**
    *   Use visual materials: schemes, cards, presentations with a minimum of text and an image maximum.
    *   Turn on rhythmic elements: pronouncing the rules in chorus, taping the rhythm.

*   **Individual approach**
    *   Adaptation of the volume of written tasks (for example, partial execution) is recommended.
    *   Give additional time for control and independent work.
    *   Allow the use of support cards with rules or algorithms.

### 4. Developing exercises

These exercises are easy to integrate into everyday life. They are aimed at strengthening the necessary cognitive functions.

#### For attention and control

*   **Correct samples:** Use print text. Ask for 1 minute to cross out a certain letter (for example, “a”) or circle another (“o”).
*   **The game "Delay-Otomory":** Turn on the music. The child is dancing. When stopping music, he must instantly freeze. This is trained by impulse control.
*   **The board game "Memory":** Develops visual memory and concentration.

#### For coordination and interhemispheric interaction

*   **Drawing with two hands:** Give the child a pencil in each hand. Ask to simultaneously draw symmetrical figures (circles, squares).
*   **Graphic dictations:** Drawing on cells for dictation (for example, “one cage to the right, two cells up”).
*   **Games with the ball:** Renting the ball between the hands, alternately beaten off the floor with the right and left hand.

#### For memory and rhythm

*   **The game "What has changed?"**: Lay out 5-7 items. Ask the child to remember them. When it is turned away, remove or change the position of one object. The task is to determine the change.
*   **Rhythmic pops:** Clap your hands with a simple rhythm. Ask the child to repeat. Gradually complicate the rhythms.

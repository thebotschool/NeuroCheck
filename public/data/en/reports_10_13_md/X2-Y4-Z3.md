# Cognitive assessment report

### Analysis of abilities: CPT + Go/No-GO + TCP (10–13 years)

This report provides key conclusions about the cognitive development of your child. It presents test results and practical recommendations for the use of home and at school.

## 1. Test Report

### Key results

*   **Visual memory (CPT).** The ability to memorize is present, but the details can be inaccurate, especially when distracting. A child can confuse or replace images.

*   **Braking and control (Go/No-Go).** There is a difficulty in containing impulsive reactions. Attention is depleted quickly, especially with tasks requiring high concentration.

*   **Self -control (TCP).** The tendency to hasty actions has been revealed. The child can answer without listening to the question, or interrupt, experiencing difficulties with a pause before the reaction.

## 2. The guide for the success of your student

### Understanding individual characteristics

*   **Pace.** Prone to fast, impulsive work. It can slow down and show uncertainty during tasks requiring high accuracy.
*   **Perception.** The most effectively absorbs information with simultaneous presentation in several formats (for example, audio and visual). Long oral instructions without visual support are perceived poorly.
*   **Attention.** Unstable, quickly exhausted. It is difficult to concentrate on monotonous tasks or those that require restraining the first reaction. The optimal focus is 15–20 minutes, then a short break is needed.

### Support strategies

*   **Visual supports.** Stickers, schemes, intelligence cards, checklists help to fix information and hold the details.
*   **Step -by -step instructions.** Missing tasks into small, understandable steps increases success. One instruction is preferred at a time.
*   **A clear rhythm.** Short working intervals (for example, 15 minutes) with mandatory breaks. Using the timer reduces anxiety and helps to track time.
*   **Praise for effort.** Focus on the process of work, and not just on the result. Conforate thoughtfulness and diligence.
*   **"Stop signals."** Simple rules or gestures (for example, “count up to three before the answer”) train a pause before the reaction.

### Factors that interfere

*   **Multitasking.** Switching between several tasks leads to errors and fatigue.
*   **Long oral instructions.** Many teams in one phrase make it difficult to memorize all the information.
*   **Speed ​​competitions.** They provoke a rush, impulsiveness and increase the number of errors.
*   **Distracting factors.** Noise, conversations, visual disorder significantly reduce concentration.

## 3. Recommendations

### For the house

#### Organization of homework

*   Miss homework into short blocks (15–20 minutes). Take short breaks (2-5 minutes) for physical activity between blocks.
*   Use a timer to visualize the remaining time.
*   Before starting work, make sure that the child understood the task. Ask him to retell the instructions.
*   Provide the workplace free from distracting items.

#### Support and promotion

*   Mark the effort, not just the result. For example: “You tried not to be distracted” or “Well done that you checked the work.”
*   When memorizing, help find individual strategies: invent stories, draw schemes.
*   With errors on inattention, offer a calm joint analysis, not criticism.

### For school

#### Structure and formulation of tasks

*   Formulate the tasks as briefly and clearly as possible. Give only one instruction at a time.
*   Duplicate oral instructions in writing on a board or individual cards.
*   Provide visual supports: plans, checks, algorithms for solving problems.

#### Organization in the classroom

*   Place the child closer to the teacher, away from distracting factors (window, door).
*   Before important tasks, make sure that the child completely understood the requirements.
*   Use pairing, where a more calm student can serve as an example.

#### Individual educational route (IOM)

*   With significant difficulties, consider the possibility of creating an IOO together with the administration and a school psychologist.
*   IOM can include additional time for control, delivery of tasks in parts, as well as regular classes with a specialist in the development of attention.

## 4. Recommended training

Regular short classes (10-15 minutes) with these games and exercises will help to develop the necessary cognitive functions.

### Development of attention

*   **Corrective tests.** Invite the child to cross out certain letters on the text page (for example, all “a”). Gradually complicate the task by adding new rules (for example, circle “O”).
*   **Board games.** Games like “Find 10 differences”, “Drum”, “Dobble” develop the speed of reaction and attentiveness.

### Development of memory and strategies

*   **The game "Memory".** A classic game to improve visual memory.
*   **"What has changed?"** Lay out several items. Give your child the time to remember them, then ask to turn away and change something (remove, add, change places). The child must determine the changes.

### The development of bodily control

*   **The game "Sea is worried once."** Effectively trains the ability to instantly stop and freeze by signal.
*   **Balance exercises.** Walking along the line, stand on one leg. The development of bodily control is directly related to impulse management.

### Development of rhythm and self -regulation

*   **"Echo".** Time or chop the rhythm, which the child should repeat exactly. Gradually complicate the rhythmic pattern.
*   **Reading verses with rhythm.** Read the poems aloud, pulling the rhythm with your hand or foot. This contributes to synchronization of speech and movement, improving self -control.

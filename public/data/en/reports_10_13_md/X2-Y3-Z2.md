### Cognitive assessment report

This report offers a detailed view of the strengths and development of your student. It contains practical tools to support its progress both at home and in the educational environment.

## 1. A brief report for each test

*   **Visual memory test (CPT):** Memorization and reproduction of information can be difficult, especially when distracting. The development of strategic methods of memorization, not mechanical memorization, is recommended.
*   **Self-control test (Go/No-Go):** There is insufficient coordination between the hemispheres of the brain. This can be manifested in difficulties with writing and performing several tasks at the same time.
*   **Test for stability of attention (TCP):** Maintaining concentration on monotonous or monotonous work causes difficulties. Attention quickly switches to more exciting incentives.

## 2. The guide for the success of your student

This guide is developed for parents and teachers. It will help to find the optimal approach to learning and interaction with the child.

### Individual characteristics

*   **Pace:** The optimal work is achieved in short, intense intervals. New or complex tasks can slow down the pace, demanding more time for concentration.
*   **Perception:** The best assimilation of information occurs through visual, visual materials: schemes, pictures, short videos. Long oral explanations without visual support are more difficult.
*   **Focus of attention:** Attention is selectively and unstable. The child is easily distracted by external stimuli, hardly holds the focus on monotonous tasks. However, with sincere interest, it can demonstrate a high concentration.

### Which helps

*   **Visual supports:** Step-by-step instructions in pictures, intelligence cards, highlighting keywords with color, short video tutorials.
*   **Clear structure:** Separation of large tasks into small, understandable steps. Principle: One task is one step.
*   **Rhythm and movement:** Using a timer (for example, 20 minutes of work, 5 minutes of outdoor activities), short physical warm -ups, pronouncing or tapping the rules.
*   **The mode of one task:** On the desktop are only the necessary objects for the current task.

### What interferes

*   **Multitasking:** The simultaneous performance of several actions, for example, listening and recording, causes overload.
*   **Long, monotonous tasks:** Monotony, such as prolonged reading or solving the same examples, quickly leads to a loss of attention.
*   **Noise and disorder:** Extraneous sounds and visual noise at the workplace significantly reduce concentration.
*   **Oral instructions without support:** Long verbal explanations without visual reinforcements (on a board or card) are poorly absorbed.

## 3. Recommendations

### For the house

*   **Organization of homework:**
    *   Sessions for 15–20 minutes with mandatory moves to traffic.
    *   Before starting classes, remove everything superfluous from the table.
    *   Use checks or plan in pictures. Refusing the points executed enhances the sense of control.
*   **Support and motivation:**
    *   Praise for diligence and following the plan, and not just for the final assessment. Example: "I see how you tried to concentrate!"
    *   When distracting, gently return to the task: “Let's see what we stopped at?”
    *   Let the child choose the procedure for completing tasks (within reasonable limits) to increase motivation.

### For school

*   **The wording of tasks:**
    *   Tasks should be clumsy, short and step -by -step.
    *   Always duplicate oral instructions visually: on the board, projector or individual card.
*   **Lesson and workplace:**
    *   Place the student in the center of the class, closer to the board and the teacher, away from distracting factors (windows, doors).
    *   Divide long tasks into stages, checking the result after each step.
*   **Style style:**
    *   Use the elements of gamification: turn the tasks into “quests”, use stickers, conduct short attention for attention.
    *   Let us be an opportunity to answer in parts, without immediately requiring a complete detailed response, especially with excitement.
*   **Individual route:**
    *   With severe difficulties, consider the adaptation of the educational program, especially in terms of the pace and volume of written work.

## 4. Recommended training

These games and exercises will help develop cognitive functions in an accessible form. Regularity is important, not duration.

*   **For attention:**
    *   “Find 10 differences”: classic for training concentration and observation.
    *   “Corrective test”: to dispensing certain letters on the page (with gradual complication).
    *   Board games: “Vybl”, “drum”, “high -speed caps” - develop the speed of reaction and concentration.
*   **For visual memory:**
    *   "Memory": Search for paired cards.
    *   “What has changed?”: Remember 5-7 items, then find a change after removal or replacement of one.
    *   “Draw from memory”: reproduction of a simple figure or pattern after a 5-second viewing.
*   **For coordination, rhythm and bodily control:**
    *   **Neurohymnastics:**
        *   Cross movements: left elbow - right knee, right elbow is the left knee.
        *   “Ear Nos”: take the nose with one hand, with the other-by the opposite ear, then quickly change.
    *   **Drawing with two hands:** Simultaneous drawing of symmetrical figures (circles, butterflies) in the air or on a large sheet.
    *   **Rhythmic games:** Removing or exhausting the rhythms you specified.

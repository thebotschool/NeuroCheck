Report. Assessment of cognitive abilities
Profile: Focus of attention and sequence with developed self -control (age: 10–13 years)

This report provides key data on the cognitive features of your student. Information is based on test results and contains practical recommendations for home and training.

---

### 1. Overview of the results

*   **Attention (CPT):** The concentration on long -term, monotonous tasks is difficult. This affects the speed and accuracy of the work.
*   **Self-control (Go/No-Go):** Great ability to self -regulation. The student demonstrates a quick reaction, the ability to restrain impulses and good coordination. This indicates the maturity of the regulatory functions of the brain.
*   **Visual working memory:** Information is remembered well (for example, objects), but there may be difficulties with its sequence. This indicates difficulties with structuring and organizing the material, and not with the memory itself.

**Conclusion:** The student has high potential for self -regulation, especially in clearly certain tasks. Monotony, a large amount of unstructured information and the need for a long concentration deplete its cognitive resources.

### 2. Understanding the student

This profile does not indicate "problematic". He describes the unique style of thinking, requiring the optimization of the environment for maximum potential disclosure.

#### Individual features:

*   **Rhythm:** Work in short, clear intervals is effective. Long or monotonous tasks reduce productivity, leading to distraction and errors.
*   **Perception:** The best assimilation of information occurs through structured, visual materials. Large text blocks or oral instructions without visual support are perceived more complicated.
*   **Focus:** The concentration is point and intensive, like a "spotlight". Maintaining wide background attention in the whole environment is difficult.

#### Which helps:

*   **Clear structure:** Plan on the board, numbered steps, checklists.
*   **Visual support:** Schemes, tables, Mayind cards, highlighting keywords.
*   **Crushing of tasks:** Separation of large tasks into small, controlled stages.
*   **Predictability:** Using a timer for working intervals (for example, 15 minutes of work, 5 minutes of rest).
*   **Game format:** Tasks with clumsy rules and quick feedback.

#### What interferes:

*   **Multitasking:** Simultaneous listening, recording and thinking.
*   **Monotone:** Long lectures, the same type of exercises.
*   **Uncertainty:** Tasks without clear criteria and stages.
*   **Background noise:** Distracting factors and the need to work at an imposed pace.

### 3. Practical recommendations

#### For home:

*   **Organization of work:**
    *   **Intervals:** Perform homework with blocks for 15–20 minutes. Between them, 5-minute breaks for relaxation are required.
    *   **Planning:** Before starting work together, make a simple plan on paper. Mark the executed points.
    *   **Structure:** Instead of "did you do lessons?" Ask: "What is the plan now? What is the next step?"
*   **Support:**
    *   **Motivation:** Praise for efforts and following the strategy, and not just for the result.
    *   **Memorization strategies:** Help create associations, draw diagrams, pronounce sequences aloud.

#### For school:

*   **Instructions:**
    *   **Steck:** Provide the instructions in stages, convinced of the understanding of every step.
    *   **Visualization:** Write down the tasks on the board or card, even if they were voiced orally.
*   **Lesson structure:**
    *   **Plan:** At the beginning of the lesson, voice and write down a brief plan for the upcoming work.
    *   **Visual:** Use schemes, algorithms, tables. For the sequence of events - timline and step -by -step schemes.
*   **Environment:**
    *   **Accommodation:** The optimal place is at the first or second desk, where there are fewer distracting factors.
    *   **Dynamics:** Alternate the formats of work: explanation, work at the board, independent exercises, pair work.
*   **Individual approach (IOM):**
    *   **Support:** The main thing is assistance in organizing activities, not a simplification of the program.
    *   **Templates:** Provide ready-made checklists or templates for complex tasks (for example, a presentation plan or laboratory work).

### 4. Developing training

These exercises will help strengthen cognitive skills in interactive form.

*   **Attention and self -control:**
    *   **Board games:** Checkers, chess, jenga, un. They require concentration, compliance with the rules and control of impulses.
    *   **Graphic dictations:** They train auditory attention and ability to follow the algorithm.
*   **Consistent memory and structuring:**
    *   **The game "I put in a bag ...":** Each participant takes turns repeating these objects and adds his own. He trains the auditory, consistent memory.
    *   **Compilation of comics:** Ask the student to draw 3-4 key frames based on the chapter or cartoon read. This teaches to highlight the main thing and build the logic of events.
    *   **Cooking by recipe:** The exact follow -up of the recipe steps is an excellent training sequence.
*   **Coordination and bodily control:**
    *   **Rhythmic exercises:** Repetition or taping of rhythmic drawings. It can be complicated using both hands in different ways.
    *   **Games "The Sea is worried once" or "Delay":** They train the ability to quickly switch between movement and statics, improving bodily control.
    *   **Juggling:** Start with two balls or scarves. This develops the coordination of the "eye-hand", concentration and interhemispheric interaction.

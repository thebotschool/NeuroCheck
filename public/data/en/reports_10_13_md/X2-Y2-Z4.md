**Individual report: Cognitive profile**
*Tests: CPT, Go/No-Go, visual working memory. Age: 10-13 years.*

The report is designed to reveal the unique profile of your student, identifying the strengths and areas for development. The proposed recommendations will help create a comfortable and effective training environment.

### **1. Analysis of tests**

*   **Attention (Continous Performance Test):** The preservation of sustainable attention to one task and control over actions is difficult, especially with the monotony of the assignment.
*   **Braking (Go/No-Go Test):** There is a tendency to impulsive actions. The timely cessation of activity requiring deterrence is difficult.
*   **Visual working memory:** The ability to memorize visual information is present, but the details can be missed, and similar images are confused, especially when distracting.

<!-- page-break -->

### **2. Guide for training**

#### **Features of perception**

*   **Pace:** Active, but may be uneven due to haste. The child begins the task without listening to the instructions, which leads to errors.
*   **Information format:** Short, clear, visual instructions are most effectively perceived. Long oral explanations lose their meaning due to scattering attention.
*   **Focus of attention:** Unstable. It is easily distracted by external stimuli or internal thoughts. The period of productive concentration on one task is limited.

#### **Supporting environment**

*   **Step -by -step instructions:** Separate large tasks into 2-3 small stages. Use checks or diagrams.
*   **Visual support:** Apply images, graphs, high words in color. This helps to keep attention.
*   **Rhythm and rituals:** Set a clear mode of operation and rest (for example, 15 minutes of work, 5 minutes of activity). The ritual of the beginning of activity (for example, several deep breaths) helps to concentrate.
*   **REALLY REALTH:** Before starting the assignment, ask the child to briefly state what needs to be done. This slows down impulsive reactions and improves understanding.
*   **A single channel of information:** Interact sequentially: either say or demonstrate. The simultaneous presentation of complex material may be excessive.

#### **Distraction factors**

*   **Multitasking:** An attempt to fulfill several things at the same time (listen and write down) almost always leads to a loss of information.
*   **External noises:** An open door, conversations, a view from the window - strong distracting factors.
*   **Frequent change of tasks:** Quick switching without a pause does not allow to adapt and leads to errors.
*   **Temporary pressure:** Phrases like “Faster!”, “Little time!” They cause anxiety and enhance impulsiveness.
*   **Long oral instructions:** Instructions of more than 1-2 sentences may not be learned at a time.

### **3. Recommendations**

#### **Home environment**

*   **Organization of work:**
    *   **Break the tasks:** Formulate specific steps, for example: "We will make three examples in mathematics."
    *   **Use the timer:** Install the timer for 10-15 minutes for productive work. After the signal - a short break.
    *   **Stage check:** Control execution after each small block. This allows you to quickly identify and correct errors.
*   **Support:**
    *   **Encourage the process:** Mark efforts: concentration, distraction, control over pulses. Focus on phrases like “You were very attentive!”, And not just on an ideal result.
    *   **Create a quiet zone:** The workplace should be ordered, free from toys and gadgets.

#### **School environment**

*   **The wording of tasks:**
    *   **One task at a time:** Write on the board or on the card only the current task.
    *   **Clear verbs:** Use direct commands: “Emphasize”, “Cover”, “Insert”, “Solve”.
    *   **Visual accents:** Select keywords in the instructions with color or bold.
*   **Lesson structure:**
    *   **Plan on the board:** At the beginning of the lesson, indicate 2-3 main points. This creates predictability and helps to navigate.
    *   **Class placement:** The optimal place is the first rows in the center, far from windows and doors. This minimizes distraction and simplifies eye contact with the teacher.
*   **Individual route:**
    *   The provision of instructions in the format of algorithm cards will be useful.
    *   Discuss with the school psychologist the opportunity to include the child in group classes on the development of attention and self -control.

### **4. Developing classes**

#### **For attention**

*   **"Corrective test":** Suggest to cross certain letters (for example, “a”) or to circle prefigures (“on”) on the magazine page. Start with 3-5 minutes.
*   **"Find 10 differences":** Classical game that train concentration on details.
*   **"What has changed?":** Place 5-7 items. Ask the child to remember, then remove or change one item.

#### **For braking and control**

*   **"The sea is worried once":** A game that develops the skill of quick stopping on command.
*   **"Edible - inedible":** He trains a quick reaction and its cancellation (not to catch).
*   **"Rhythmic pops":** You set the rhythm (for example, two cotton, pause, one cotton), the child repeats.

#### **For visual memory**

*   **"Memory" ("Find a couple"):** Ideal for training accurate memorization of visual images.
*   **"Remember and draw":** Show a simple figure (house, flower) for 10 seconds. Ask to draw from memory.
*   **"I put in a bag ...":** Compose history, consistently adding objects and recalling all the previous ones. Use cards with images for visual support.

**Cognitive profile. Age 19–22.**

This report is an individual view of the cognitive features of your student. It is designed to identify strengths, determine the potential for development and offer practical strategies to optimize the educational process.

## 1. Analysis. Key conclusions.

*   **Working memory (CPT).** Successful memorization of individual facts and details is noted. There is a potential for improving the systematization of information and building logical connections.
*   **Braking (Go/No-Go).** The tendency to impulsive reactions and difficulties with maintaining attention are manifested, especially with fatigue or high complexity of the task.
*   **Attention and impulsiveness.** In situations requiring quick solutions, there is a tendency to haste. This can lead to errors by carelessness, even with confident knowledge of the material.

<!-- page-break -->

## 2. The guide for the success of your student.

### Personal profile.

*   **Pace.** The tendency to a quick, but impetuous beginning of tasks. It is possible to reduce concentration without a clear structure.
*   **Perception.** Effective assimilation of information through visual and structured formats (diagrams, tables, step -by -step lists). Monotonous text or prolonged oral explanations are more complicated.
*   **Focus.** Attention can be unstable. High sensitivity to external stimuli. With fatigue, it is possible to “focus” on the details to the detriment of the general understanding.

### Optimal conditions.

*   **Clear instructions.** Step -by -step, brief, numbered instructions.
*   **Visual supports.** Schemes, timline, highlighting keywords, recovery stickers.
*   **The habit of "stop - think - do it."** A short pause before action or answer.
*   **Interval work.** Method "Prydor" (20–25 minutes of work, 5 minutes of rest) to maintain concentration.
*   **Specific feedback.** Focus on achievements and opportunities for growth.

### Obstacles for progress.

*   **Multitasking.** The requirement of the simultaneous performance of several actions or the retention of many instructions.
*   **Hurry and pressure.** Time restrictions provoking impulsive errors.
*   **Information overload.** A large amount of new material without visual or structural support.
*   **Distracting factors.** Noise, conversations, disorder in the workplace.

<!-- page-break -->

## 3. Recommendations. Individual approach.

### Home environment.

*   **Organization of work.**
    *   Divide large tasks into small, controlled steps. Use checklists.
    *   The recommended duration of continuous work is 20–25 minutes. This is followed by a short break (movement, water).
    *   Plan lessons in advance: "First mathematics (20 minutes), then rest, then history (20 minutes)."
*   **Support and promotion.**
    *   Praise for thoughtfulness and self -testing, and not for speed. Example: "Great idea, let's cheat with the plan."
    *   Help structure the information: offer a paragraph scheme or a plan for writing.

### Educational process.

*   **The formulation of tasks.**
    *   Tasks should be as specific as possible, preferably step by step. Example: "1. Choose the topic until Friday. 2. Compose a plan of 5 points to Wednesday. 3. Choose 3 sources of information ..."
    *   Place the key instructions or the lesson plan in a prominent place.
*   **The structure of the material.**
    *   Use the interactive style: alternate short lectures (10-15 minutes) with discussions, practice or pair work.
    *   When explaining the new material, actively use visual means: schemes, tables, presentations, videos.

### Individual settings.

*   **Control work.** In exams and control, it is advisable to provide additional 5-10 minutes for self -testing. This will reduce stress and prevent impulsive errors.
*   **Workplace.** Accommodation on the first desk, far from distracting factors (window, door), can significantly improve concentration.

## 4. Training. Practical skills.

These exercises will help to develop self -control, attention and organizational abilities in a playful way.

### Focus and self -regulation.

*   **Board games.** Chess, checkers, karcasson, colonialists. Develop strategic thinking and restraint of impulses.
*   **The game is "edible-universal" (complicated).** Clap or catch the ball according to additional signs (for example, on "round" or "living"). He trains flexibility of attention and braking.

### Memory and logic.

*   **Comics stories.** Compilation of a coherent and logical plot from pictures without text.
*   **Recipe preparation.** Following the step -by -step algorithm.
*   **Assembly of models.** Lego Technic, prefabricated models. It requires careful reading of instructions and accurate reproduction of the sequence of actions.

### Coordination and rhythm.

*   **Musical instruments.** Especially shock. Fixing the rhythm improves the connection between thinking and action, contributes to general self -control.
*   **Juggling.** Develops coordination, concentration and distribution of attention.
*   **Dancing with repeating movements.** Learning and reproduction of ligaments trains motor and visual memory.

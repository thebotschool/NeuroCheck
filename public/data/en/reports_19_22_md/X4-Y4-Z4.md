# The cognitive profile of the student
## Assessment of attention, control and working memory (age: 19–22 years)

This report is designed to help parents and teachers better understand the unique features of your student. Information is based on the results of cognitive tests and offers practical recommendations to support development.

***

### 1. Test results

*   **Preservation (CPT):** Difficulties in maintaining the focus, especially with fatigue or external stimuli, have been noted. It is difficult for a student to keep several tasks or steps at the same time.
*   **Control and braking (Go/No-Go):** Difficulties are observed in switching tasks and controlling impulsive reactions. This can be manifested in hasty answers or actions.
*   **Information processing speed (TCP):** In conditions requiring speed and accuracy, cognitive overload is manifested. This slows down the reaction and increases the likelihood of errors.

**General conclusion:** Tests revealed cognitive overload. It is difficult for a student to manage attention, control impulses and effectively process information, especially under pressure. These results do not reflect the student’s abilities, but indicate the need for adaptation of teaching and support methods.

### 2. The guide for the success of your student

#### A unique approach

*   **Pace:** Slow, thoughtful. It takes time to comprehend. Hurry reduces productivity and can lead to errors.
*   **Perception:** The visual, step -by -step format is optimal. Schemes, lists, illustrations are more effective than continuous text or oral explanations.
*   **Attention:** Exhausted. The effective concentration lasts 10-15 minutes, then a brief break is required for “rebooting”.

#### Support

*   **Visual supports:** Algorithms, rules, plans are more visible.
*   **Step -by -step instructions:** One action at a time. Example: "Open page 25. Find exercise 5. Read the task."
*   **Predictability:** A clear daily routine and the structure of the lesson reduce anxiety and release the resource for training. Example: "We write for 5 minutes, then we discuss."
*   **Check lists:** Short lists for independent verification.
*   **Organized environment:** Silence, a minimum of distracting factors. Clean workplace.

#### Obstacles

*   **Multitasking:** The implementation of several tasks simultaneously reduces effectiveness.
*   **Long instructions:** More than 2-3 steps require division into parts and visual support.
*   **Time pressure:** Phrases like "Faster!" They cause stress, block thinking and increase errors.
*   **Chaos and distraction:** Noise, disorder in the workplace.
*   **Abstract explanations:** Lack of specific examples or clarity.

### 3. Recommendations

#### Home environment

*   **Working process:**
    *   Short "sprints": classes for 15-20 minutes, with 5-minute interruptions in activity.
    *   Priority: start with complex tasks while the energy is high.
    *   Decomposition: Savior large tasks into small steps. Example: "We will solve the first problem, then the second."
*   **Motivation:**
    *   Praise: mark the process and efforts, and not just the result. Example: "I see how you tried!"
    *   Planning: Make a work plan together. Example: "Where will we start?"
*   **Space:**
    *   Workplace: clean, permanent. Take out extra objects before the start of classes.

#### School environment

*   **Instructions:**
    *   Clarity: short, step -by -step instructions. Use the imperative verbs: “Emphasize”, “Cover”, “Write down”.
    *   Visualization: Duplicate oral instructions in writing on a board or on an individual card.
*   **Training process:**
    *   Consistency: important information on the board is always in one place (page number, tasks).
    *   Interactive: Use cards, schemes, mental cards.
    *   Location: the first-second desk in the center of the class is an ideal place to minimize abstracts.
*   **Individual route:**
    *   Additional time: provision in control and independent work.
    *   Tools: Allow the draft with an algorithm or a response plan.
    *   Support: Work with a tutor or a school psychologist for the development of planning and self -organization skills.

### 4. Developing training

Games and exercises for home and schools that contribute to the development of key skills.

*   **Attention:**
    *   Graphic dictations.
    *   Corrective tests (deleting letters).
    *   Games "Find Differences".
*   **Coordination:**
    *   Games with the ball (throws, beating).
    *   Exercises for balance ("heron", walking along the line).
*   **Memory:**
    *   The game "Memory".
    *   Words chains (consistent addition).
*   **Rhythm and self -regulation:**
    *   Testing rhythms after adults.
    *   The game "Stop game" (movement to music and fading).

### The cognitive profile of the student

This report offers a detailed view of the cognitive features of your student. The goal is to provide clear recommendations to support its development and training, applicable both at home and at school.

---

### 1. The main results

*   **Attention and memory (CPT):** Difficulties with the retention of several elements of the task, especially with an increase in their number, have been noted. This indicates a quick cognitive overload and difficulties in planning actions from beginning to end.

*   **Impulse control (Go/No-Go):** The tendency to quick, impulsive actions without a complete analysis of conditions has been revealed. This can lead to hasty decisions and errors that can be avoided.

*   **Visual working memory:** In conditions requiring concentration, there is a tendency to haste. Such a reaction is an adaptive mechanism to stress, but reduces the accuracy of the tasks.

**Review:** Key features - **impulsiveness in combination with working memory prone to overload**. The student is inclined to act until comprehension and planning. An attempt to keep a lot of information causes frustration and desire to complete the task faster.

### 2. The guide for the success of your student

#### Individual style

*   **Pace:** The natural pace is fast. External support is required for the development of a thoughtful, measured approach.
*   **Perception:** Information is better absorbed in visual and step -by -step format. Long oral instructions can be missed.
*   **Focus of attention:** Attention can be distracted by internal impulses. This is not distraction, but the desire to react immediately.

#### Support: what works

*   **Visual supports:** Check lists, schemes, intelligence cards, highlighting key points. Any information that can be seen.
*   **Rule "Stop - Think - do":** The introduction of a conscious pause before action.
*   **Crushing of tasks:** Small the large tasks into 2-4 small, clear steps. The completion of each step gives a sense of control.
*   **Predictable environment:** A clear daily routine, understandable rules and algorithms reduce the cognitive load.

#### Calls: what to avoid

*   **Multitasking:** Simultaneous requests or tasks (for example, "clean and think about mathematics") lead to inefficiency.
*   **Long oral instructions:** Instructions consisting of more than 2-3 sequential actions can be partially or inaccurately performed.
*   **High pace and pressure of time:** The phrases "Faster!" Or strict time frames enhance stress and impulsiveness.
*   **Unstructured information:** A continuous text without paragraphs, lists or discharge is perceived as "information noise".

### 3. Practical recommendations

#### At home

*   **Homework:**
    *   Break work into short sessions (15–20 minutes) with 5-minute active breaks.
    *   Use a timer to structured working intervals, not to accelerate.
    *   Make a simple plan before starting: "1. Read the rule. 2. Perform exercise 5. 3. Check." Mark the executed points.
*   **Motivation and support:**
    *   Praise for **process and effort**: "You are in no hurry and double -check - this is excellent!", "It is good that you followed the plan."
    *   In case of errors, analyze: "Where are we hurrying? What can be taken into account next time?"
*   **Daily tasks:** Use stickers or checks for routine cases (morning fees, cleaning). This reduces the cognitive load.

#### At school

*   **Instructions:**
    *   Use the imperative mood and numbered lists: "1. Read text. 2. Emphasize the main words. 3. Write them out."
    *   Give only one task at a time.
*   **Learning environment:**
    *   Place the student in place with a minimum of distracting factors (closer to the teacher).
    *   Use visual materials: schemes, plans, algorithms on the board.
    *   Before the control work, remind you of the importance of first read all the tasks.
*   **Individual approach:**
    *   Consider the possibility of providing additional time for testing.
    *   Allow the use of a draft plan before writing a final option.
    *   Provide short, regular feedback in the process of work (“move correctly”, “check this step”).

### 4. Developing exercises

These exercises are aimed at developing self -control, attention and working memory in an accessible format.

*   **Attention and control:**
    *   **"Corrective tests":** Drawing certain letters in the text or bypassing them. Start from 5 minutes.
    *   **"FROME-OUTOMI":** Classic games for bodily control associated with impulse control.
*   **Working memory:**
    *   **"Memory" ("Find a couple"):** He trains visual memory and concentration.
    *   **"What has changed?":** Lay out 5-7 items, ask to remember. Take out or change one item. The task is to name the change.
*   **Rhythm and sequence:**
    *   **"Graphic dictations":** Drawing on cells under dictation. He trains following the instructions.
    *   **Tapping rhythms:** You set the rhythm, the student repeats. Gradually complicate.

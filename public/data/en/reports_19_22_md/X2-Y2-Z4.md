# Cognitive abilities report
**Attention, self -control and visual memory**

This report is designed to reveal the strengths of your child and determine the areas for growth. The recommendations presented will help create an optimal environment for training and development.

# 1. Test results

*   **Visual memory (CPT)**
    The perception of general information is effective. Details can be missed or interpreted erroneously without sufficient concentration. Exact visual instructions are critical.

*   **Reactions inhibition (Go/No-Go)**
    There is a tendency to impulsive actions. Slowing the reaction and thinking before the answer can prevent errors.

*   **Attention and performance (TCP)**
    There is difficulty in simultaneously maintaining attention, reaction speed and accuracy. This may indicate cognitive fatigue or current overload.

# 2. The guide for the success of your student

### Features of training

*   **Pace:** Prone to quick, sometimes hasty completion of tasks. Often begins to act to complete analysis, which leads to errors.
*   **Perception:** Better assimilates visual information (diagrams, video). Requires clear accents to prevent oscillation of parts.
*   **Attention:** Unstable, especially with multitasking or fatigue. External distracting factors (noise, conversations) significantly interfere with concentration.

### Supporting strategies

*   **Step -by -step instructions:** Separation of tasks into specific, consistent steps.
    *   *Example: instead of “Do the exercise” - “Read the task”, “Emphasize the verbs”, “Check the spelling”.*
*   **Visual supports:** The use of checklists, diagrams, color coding, stickers. Helps to structure tasks and hold the details.
*   **The principle of "stop-think-make":** The practice of a conscious pause before action or answer.
    *   *It can be supported by a physical gesture (for example, a finger to the lips).*
*   **Rhythm and predictability:** Establishing a clear daily routine with alternating work and rest. Using a timer for focused intervals (for example, 20 minutes of work, 5 minutes break).

### Interviewing factors

*   **Multitasking:** The simultaneous performance of several actions causes overload and reduces efficiency.
*   **Fast change of activity:** The disadvantage of the pause when switching between tasks leads to "stuck" and errors.
*   **External distracting factors:** Noise, disorder on the table, active movement exhaust the resource of attention.
*   **Long oral instructions:** Only the beginning or end is perceived. The instructions should be short and supported visually.

# 3. Practical recommendations

### For the house

*   **Homework**
    *   Miss large tasks into smaller parts.
        *   *Example: instead of “make mathematics” - “solve the first three examples”, then a break, then “solve the next problem”.*
    *   Use the timer (for example, the method of prying: 20 minutes of work, 5 minutes of rest) to train concentration and control of control.
    *   Organize a workplace without extra items.
*   **Support and promotion**
    *   Mark attentiveness and ability to test yourself, not just speed.
    *   Discuss errors as opportunities for growth: "You hurried. Next time we will try to read twice?"
*   **Holding attention**
    *   The optimal focus time on the educational task is **15–20 minutes**. After that, a short physical or visual break is needed.

### For school

*   **The formulation of tasks**
    *   Provide specific tasks, one at a time. Write them down on a board or card.
    *   Use visual markers: emphasizing, coloring, numbered lists.
*   **The structure of the lesson**
    *   Put the student closer to the teacher, away from distracting factors (window, door).
    *   Before the control work, accent the rule "First check, then hand over."
    *   Give short, clear instructions, duplicating them visually on the board.
*   **Elements of an individual educational route (IOM)**
    *   Consider the provision of additional time to perform tests.
    *   Allow the use of a simple checklist or plan for complex tasks.
    *   Encourage a short pause before the oral answer.

# 4. Targeted training

The following exercises develop skills in need of support. Regularity (10-15 minutes daily) is more important than the duration.

*   **For attention**
    *   Games "Find 10 differences", "Corrective test" (deleting certain letters), Shultte tables.
    *   Labyrinths: Passing with a pencil without separation trains concentration and visual-motor control.
*   **For coordination and control (helps with impulsiveness)**
    *   Balance exercises: standing on one leg, walking along the line.
    *   Games with the ball: juggling, fishing by signal (for example, cotton).
*   **For memory and visual accuracy**
    *   The game "Memory": Search for paired cards. Start with 4-6 pairs, gradually increasing.
    *   The game "What has changed?": Memorization of 5-7 items, then a change in their position/removal of one.
*   **For rhythm and self -regulation**
    *   Testing/cooling rhythms: repeating the given rhythm for synchronization of the nervous system.
    *   Dance games (for example, just dance) with an accurate repetition of movements.

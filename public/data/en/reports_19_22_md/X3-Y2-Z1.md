# Report on the development of the student

This report is designed to help parents and teachers understand the strengths of the student and determine the growth zones. Recommendations are aimed at creating a favorable environment for effective training and comprehensive development.

---

# 1. Key results

### Test for attention (CPT/TCP)
The student demonstrates high concentration. It reacts perfectly to tasks and works without errors caused by inattention. This is a valuable quality, especially for independent work and preparation for important tests.

### Braking Test (Go/No-Go)
There is a tendency to hasty, impulsive actions. It can be difficult for a student to stop in time, which can lead to rash answers or decisions.

### Visual Working Test
The student remembers individual elements of information well. However, they can confuse their sequence. This indicates the need for the development of data organization skills in logical chains and building step -by -step plans.

# 2. The guide for the success of the student

The student has powerful potential: strong attention and high speed. He needs support in the development of "steering" (planning) and "brakes" (self -control).

### Peculiarities
*   **Pace:** Tall, capable of working very quickly. However, this speed sometimes reduces quality due to haste and lack of self-testing.
*   **Perception:** It is best assimilating structured, visually presented information. Schemes, maps, lists and algorithms are perceived easier than a continuous text.
*   **Focus of attention:** Capable of deep concentration. At the same time, impulsiveness can lead to “jumping” between the details, the loss of the general structure or distracting external incentives without clear boundaries of the task.

### Which helps
*   **Step -by -step instructions:** Check lists and clear algorithms for complex tasks.
*   **Visual supports:** Mental cards for abstracts, timline for history, schemes for natural sciences.
*   **The principle of "think-make":** The introduction of a conscious pause before the answer or action to reduce impulsivity.
*   **Clear deadlines:** Intermediate control for planning and completing tasks.

### What interferes
*   **Multitasking:** Simultaneous tracking of several processes leads to errors and chaos.
*   **Abstract tasks:** General instructions without a clear plan, such as "write an essay", make it difficult to organize the work.
*   **Strong dwell:** The pressure of time provokes rash decisions, instead of mobilizing resources.
*   **Chaotic atmosphere:** Infusal or background noise enhance hasty reactions, interfering with consistent thinking.

# 3. Recommendations

### For the house
*   **Homework organization:**
    *   Miss large tasks into small, understandable steps. For example, for history: "Read the paragraph, write out 5 key dates, answer questions."
    *   Use a timer (for example, Pomodoro: 25 minutes of work, 5 minutes of rest). This helps to structure time and maintain concentration.
    *   Together drawing up a weekly plan using stickers or digital planners to visualize the load.
*   **Support and encouragement:**
    *   Praise not only for the result, but also for the efforts. For example: "It is wonderful how you planned this task!" Or "well done, which was in no hurry and checked everything."
    *   Introduce the “second glance” rule: after completing the task, set it up for 10-15 minutes, then check with a fresh look. This develops reflection.
*   **Mercing of attention:**
    *   The student works effectively on one task for 30–45 minutes. After that, he needs a short break (5-10 minutes). It is advisable with a change in activity: get up, grieve, look out the window.

### For school
*   **The wording of tasks:**
    *   Provide tasks with clockwork, measurable criteria. For example: "The essay should contain at least three arguments with examples from the text."
    *   At the beginning of the lesson, voice the plan: "Today we will study X, complete the exercise Y, discuss Z." This creates a predictable structure.
*   **Material feed structure:**
    *   Actively use visual materials: schemes, tables, infographics.
    *   When explaining the new material, emphasize logical connections and sequence. For example: "This is because ...", "At first it was a, then b."
*   **Individual approach:**
    *   **Class placement:** Plant the student away from distracting factors (windows, doors). This will help him not switch attention to extraneous incentives.
    *   **Style style:** Before the question, make a small pause. This gives the student the time to think over the answer, and not shout the first thing that occurred.
    *   **Project activity:** Provide templates or checklists for planning. This will help to break large projects into controlled stages and successfully complete them.

# 4. Training classes

These exercises will help to develop self -control and planning skills in a fascinating form.

### Attention and braking
*   **Game "Delay":** During active activity (dance, cleaning), by signal (for example, cotton), you need to freeze instantly. Exercise trains the skill of quick stop stop.
*   **Board games:** Chess, checkers, "jenga" require the expectation of their course, the miscalculation of the consequences and restraint at momentary impulses.

### Memory and sequence
*   **Drawing up instructions:** Ask to write or sketch a step -by -step instruction for simple action (brew tea, assemble a model from LEGO).
*   **The game "The story on the chain":** One begins the story, the second repeats and adds its own. This trains consistent memory and logic.

### Bodily control and rhythm
*   **Balance exercises:** Standing on one leg, walking along the line. Improve the connection between the brain and the body, developing self -control.
*   **Rhythmic exercises:** Removing or cutting rhythms according to the model. It helps to "set up" the internal pace and better control your reactions.

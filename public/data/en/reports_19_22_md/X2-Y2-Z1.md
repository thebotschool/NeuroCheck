## Assessment of cognitive development

Your guide for the understanding and development of the student’s potential. Our recommendations are created to support an effective training environment.

---

### 1. Key conclusions

*   **Attention (CPT):** With fatigue or distraction, inaccuracies in the perception of parts may occur.
*   **Impulsive control (Go/No-Go):** The tendency to quick, rash actions has been revealed. The development of a pause before the reaction is required.
*   **Attention (TCP):** The high potential for a quick reaction and deep concentration, especially in the presence of interest in the task.

**General profile:** The combination of high potential for concentration with the need for impulsive management. Efficiency depends on external conditions and self -control.

<!-- page-break -->

### 2. The guide for the success of your student

**Individual characteristics**

*   **Pace:** Fast, gusty. A conscious slowdown for accuracy is required.
*   **Perception:** Optimally assimilates structured, visual information (diagrams, lists). Monotony and excess details reduce attention.
*   **Focus:** High concentration on interesting tasks. On routine tasks, attention is reduced, impulsiveness is growing.

**Which contributes**

*   Step-by-step instructions and checklists.
*   Visual support: text highlighting, stickers, supporting abstracts.
*   The principle of "stop-think-make."
*   Short working intervals (for example, a method of siring).
*   The predictable environment and a clear daily routine.

**What prevents**

*   Multitasking and frequent switching.
*   Tough time frame.
*   Visual and audial background (notifications, background noise).
*   Long, monotonous tasks.

<!-- page-break -->

### 3. Practical recommendations

**For the house**

*   **Homework organization:**
    *   Separate large tasks into several small ones.
    *   Use the timer (25-30 minutes of focused work, then a short break).
    *   Form a brief written plan before starting work.
*   **Support and encouragement:**
    *   Encourage attentiveness and self -control, not speed. Mark the manifestation of independent verification.
    *   Discuss the process of completing the tasks: "What helped?" "What was difficult?"
*   **Attention management:**
    *   Create a “zone without gadgets” for the duration of classes (phone outside the access zone).

**For school**

*   **The wording of tasks:**
    *   Provide clear, step -by -step instructions, preferably in writing.
    *   Sound the lesson plan and expected results at the beginning of the lesson.
*   **Lesson structure and material presentation:**
    *   Optimize the place in the class (first-second desk, center) to minimize abstracts.
    *   Use visual supports (key theses, formulas, plan on the board).
    *   Paise before questions to the class. Use phrases that attract attention ("Question on attentiveness ...").
*   **Elements of an individual educational route (IOM):**
    *   Provide additional time on control for verification and correction.
    *   Allow the use of checklists for self-control in design work.

<!-- page-break -->

### 4. Recommended practices

These exercises are aimed at developing key skills.

*   **Attention and visual accuracy:**
    *   Board games: “set”, “code names” (develop symbol analysis and search for coincidences).
    *   Corrective test: daily 5-minute exercises (cutting off the set letters in the text).
*   **Monitoring and braking of impulsiveness:**
    *   Jugling: perfectly develops coordination and control of movements.
    *   Games with “Stop Signal”: for example, “Freeze” (they train an instant bus stop).
*   **Memory and rhythm:**
    *   Rhythmic exercises: tapping and repetition of rhythms for adjusting the internal pace.
    *   Neurotransers: short daily sessions (10-15 minutes) in memory training and attention (lumosity, wikium).

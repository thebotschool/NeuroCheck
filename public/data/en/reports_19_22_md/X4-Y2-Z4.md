## Cognitive functions report

This report offers a detailed view of the cognitive abilities of your student. It is designed to identify strengths and designate areas for development, providing practical recommendations for home and school.

---

## 1. Review of tests

*   **CPT test:** Holding consecutive information in memory requires effort. In prolonged tasks, fatigue may occur, reducing the accuracy of the execution.
*   **Test Go/No-Go:** The tendency to impulsive reactions has been revealed. Fast answers, ahead of understanding, can lead to inaccuracies.
*   **TCP test:** Indicates current difficulties with attention resistance and concentration. Possible reasons include general fatigue or increased anxiety, which affects the resource of attention.

## 2. Profile of the success of the student

### Individual profile

*   **Pace:** Variable. The period of adaptation to the task is characteristic, followed by a rush sometimes, which affects the quality. There is a tendency to impulsive solutions.
*   **Perception format:** The most effective information is absorbed through **visual and step -by -step formats**: schemes, algorithms, checklists, short video materials. Volumetric text or prolonged oral explanations can quickly cause overload and loss of attention.
*   **Focus of attention:** Unstable. Productive holding of concentration on one task is about **10-15 minutes**. Distraction is possible both on external stimuli (noise, movement) and on internal thoughts.

### Supporting factors

*   Visual tools: checklists with stages of execution, intelligence cards for structuring information, highlighting key concepts by marker.
*   Decomposition of tasks: dividing large tasks into smaller, controlled parts. For example, instead of one homework - several short stages with breaks.
*   The “internal pause” technique: a short -term stop (for example, an account up to three or a deep breath) before the answer or action. This contributes to the development of self -control.
*   Clear routine: predictable structure of the day and educational process. This reduces anxiety and optimizes the use of resource attention.

### Factors that reduce efficiency

*   Multivativity: the simultaneous performance of several actions (for example, study and the use of gadgets) leads to errors and rapid fatigue.
*   Time pressure: phrases stimulating a rush increase impulsiveness and anxiety, provoking errors.
*   Monotony: voluminous textual material or long -term oral instructions without visual support are perceived with difficulty.
*   The disadvantage of the structure: General tasks without a clear plan of action can cause difficulties, since an independent execution algorithm is complicated.

## 3. Practical recommendations

### For the home environment

1.  **Homework organization:**
    *   Divide the school time into 15-minute blocks, alternating with 5-minute breaks to physical activity.
    *   Apply a timer as a visualization tool for a working cycle and recreation periods, not as a means of speed control.
    *   Create **Visual plan** Homework: for example, the sequence of actions and objects. Distribution of the points executed enhances the feeling of control and achievement.

2.  **Support and encouragement:**
    *   Mark **The effort and the process itself**, and not just the final result. For example: "You followed the plan!" Or "It's good that I paused before the answer!"
    *   With errors caused by inattention, avoid criticism. Calmly suggest: “Perhaps you hurried. Let's analyze this moment together, step by step. ”

### For the school environment

1.  **Submission of educational material:**
    *   Step -by -step instructions: formulate the tasks in stages, convinced of the understanding of every step.
    *   Visual duplication: Write down the key elements of oral explanations (plan, terms, links) on the board.
    *   Clear formulations: replace the general instructions with specific actions (for example, “Read point 1, write out three concepts, create a diagram” instead of “Study the paragraph”).

2.  **Organization of working space and time:**
    *   Choice of a place: the first-second desk in the central row, away from distracting factors (windows, doors, active zones).
    *   Dynamics of the lesson: alternating activities every 15–20 minutes (letter, survey, steam work, short videos).
    *   Adaptation for control: When performing control works, it can be useful:
        *   Extra time.
        *   The use of pre-composed checklists or algorithms.
        *   Separation of volumetric work into several stages.

## 4. Training activity

### For the development of attention

*   **The game "Corrective test":** On the text page, suggest to disrupt certain letters or circle others. You should start with simple options, gradually increasing the complexity.
*   **Game "Find the differences":** Classical exercise for the development of concentration and visual attention.

### For self -control and coordination

*   **The game "Sea is worried":** Develops the ability to stop and control movements by signal.
*   **The game is "edible-universal":** Promotes to train the reaction speed and suppress impulsive movements.
*   **Balance exercises:** For example, a stand on one leg or walking in a straight line.

### For working memory and rhythm

*   **The game "What has changed?"**: Lay out several items. After remembering the child, remove or move one. The task is to determine the change.
*   **Rhythmic exercises:** Cut or fit a simple rhythm, the task is to repeat it exactly. This develops auditory attention, memory and a sense of rhythm that are important for self -regulation.

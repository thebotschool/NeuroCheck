# **Your cognitive profile**

This report reveals the unique abilities of the student, determining his strengths and areas for development. Recommendations are designed to support effective training and everyday activity.

---

## 1. Key results

*   **Attention (TCP):** High concentration. The effectiveness of the work depends on the clarity of the task and the level of rest.
*   **Visual memory (CPT):** He perceives the overall picture well. Attention is required to avoid superficial assimilation.
*   **Self-control (Go/No-Go):** In conditions of stress or fatigue, difficulties with reaction control are possible.

**Conclusion:** Powerful concentration potential. The performance is sensitive to fatigue and stress, which affects self -control and accuracy.

## 2. Learning style

### Individual characteristics
*   **Pace:** "Sprinter". High productivity in short intervals. Long -term monotonous load reduces the pace and increases errors.
*   **Perception:** Prefers structured, visual information (diagrams, infographics). The accents on key details are important.
*   **Focus:** Deep immersion in one task. Sustainable in calm, but sensitive to multitasking, noise and overwork.

### Which helps
*   **Timer:** Ideal method "Prydor" (25/5).
*   **Visual anchors:** Color selection of key elements.
*   **Step -by -step instructions:** A clear plan of action reduces the load.
*   **Organized environment:** A quiet place without distracting factors.

### What interferes
*   **Multitasking:** Simultaneous actions lead to overload.
*   **Monotone:** More than 40 minutes of the work of the same work deplete the resource.
*   **Pressure:** Hurry provokes stress and errors.
*   **Unstructured information:** Lack of plan and visual support.

## 3. Support strategies

### Home environment
*   **Organization of tasks:** Separate large tasks into small blocks. Example: "Mathematics" -> "5 tasks, a break, then the next."
*   **Work on the timer:** Set the timer for 25-30 minutes for each block. A mandatory 5-minute break follows.
*   **Planning:** Make a simple written plan for the evening. This reduces anxiety and gives control.

*   **Support and motivation:**
    *   Mark the process, not just the result. Example: "It's good that I made a break when tired."
    *   Discuss signs of fatigue. Help recognize them as a signal for rest, and not as a manifestation of laziness.
    *   Effective attention lasts about 30 minutes. Do not expect more without a break.

### Educational process
*   **The wording of tasks:** The instructions should be as clear, step -by -step, preferably in writing.
*   **Lesson plan:** In the beginning, voice the structure of the lesson: repetition, new topic, practice.

*   **Structure and material:**
    *   Alternate activities every 15–20 minutes (explanation, work in pairs, independent work).
    *   Use visual supports (presentations, diagrams, terms on the board) to fix attention.
    *   Recommended place in the class: front, far from distracting factors.

*   **Individual approach:**
    *   On control: allow me to use a draft for a response plan.
    *   Use colored handles/markers to highlight the important. This will reduce errors in carelessness.
    *   In an oral answer: give 10-15 seconds to think about impulsive reactions.

## 4. Practical exercises
These training are aimed at developing key skills and increasing efficiency.

*   **Attention and details:**
    *   **Schultte tables:** Develop concentration and peripheral vision.
    *   **"Find the differences":** Improves attentiveness to details.
    *   **Correct samples:** They train selective attention.

*   **Self -regulation:**
    *   **Respiratory exercises:** “Square breathing” (4-4-4-4) helps to relieve stress and restore control.
    *   **Working with metronome:** Rhythm is tapping the internal control and a sense of pace.

*   **Memory and coordination:**
    *   **Mnemics:** Studying methods of memorization (association, "Palace of Memory") for information.
    *   **Sports games:** Table tennis, badminton, juggling. Improve the coordination of the "eye-hand" and contribute to switching.

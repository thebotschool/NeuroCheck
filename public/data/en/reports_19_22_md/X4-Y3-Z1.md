# The cognitive profile of the student

This report represents the results of cognitive testing. He is designed to help parents and teachers understand the unique features of the development of the student, to determine his strengths and growth zones. The goal is to create optimal conditions for training and comprehensive development.

---

### 1. A brief report for each test

*   **Attention and memory test (CPT)**
    Difficulties with prolonged attention and sequence of actions were revealed, especially with fatigue or information overload. This indicates the need to support self -control and organization.

*   **Braking and control test (Go/No-Go)**
    Difficulties with the control of actions requiring the participation of both hands or quick switching between tasks were noted. Asymmetry is observed in the activity of the hemispheres, which affects the coordination and development of new actions.

*   **Visual Working Memorial Test**
    The results demonstrate difficulties with the simultaneous retention of several visual elements (for example, information from the board, textbook and own records). This leads to rapid memory and errors.

### 2. Instructions for the child as a student

#### Individual characteristics

*   **Pace**
    Optimal, a leisurely, measured pace of work with brief pauses. Hurry and excessive pressure cause chaotic actions and errors.

*   **Perception**
    Information is better absorbed through visual, structured materials (diagrams, maps, algorithms). Visual and tactile contact is important. Long oral instructions without visual support are ineffective.

*   **Focus of attention**
    Attention is unstable, quickly depleted with monotonous work or overload. To maintain concentration, an external structure is necessary: ​​a clear plan, timer, step -by -step instructions.

#### Which contributes

*   **Step -by -step instructions**
    Miss large tasks into 2-3 small, specific steps.

*   **Visual supports**
    Use the selection of keywords, recall stickers, short algorithms on cards.

*   **The rhythm of work**
    Alternate periods of intensive work (15–20 minutes) with short breaks (2-5 minutes) for recovery.

*   **Predictability**
    Report the lesson plan or preparation of tasks in advance. It is important to know the sequence of actions.

#### What makes it difficult

*   **Multitasking**
    The simultaneous performance of several actions (listen, watch, write) overloads and leads to failures.

*   **Fast change of tasks**
    It takes time to move from one task to another. Frequent change of activity disorients.

*   **Distracting factors**
    Excess visual and auditory stimuli (mess, noise) reduce concentration.

*   **Long explanations**
    More than 2-3 offers without a pause or visual support are difficult to perceive.

### 3. Recommendations

#### Home environment

*   **Organization of work**
    *   **The principle of "one table is one task"**
        Before starting work, remove all the extra. On the table there should be only material for the current task.
    *   **Decomposition of tasks**
        Avoid common phrases such as "make lessons." Make a clear plan: 1. Decide 3 examples in mathematics. 2. Rest for 5 minutes. 3. Read 2 pages in literature.
    *   **The use of a timer**
        Set the timer for 15-20 minutes. At the signal - a mandatory short break for activity (movement, water).
*   **Support and motivation**
    *   **Focus on the process**
        Mark efforts and the process (“I see how you have concentrated!”), And not just the result.
    *   **Concentration limit**
        Effective attention remains 15–20 minutes. Do not require long -term continuous work.
    *   **The role of an external organizer**
        Help in planning, but do not complete the task for the child.

#### School environment

*   **The formulation of tasks**
    *   **The clarity of instructions**
        Let's briefly, one action at a time. For example: “They opened notebooks. (Pause). Record the number. (Pause). Open page 5. "
    *   **Verbs of action**
        Use active verbs: “emphasize”, “monitor”, “connect the arrows”.
*   **Structuring the material**
    *   **Visual structure**
        Use visual supports: a plan of the lesson on the board, algorithms on cards, highlighting key points.
    *   **The optimal location**
        It is recommended that a place on the first or second desk in the central row to minimize abstracts and convenient visual contact.
*   **Elements of an individual educational route (IOM)**
    *   **Extra time**
        If necessary, provide extra time for testing.
    *   **Printing templates**
        Allow the use of printed templates for complex tasks (for example, analysis of a word). This will reduce the load on memory and organization.

### 4. Recommended training

These exercises will help to develop key cognitive functions. Regularity is important: 10-15 minutes daily.

*   **For attention and memory**
    *   **Graphic dictations**
        Example: "One cage to the right, two up." Develop auditory attention and retention of instructions.
    *   **Memory game**
        Search for paired cards. Strengthens visual working memory.
    *   **Schultte tables**
        Search for numbers in order by time. Develops concentration and peripheral vision.

*   **For coordination and bodily control (interhemispheric interaction)**
    *   **"Crossroads"**
        Alternate touching the left knee with the right hand and vice versa. Perform slowly, rhythmically.
    *   **"Mirror drawing"**
        Drawing with both hands of symmetrical figures (circles, squares, waves).
    *   **Juggling**
        Start with two items. Synchronizes eyes, hands and brain.

*   **For rhythm and self -regulation**
    *   **Tapping rhythms**
        You set a simple rhythm, the child repeats. Gradually complicate.
    *   **Rhythmic music exercises**
        Simple aerobics, dancing, march. The rhythm organizes the nervous system.

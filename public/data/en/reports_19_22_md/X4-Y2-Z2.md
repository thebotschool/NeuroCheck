# The cognitive profile of the student
## Assessment of attention, self -control and working memory (19–22 years old)

## 1. Key results

*   **Working memory:** Difficulties with retention and consistent use of information. Fast overload and difficulties in planning are possible.
*   **Self -control:** Teenage to impulsive reactions. It can make hasty decisions that leads to errors due to inattention.
*   **Attention:** Increased distress, especially with auditory or monotonous perception. Fast cognitive fatigue and loss of concentration are noted.

<!-- page-break -->

## 2. Individual approach

### Features of perception

*   **Pace:** Requires a measured pace. Hurrying provokes errors, interferes with planning. We need an "internal pause" before action.
*   **Format:** Pronounced visual. Effectively perceives information through diagrams, tables, checklists, infographics. Long oral monologues quickly tire.
*   **Focus:** Unrestrained, easily distracted. The effective concentration lasts 15–20 minutes, then a short break is needed.

### Which contributes to training

*   Clear structure: tasks broken into small steps.
*   Visual supports: diagrams, checklists, Mayind cards, color discharge.
*   Work on the timer: Tomator method (20 minutes of work / 5 minutes of break).
*   Predictability: pre -known algorithms and procedure.
*   Organized workplace: a minimum of distracting items.
*   One task at a time: multitasking is ineffective.

### What complicates training

*   Hurry and pressure of time.
*   Monotonous lectures, long oral instructions.
*   Unstructured, open tasks.
*   Multitasking.
*   Noise and visual disorder.

<!-- page-break -->

## 3. Recommendations

### Home environment

*   **Organization of work:**
    *   Create a visual plan of classes with a timer.
    *   Use checklists for complex tasks.
*   **Support and encouragement:**
    *   Praise for the process and effort, and not just for the result.
    *   Develop reflection with questions: "What was difficult? How did you deal?"
*   **Attention:**
    *   Observe the limit in 15-20 minutes of active work.
    *   Provide short active breaks.

### Educational process

*   **The wording of tasks:**
    *   Use fractional, sequential instructions (numbered lists).
    *   Start instructions with the verbs of action.
*   **Lesson structuring:**
    *   Duplicate key information visually (board, slides).
    *   Break the material into blocks for 15–20 minutes with short pauses.
*   **Class placement:**
    *   Optimal: the first-second desk, far from distracting factors.
*   **Individual route:**
    *   Provide templates/plans for written work.
    *   Consider an additional time or a quiet room for control.
    *   Attracting a school psychologist/tutor to work on planning and self -control.

## 4. Developing exercises

Integrate these exercises into everyday life for the development of cognitive skills.

*   **Attention:**
    *   **Correct samples:** Search for certain symbols in the text.
    *   **Find the differences:** Visual puzzles.
    *   **Board games:** For example, “Dobble” or “set”, requiring quick concentration.

*   **Self -control and coordination:**
    *   **FROM THE FORMERIA:** He trains the inhibition of the reaction.
    *   **Juggling:** Develops visual-motor coordination.
    *   **Balance exercises:** Walking along the line, stand on one leg.

*   **Working memory and rhythm:**
    *   **Memory game:** Develops visual working memory.
    *   **Rhythmic exercises:** Repetition and creation of rhythms.
    *   **"Snowball":** Consistent repetition and addition of elements.

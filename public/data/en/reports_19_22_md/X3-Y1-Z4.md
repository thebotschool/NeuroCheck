# 2. The guide for the success of the student

## Features of perception
*   **Pace:** A measured pace is preferred. A quick change in tasks or rush causes exhaustion.
*   **Format:** The best perception of structured information: schemes, plans, lists. Long oral explanations are more complicated.
*   **Attention:** Attention is unstable, easily exhausted. It may lose focus on long or unstructured tasks. Self -control helps to return, but requires considerable effort.

## Success factors
*   **Visual supports:** Schemes, mental cards, checklists, timline. They reveal the overall structure.
*   **Algorithms:** Break big tasks into 3-5 simple steps. This simplifies the implementation and reduces the alarm.
*   **Rhythm:** Use techniques like "Prydor" (25 minutes work, 5 minutes rest) to maintain energy.
*   **Predictability:** Report in advance about the plans of the lessons or the amount of tasks. This helps to distribute strength.
*   **Focus:** Work on one task. Eliminate distracting factors (disorder on the table, extra tabs).

## Obstacles
*   **Multitasking:** Simultaneous actions (listen, write, watch) cause overload.
*   **Oral instructions:** Long oral instructions without written support are ineffective.
*   **Squeezed deadlines:** The "Tseutite" mode enhances the alarm and suppresses motivation.
*   **Lack of structure:** Faring creative tasks (for example, "write an essay about summer") can cause a stupor. A clear framework ("essays of three paragraphs about the best day of summer") are more understandable.
*   **Noise:** Distracting sounds reduce attention efficiency.

# 3. Personal recommendations

## Home environment
**Work on tasks:**
*   Break work into 20-30-minute blocks with 5-minute breaks.
*   Together, plan the execution: "1. Read. 2. Write out dates. 3. Reply." Focus on **process organization**, not on the decision.
*   Start with the most complex or unloved items.

**Support:**
*   Praise for efforts and following the plan, not for the assessment. Mark the successful performance of small steps.
*   Discuss the condition: "It seems you are tired. Do you need a break?" This will help him recognize fatigue.
*   Provide a quiet, organized workplace.

## School environment
**The wording of tasks:**
*   Present the tasks in writing (board, chat, cards) with a clear algorithm or a list of steps.
*   For large projects, install intermediate deadlines (plan, draft, final work).

**Lesson structure:**
*   Sive up the lesson plan at the beginning: "Today we will consider A, B, V."
*   Duplicate the key points of the lesson on the board/presentation (schemes, lists). This serves as an “anchor” for attention.

**Accommodation:**
*   The optimal place: Closer to the teacher, away from the windows/doors, to minimize abstracts.

**Material feed:**
*   Use a calm, measured pace. Make micropauses before important information.
*   Enjoy its strong side - self -control. Trust the role of an assistant with clear responsibility (for example, distribution of materials) to increase involvement.

# 4. Developing training
The following exercises develop key skills in interactive form.

## Development of attention
*   **Board games:** "Seth", "Volga", "Drum". Develop the reaction rate and the search for coincidences.
*   **Applications:** Lumosity, Elevate. Use dosage, 10-15 minutes daily.

## Memory and organization
*   **Plot construction:** Make comics or storytellers on texts/films. It trains the sequence.
*   **Mental cards:** For any topic (for example, "My Day"), create associative branches, structuring information.

## Rhythm and coordination
*   **Juggling:** Even two balls. Improves concentration, coordination, interhemispheric ties.
*   **Rhythmic exercises:** To the music, tap the specified rhythm alternately with your hands/legs.

## Balance and relaxation
*   **Balance exercises:** Standing on one leg (with open/closed eyes), walking along the line.
*   **Respiratory practices:** Detachment "breathing on the square" (inhale 4, delay 4, exhale 4, delay 4). Effectively reduces anxiety.

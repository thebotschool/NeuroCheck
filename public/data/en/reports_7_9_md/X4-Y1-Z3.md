### **Cognitive assessment report: memory, self -control, attention**

This report contains the results of a cognitive assessment of the student. It is designed to help in revealing its potential and creating an optimal environment for training and development.

---

### **1. Overview of the results**

*   **Memory and stability of attention (CPT):** Difficulties with consistent memorization of volumetric information are noted, especially with fatigue. The schoolboy quickly gets tired of such tasks and does not yet have a developed strategy of memorization.
*   **Braking and self-control (Go/No-Go):** A high level of self -control in structured situations with clumsy rules (“do/do not do” has been demonstrated.
*   **Impulsiveness (TCP):** There is a tendency to act by first motivation, rush and respond to a complete analysis of the task.

**Main conclusions:** The student has significant potential for self -control, which manifests itself in conditions of clear rules. Key difficulties are impulsiveness and lack of strategy for planning complex tasks, which leads to fatigue and errors.

### **2. The guide for the success of your student**

#### **Individual profile**

*   **Pace:** Dual. Natural impetueness is replaced by thoughtfulness with clear rules and a single task.
*   **Perception:** Optimally perceives the information visually and step by step. Long oral or abstract instructions (for example, retelling of the text) cause difficulties.
*   **Focus of attention:** Tunnel. It is capable of deep concentration on one simple task, but attention is scattered with a complication or appearance of the second task. The retention of several conditions is at the same time difficult.

#### **Success factors**

*   **Visual supports:** Check lists, “first → later” schemes, cards with rules, highlighting the text with a marker.
*   **The principle of "one task at a time":** Consistent implementation of simple assignments.
*   **Step -by -step instructions:** Separation of large tasks into 2-3 small, specific steps.
*   **Rhythm and rituals:** Repeating actions (for example, the organization of the workplace) help to tune in and reduce impulsiveness.

#### **Development zones**

*   **Multitasking:** Simultaneous requests or tasks cause overload and stupor.
*   **Hurry and pressure of time:** The phrases “Come on faster” on complex tasks increase chaos and errors.
*   **Long oral instructions:** Only the beginning or end of the sentence is remembered.
*   **Strong distracting factors:** The noise, active games or anticipation of the interesting sharply reduce concentration.

### **3. Recommendations**

#### **At home**

*   **Homework organization:**
    *   Divide homework into short sessions (15–20 minutes) with 5-minute movements.
    *   Use a timer to indicate working intervals, not for speed.
    *   Make a simple written plan for working on a sticker before starting (for example: 1. Solve problem No. 1. 2. Break. 3. Write 3 lines).
*   **Support and encouragement:**
    *   Praise for the process, not for speed: “You try to write carefully”, “It's great that you checked first.”
    *   With errors due to haste, calmly say: “It seems you hurried. Let's get back and do it together, slowly. ”
*   **Development of strategies:**
    *   Before reading: “What can it be about here? Let's look at the pictures. ” This develops a preliminary analysis.
    *   When memorizing verses: draw icons-subscriptions to each line.

#### **At school**

*   **The wording of tasks:**
    *   Give the instructions one at a time, clearly and briefly. Duplicate important steps in writing on the board.
    *   Use visual signals (for example, a green card - start, red - stop).
*   **Lesson structure:**
    *   The optimal place is at the first or second desk to minimize distracting factors and quick contact.
    *   For control: Provide a sheet with registered steps (Planning Plan) so that you can spin the executed.
*   **Material feed style:**
    *   A calm, structured style with an interactive is preferred. Alternate short explanations (5-7 minutes) with practical actions.
*   **Elements of an individual educational route (IOM):**
    *   The visual schedule of the day, personal assistant cards for routine actions (for example, “How to collect a portfolio”) is useful.

### **4. Recommended training**

These exercises will help to develop skills that require attention.

*   **Attention and self -control (braking):**
    *   **"Frozen!" or "the sea is worried once":** Games for an instant stop at a signal.
    *   **"Edible-Sreedy":** The development of the reaction rate and the rapid decision -making “yes/no”.
    *   **"You go quieter - you will continue":** Training control over impulse to movement.

*   **Memory and sequence:**
    *   **"Find a couple" (Memory):** Classical game for the development of visual working memory.
    *   **"Graphic dictation":** Drawing on cells under dictation. Develops following step -by -step instructions and their retention.
    *   **"I put in a bag ...":** The game of repetition of previous items and adding your own.

*   **Rhythm and bodily control:**
    *   **Cooling rhythms:** The exact repetition of the given rhythm with claps with gradual complication.
    *   **Games with the ball:** Throwing the ball with the simultaneous name of something (cities, names). It connects movement, rhythm and cognitive task.
    *   **Walking along the line:** Teaches concentrations and control over the body, which is associated with mental control.

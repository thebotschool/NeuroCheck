# Cognitive functions report

### Introduction

This report offers a systemic view of the cognitive features of your student. The goal is to provide practical tools for creating an optimal environment of development and training.

---

## 1. Analysis of competencies

### Sustainability of attention and working memory
The ability to maintain focus of attention and operate information in memory requires support. Long -term tasks and audio memorization can be difficult, especially with fatigue or distraction.

### Self -control
There is a tendency to impulsive decisions. This can lead to haste and errors caused by inattention.

### Cognitive efficiency
Multipling actions, such as simultaneous perception and recording information, require significant efforts. This can lead to rapid fatigue and loss of concentration.

## 2. The guide for the success of your student

### Individual characteristics

*   **Pace.** Impulsiveness is noted. Interest can quickly arise and fade away just as quickly, which leads to haste. An external rhythm and measured is necessary.
*   **Perception.** Visual and step -by -step format is preferred. Information is better absorbed if you can see or touch it. Long verbal instructions are ineffective.
*   **Focus of attention.** Short -term and vulnerable. Easily distracted by external stimuli. To maintain concentration, silence is necessary and clearly formulated tasks.

### Support methods

*   **Step -by -step instructions.** Offer one task at a time. For example, instead of “Take the room” - “First Gather the toys”.
*   **Visual supports.** Check lists, schemes, visual examples. Everything that can be visualized is more effective than verbal instructions.
*   **Timer.** The visual timer (sand clock, kitchen) helps to track time and plan breaks.
*   **Rhythm.** The tapping of the rhythm before the task helps to configure. Rhythmic exercises improve concentration.
*   **Clear boundaries.** Understanding the beginning and completion of the task gives a feeling of control and confidence.

### Factors requiring attention

*   **Multitasking.** Parallel performance of actions (for example, listening and drawing) depleted attention of attention.
*   **Long instructions.** Oral instructions of three or more steps often lead to incomplete or inaccurate execution.
*   **Hurry and pressure.** Phrases like “let's faster” increase anxiety, provoke impulsiveness and errors.
*   **Background noise.** Extraneous sounds (TV, conversations) distract, reducing the concentration resource.
*   **Lack of structure.** The disorder and the fuzzy plan of the day will disorient, preventing concentration.

## 3. Recommendations

### Home environment

#### Organization of classes

*   **Time.** Start classes after 1-1.5 hours of rest after school (snack, free game).
*   **Duration.** Effective concentration on the task - before **10-15 minutes**. Then a short break is required for 3-5 minutes (water, warm -up).
*   **Structure.** Miss large tasks into small stages. For example, for mathematics: “solve 2 examples”, “relax”, “solve the problem”.

#### Support and motivation

*   Encourage the process, not just the result. Emphasize the effort: “I see how you tried to concentrate!”, “You followed the plan, excellent.”
*   Together, develop visual rules or checklists for routine actions (morning, evening).

#### Emotional regulation

*   Before complex tasks, perform deep breaths-louds. Enjoy the rule: "Feel a rush or irritation - breathe."

### Educational environment

#### The formulation of tasks

*   Use short, clear instructions with verbs of action: ""**Cover** blue "," "**Emphasize** The main thing", "**Solve** example".
*   Select keywords (bold or color).

#### Organization of space and materials

*   **Placement.** It is desirable to first place in the central row to minimize abstracts.
*   **Material feed.** Offer tasks on individual cards or sheets, avoiding distracting elements of the textbook.
*   **Instructions.** Imagine one step at a time. Confirmation of understanding (nod, raised hand) after each step.

#### Individual approach

*   Allow the visual schedule on the desk.
*   Provide extra time for testing.
*   Give the time to think about the answer before the question: "Now I will ask, think." This reduces impulsive reactions.

## 4. Recommended training

These exercises and games are aimed at developing key skills. It is recommended to engage in 10-15 minutes daily in a playful way.

### Development of attention

*   **Corrective tests.** In the text, shut up or circle certain letters (for example, all “a”).
*   **Find 10 differences.** Develops concentration and visual attention.
*   **Graphic dictations.** (For example, “one cage to the right, two up”). Develop auditory attention and spatial thinking.

### Coordination and rhythm

*   **Neurohymnastics.** Exercise "Fist-Robric Ladon". Alternately change the position of the hands on the table, increasing the pace.
*   **Games with the ball.** Beat off the ball from the floor or walls in a given rhythm (slowly, fast).

### Strengthening memory

*   **Memory (find a couple).** He trains visual working memory.
*   **I put in a bag ...** Oral game: everyone repeats previous objects and adds his own. Start with 3-4 items.

### Control and regulation

*   **The sea is worried once.** Teaches body control and timely stop at a signal.
*   **Yes, and no, do not say.** Develops speech control and the ability to restrain impulsive answers.
*   **Edible-universal.** He trains the reaction speed and decision making ('catch/do not catch').

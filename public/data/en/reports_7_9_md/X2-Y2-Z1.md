# Cognitive assessment report

This report is an analysis of the cognitive abilities of your student, based on testing results. We converted the data into practical recommendations for home and school.

## 1. Analysis of key areas

- **Attention for stability (CPT):** The ability to long -term concentration of attention on an understandable task. This is a strong side and an important resource for learning.
- **Self-control test (Go/No-Go):** Difficulties with the suppression of impulsive reactions. The tendency to quick actions without preliminary reflection, especially with enthusiasm.
- **Test of visual working memory:** The desire to remember visual data. Difficulties with the exact reproduction of similar details and information from the middle of the sequence are noted. The initial and final elements are more effective.

## 2. The profile of learning a student

This section describes the features of interaction with the student in the learning process.

### Individual characteristics

-   **Pace:** Fast, sometimes impulsive. It slows down when working with complex visual details that require great efforts.
-   **Perception:** It more efficiently assimilates information by ear and through step -by -step instructions. Visual material (long text, diagrams) requires adaptation and structuring.
-   **Focus:** Deep concentration on one understandable task in the absence of distracting factors. Attention is strong, but narrowly focused.

### Supporting factors

-   **Step -by -step instructions:** Divide tasks into short, numbered steps. Apply the principle: "One instruction is one action."
-   **Visual accents:** Use bright stickers, emphasizing with color, a framework for highlighting key information.
-   **Rhythm and rituals:** Introduce short rituals before starting work. Use rhythmic actions (pops, score) to switch between tasks.
-   **Speaking out loud:** Ask the student to repeat the instructions in your own words before starting work. This helps to comprehend the task.
-   **The principle of "beginning-console":** Use the tendency to remember the first and last elements. Example: “The most important thing in this rule is the first word. Now look at the last example. ”

### Interviewing factors

-   **Multitasking:** The simultaneous performance of several actions (for example, “listen and write down”) leads to errors.
-   **Fast change of tasks:** It takes time for "rebooting" and assimilation of a new instruction. The transition is too fast causing bustle and errors.
-   **Visual overload:** Volumetric pages of the text or many tasks on one sheet can cause confusion.
-   **Complex oral instructions:** Long explanations without pauses and visual support are poorly absorbed.

## 3. Practical recommendations

### Home environment

-   **Homework organization:**
    -   Break your homework into short blocks: 15–20 minutes of work, then 5 minutes of active break.
    -   Make a simple plan before the start: “1. Mathematics. 2. Rest. 3. Reading. " The plan can be recorded.
    -   Use sheets with large markings. When writing, close the irrelevant lines with a ruler or a clean sheet.
-   **Support and feedback:**
    -   Praise the process, not only the result: “I see how you try!”, “It's great that you stopped and thought.”
    -   Avoid common phrases such as “Be careful!”. Offer specific help: “Let's check this word together” or “What was the rule here?”
-   **Duration of concentration:** The student effectively keeps attention on one type of activity for about 15–20 minutes. After this, a change in activity or a short rest is required.

### School environment

-   **The wording of tasks:**
    -   Let's briefly, clearly, step by step. Preferably orally, with duplication on the board.
    -   After voicing the instructions, pause, ask: “What are we doing now in the first place?”
-   **Structuring the material:**
    -   On the work sheets, place no more than 2-3 tasks, sharing them visually.
    -   Select keywords, dates, rules on the board with a colored chalk.
    -   For complex topics, use algorithms and supporting schemes (for example, cards).
-   **Class placement:**
    -   The recommended place is the first-second desk, in the center. This minimizes distraction and facilitates eye contact with the teacher.
-   **Individual learning strategies:**
    -   Allow me to use a ruler or bookmark for following the text when reading or writing off.
    -   Give the opportunity to use a draft.
    -   For testing, requiring visual memory, offer an option with fewer tasks or break them into several parts.

## 4. Developing exercises

These games and exercises are easy to integrate into everyday life for the development of the necessary skills.

-   **For attention:**
    -   **Correct samples:** Ask to cross out certain letters in the text. Complicate by adding other symbols for circuit.
    -   **"Find 10 differences":** Classic exercise for training concentration on details.
-   **For self -control:**
    -   **The game "Sea is worried once":** He teaches to freeze by signal and control the movement of the body.
    -   **The game "edible is inedible":** He trains a quick reaction and the ability to suppress the impulsive effect (not to catch the ball).
    -   **"Please"-games:** The performance of the team is only if there is a word "please."
-   **For visual memory:**
    -   **Memory:** Search for paired cards. Start with a small number of steam, gradually increasing.
    -   **"What has changed?":** Place 5-7 items on the table. The schoolboy remembers them, then turns away. You change objects in places or cleaning one. The task is to find changes.
    -   **Graphic dictations:** Drawing on cells for dictation ("One cage to the right, two upwards ...").
-   **For a sense of rhythm:**
    -   **Echo-chill:** You are tapping a simple rhythm, the student exactly repeats.
    -   **Marching to the music:** Step to the beat of a quick or slow melody. He trains body adjustment to the external rhythm.

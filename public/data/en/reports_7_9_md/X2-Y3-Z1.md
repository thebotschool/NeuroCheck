# Neurocheck: Cognitive profile

**Key designations:**
*   **VP:** Visual memory
*   **MP:** Interhemistered interaction
*   **UV:** Sustainable attention

This report provides a comprehensive analysis of the cognitive functions of the student, identifying individual strengths and areas for further development. It contains practical recommendations aimed at supporting effective training both at home and in the school environment.

### 1. Overview of the results

*   **Working memory (visual):** Removing accurate visual details may be a challenge. However, the initial and final elements of information are absorbed effectively.
*   **Coordination and braking:** Asymmetry was noted in productivity between the leading and unknown hands, indicating the potential for the development of interhemispheric interaction, especially in visual-motor tasks.
*   **Sustainability of attention:** A high ability to prolonged concentration on the task has been revealed. This is a key resource for learning.

### 2. The guide for the success of your student

This section is designed to help teachers and parents find an optimal approach to teaching and developing a student.

#### Key features

*   **Pace:** Thoughtful, measured. It takes time to process information, especially visual. A rush can lead to errors associated with processing, and not with inattention.
*   **Perception:** The most effectively perceives information presented clearly, step -by -step and without excessive visual elements. Audial and kinesthetic support (pronouncing, action) improves visual memorization.
*   **Focus of attention:** High. Capable of prolonged concentration under comfortable and understandable conditions. Attention is a resource.

#### Effective strategies

*   **Step -by -step instructions:** Decomposition of complex tasks for consistent stages.
*   **The principle of the region:** Focusing attention on the first and last elements in sequences (words, rules, actions) to improve memorization.
*   **Rhythm and pronouncing:** The use of auditization and rhythm (pronouncing aloud, tapping) to activate bodily memory and improve memorization.
*   **Bimanual tasks:** Activities that require the use of both hands (modeling, cutting, games with the ball) contribute to the development of interhemispheric interaction.
*   **Pure visual materials:** Preference for a simple, minimalist visual design without excessive distracting elements.

#### Areas for optimization

*   **Multitasking:** The simultaneous performance of several tasks can cause stress and decrease in productivity.
*   **Visual overload:** Complex or saturated visual design can complicate the processing of information.
*   **Frequent change of tasks:** It takes time to transition between tasks. A quick change in activities can deplete cognitive resources.
*   **Mental calculations:** Tasks without external support (records, diagrams) can be more complicated.

### 3. Recommendations

#### Home environment

*   **Organization of work:**
    *   Divide voluminous tasks into 15–20-minute intervals.
    *   Turn on short motor breaks between blocks.
    *   Recall the support of the working surface with an unaware of hand to improve interhemispheric interaction.
*   **Motivation:**
    *   Mark care and accuracy, not speed. Phrases of the type “I see your concentration” are more effective.
    *   For difficulties with similar characters (for example, b/d), use tactile methods (modeling, drawing on your back) for better memorization.

#### School environment

*   **The structure of tasks:**
    *   Use short, clear, step -by -step formulations, preferably in numbered lists.
    *   Present the tasks sequentially, one after another. Give the time to perform each step.
*   **The location of information:**
    *   Place the key data (for example, pages, homework numbers) in the upper left corner of the board for optimal visual perception.
*   **Material feed:**
    *   Apply the “See - Talk - Make” method to activate multimsenced perception.
    *   Allow the use of auxiliary products (a reading line, a finger to track examples).
    *   Consider the possibility of providing additional time for written work and the use of materials with increased font and inter -line interval.

### 4. Developing practices

The proposed exercises can be integrated into everyday life in a playful way for the development of the necessary skills.

#### Attention and visual memory

*   **Memory game:** Start with 5-6 pairs of cards, gradually increasing their amount to stimulate memory.
*   **Graphic dictations:** Exercises such as “one cage to the right, two up” develop a connection between audio perception, visual representation and motor skills.
*   **"What has changed?":** Place 5-7 items. Ask to remember their location, then change something. Ask to determine the changes.

#### Coordination and bodily control

*   **"Crossroads":** Standing or marching, touching the right elbow of the left knee, then with the left elbow - the right. Perform slowly, 1-2 minutes daily.
*   **Games with the ball:** Exercises with transferring, catching and picking the ball with both hands or alternately develop coordination.
*   **"Mirror":** The adult demonstrates the movements that the child repeats mirror (for example, raise his right hand - the child raises the left).
*   **Bimanual modeling:** Modeling plasticine or clay, requiring simultaneous work with both hands (for example, rolling two balls).

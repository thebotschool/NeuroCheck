# Cognitive profile: individual report

This report offers a deep understanding of the unique cognitive features of your student. It is designed to help in creating an optimal environment for training and development, revealing the potential through an individual approach.

## 1. Test results

### Memory and attention (CPT)
Holding information in a given sequence and prolonged concentration represent difficulties. This indicates the need to support in the development of strategies for memorization and control of attention when performing volumetric tasks.

### Self-control (Go/No-Go)
The high level of test demonstrates the strengths in the speed of the reaction and control of pulses. An excellent ability to promptly assess the situation and timely deterrence.

### Sustainability of attention (TCP)
Maintaining concentration on the same type of, monotonous tasks causes complexity. There is a tendency to rapid fatigue and distraction, which is a normal phase of development that requires attention.

## 2. Profile of a student

The data presented form a “key” to effective interaction with the unique nervous system of your student in the learning process.

### Key features

*   **The pace of processing:** Quick reactions contrast with a slower pace of assimilation of complex information. Instant answers to simple questions are combined with the need for additional time to analyze multi -stage instructions or rules.
*   **Perception format:** The best perception is achieved at **visual** submitting information (diagrams, images, cards) and its division into **Short, clear steps**. Long oral explanations are perceived less effectively.
*   **Citrilation of attention:** Attention is strong, but **short -term**. A high concentration on dynamic, interactive tasks (as in a test for self -control) is replaced by rapid exhaustion with monotonous, prolonged work (as in memory tests and attention). This is a feature of the functioning of the brain, not the lack of perseverance.

### Supporting factors

*   **Visual tips:** Lesson plan, diagrams, cards with rules, recovery stickers.
*   **Step -by -step instructions:** Breaking volumetric tasks into consistent, single actions.
*   **Structured rhythm:** Short working intervals (15–20 minutes) with regular physical or visual pauses. Using a timer.
*   **Game elements:** Transformation of routine tasks into quests, personal calls or games.

### Obstacles

*   **Multitasking:** Simultaneous instructions ("Take a notebook, open page 25 and write down the number") often lead to incomplete performance of the task.
*   **Long oral instructions:** Only the beginning and end are remembered, the middle part of the information is lost.
*   **Monotone:** Forty -five minutes of the same type of exercises cause fatigue, loss of interest and focus.
*   **Distracting factors:** Excessive visual and sound stimuli easily tug of attention.

## 3. Recommendations

### Home environment

*   **Homework performance:**
    *   Break homework into blocks for 15–20 minutes. Between the blocks - short breaks (drink, several physical exercises).
    *   Use a timer to visualize the remaining time of work, which contributes to concentration.
    *   Together, make a simple plan before the start: "1. Mathematics (2nd example). 2. Break. 3. Dictionary words."
    *   Organize a quiet workplace free from extra items, gadgets and distracting noise.
*   **Support and encouragement:**
    *   Praise for efforts and the process: “You focused on 15 minutes today!”, “It is wonderful that you yourself remembered the break!”
    *   Keep in mind that productive attention on one educational task does not exceed 15–20 minutes. Expectations should be realistic.

### School environment

*   **The wording and structure of tasks:**
    *   Tasks should be clumsy, brief and given one at a time. Make sure of understanding by asking to repeat the instructions.
    *   Important information (lesson plan, page, homework) should be placed in a prominent place - on a board or individual card.
    *   Large tasks should be divided into minor subtasks with clear criteria for execution.
*   **Subject style and organization of the lesson:**
    *   Actively use visual materials: schemes, mental cards, infographics, video.
    *   Alternate activities every 15–20 minutes (letter, oral answers, pairing, short physical education).
    *   If possible, bring the schoolboy closer to the teacher, away from distracting factors (window, door).
*   **Personalized training:**
    *   Consider the provision of cards with step -by -step plans to complete complex tasks.
    *   If necessary, increase the time to perform control work or offer them in a divided format.

## 4. Practical exercises

Simple games and exercises that contribute to the development of the necessary skills in an accessible form.

### Attention and strategy

*   **Visual dictation:** "One cage to the right, two cells up ..." - he trains auditory attention and follow the instructions.
*   **"Find the differences":** Develops concentration and observation.
*   **"Corrective test":** The task of cutting off certain symbols in the text. Start with short intervals (2-3 minutes).

### Working memory

*   **The game "Memory":** Classic game with paired cards.
*   **"Remember and draw":** Draw a simple pattern (3-4 figures) for 5-10 seconds followed by reproduction from memory.
*   **"Snowball":** The consistent addition of words to the phrase (for example, "I go on a hike and take a tent with me", then the child adds: "... and a flashlight").

### Physical coordination

*   **Games with the ball:** Throwing the ball with a simultaneous name of words in a given category (cities, animals).
*   **"FROME-OUTOMI":** Movement to music with bated breath in a certain position when it stops it.

### Rhythm and structure

*   **Rhythm-repetition:** The exact reproduction of the rhythm specified by cotton. Gradual complication.

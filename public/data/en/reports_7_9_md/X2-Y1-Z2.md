**Cognitive assessment**
**Attention. Control. Memory.**
*For the age of 7–9 years*

*Understanding the potential.* This report reveals the unique abilities of your student and offers strategies for development. Our goal is to make training and everyday life more effective and harmonious.

**1. Analysis of tests**

*   **Attention (CPT/TCP):** Focused, but short -term. Tasks requiring monotonous concentration can cause rapid fatigue and distraction.
*   **Braking (Go/No-Go):** Outstanding ability. The student effectively controls impulses, complies with the rules and acts deliberately.
*   **Visual memory:** The preservation of accurate visual details causes difficulties. However, the anchor strategy intuitively applies: information at the beginning and end is perceived better.

**2. Success Guide**

**Features of training**

*   **Pace:** The work is productive, but short impulses. Maintaining monotonous concentration for a long time causes difficulties. Energy resembles a sprint.
*   **Perception:** Information is absorbed optimally when it is represented by small, clock blocks. The first and last elements of the list, rules or instructions are best remembered.
*   **Focus:** Attention is changeable. A high concentration is possible for a short period (15–20 minutes), after which a change of activity is required. In moments of concentration, control and accuracy are maximum.

**Learning support**

*   Step -by -step instructions.
*   Visual accents (frames, color) for key information at the beginning and end.
*   Predictable rhythm: alternation of work and short breaks (by timer).
*   Clear rules and criteria for completing tasks.
*   Focus on one task at a time.

**Obstacles in learning**

*   Multitasking (listen, watch, write at the same time).
*   Long monotonous tasks (for example, the same examples or reading without a break).
*   Visual "noise" (overloaded pages of textbooks).
*   A sudden change of rules without warning.

**3. Personal recommendations**

**At home**

*   **Organization of work:**
    *   Divide homework into blocks for 15–20 minutes.
    *   Between the blocks, take 5-minute breaks for movement or rest.
    *   Use a kitchen timer to visualize intervals.
    *   Start with the most difficult or important task.
*   **Support:**
    *   Praise efforts, not just the result: "How great you were attentive today!" Or "I noticed that you did not give up when it became difficult."
    *   Mark the manifestations of self -control.
*   **Memorization strategies:**
    *   When memorizing, pay attention to the first and last lines - they serve as "anchors."
    *   Use cards: one card is one concept.

**At school**

*   **Instructions:**
    *   Provide the instructions sequentially, one step. For example: "First read the text. Ready? Now find the answer to question 1."
    *   Oral instructions should be brief and clear.
*   **Materials:**
    *   Visually select key information on the board or in handouts (frame, color).
    *   Use numbered or marked lists instead of continuous text.
*   **Organization:**
    *   Place the schoolchild in the first or second desk, away from distracting factors (window, door).
    *   Turn on short "switches" of activity every 15–20 minutes (physical education, oral survey, pairs).

**4. Targeted training**

The proposed games and exercises develop key skills in an exciting form.

*   **For attention:**
    *   **Graphic dictations:** They develop the retention of instructions and concentration ("Two cells to the right, one cell up ...").
    *   **Correct samples:** Find and cross out all the letters "A" in the text. Start with 3-5 minutes.
*   **For visual memory:**
    *   **The game "Memory":** Start with 4-6 pairs of cards, gradually increasing their number.
    *   **"Remember and draw":** Show a card with 3-4 simple figures for 10 seconds. Then ask to reproduce them from memory in the same order.
*   **For coordination and control (strong side):**
    *   **"FROME-OUTOMI":** He trains a quick reaction and braking.
    *   **RIGHT RIGHTS:** Repetition of rhythmic patterns (cotton, table) improves the connection of hearing and motor skills.
*   **For self -control:**
    *   **Board games-"Walking":** They teach to observe the rules, wait for the queue and control emotions.

**The cognitive profile of the student**
Age group: 7–9 years old

This report reveals the unique cognitive features of your student. The goal is to identify strengths and designate areas for development, creating an optimal environment for training and growth. It is based on the results of cognitive tests.

---

### **1. Key results**

*   **Visual working memory.** Effectively remembers visual information. However, the preservation of the exact sequence of elements requires development.
*   **Control and braking.** Confident control of the leading hand. Tasks requiring the coordination of both hands (and cerebral hemispheres) can be a challenge.
*   **Sustainability of attention.** High and stable concentration. The ability to work in optimal conditions.

<!-- page-break -->

### **2. The guide for the success of your student**

This guide will help make the learning process more smooth and productive.

#### **Individual profile**

*   **Pace.** Prefers a measured, consistent pace. It takes time to comprehend each stage of the task.
*   **Perception.** Optimally perceives visual, step -by -step instructions. Long oral explanations can be difficult.
*   **Focus.** High concentration on one task. Attention is effective with a clear structure and clarity of the assignment.

#### **Which contributes to progress**

*   Clear step -by -step instructions.
*   Visual tools: checklists, schemes, color release.
*   Rhythmic exercises (tapping) for synchronization.
*   Preliminary knowledge of the structure of the lesson or task.

#### **What creates challenges**

*   Multitasking (listen and write down at the same time).
*   Long oral instructions (more than 3-4 actions).
*   Hurry and temporary restrictions.
*   Lack of structure, noise, fast change of activity.

<!-- page-break -->

### **3. Development strategies**

#### **Home environment**

*   **Organization of work**
    *   Miss tasks into short blocks (15–20 minutes) with interruptions.
    *   Together, create a work plan (for example, a checklist). Mark the executed points for a sensation of control.
    *   Use drafts for complex tasks: draw diagrams, try options without fear of errors.
*   **Support and motivation**
    *   Encourage the process, not just the result: "You made efforts!", "You follow the plan!"
    *   If difficult, ask the leading questions: "What is the next step?", "Check the scheme?" Instead of a direct answer.

#### **School environment**

*   **The formulation of tasks**
    *   The instructions should be short, cherished, step -by -step. Divide complex indications of simple commands.
    *   Duplicate oral instructions visually (on the board, cards).
*   **Organization of the lesson**
    *   Put on the desk a lesson plan or an algorithm of actions (for example, to solve problems).
    *   Use methods that combine a hearing with simple rhythmic actions (pops, stomping) to activate the hemispheres.
*   **Individual approach**
    *   The targeted development of planning and self -control (regulatory UUD) is recommended.
    *   Consultation of a neuropsychologist or Tutor support can be useful for the formation of these skills.

### **4. Recommended practices**

These exercises will help to develop the necessary skills in an exciting form.

*   **Development of attention** (maintaining the strength)
    *   Board games: "Find 10 differences", "cortex", labyrinths.
    *   Corrective tests: deleting certain letters in the text.

*   **Coordination and interhemispheric interaction**
    *   **Neurohymnastics:** "Fist-Robric Ladon", "Ear-Shop" (daily).
    *   **Drawing with two hands:** Symmetric figures in the air or on paper.
    *   **Juggling:** Start with light scarves or bags.

*   **Memory for a sequence**
    *   **"What first, then what?":** Restoring the order of plot pictures (3-4).
    *   **"Repeat me":** Reproduction of a pattern of chips/cubes after short viewing.
    *   **Graphic dictations:** "One cage to the right, two up ..."

*   **Rhythm and bodily coordination**
    *   Tapping and repetition of rhythmic drawings.
    *   Games with the ball: fighting off the floor or wall in a given rhythm.

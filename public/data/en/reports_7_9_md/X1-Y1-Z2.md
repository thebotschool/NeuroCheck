# The cognitive profile of the student

This report offers a detailed analysis of cognitive abilities and practical recommendations. It is developed to help parents and teachers to maximize the student’s potential.

---

### 1. Evaluation. Test results

*   **Visual memory.** The schoolboy demonstrates excellent abilities for short -term memorization of visual information. This includes sequences of actions, images and instructions. Memory is its strong side.
*   **Self -control. Braking.** High level of self -control. The schoolboy effectively follows the rules and is able to ponder actions before the answer. This indicates a developed ability to plan and regulation of behavior.
*   **Attention. Sustainability.** Preserving attention on the same type or long -term tasks is the main growth zone. The schoolboy can quickly get tired and distracted, especially with monotonous work.

**Key conclusion:** Excellent basic cognitive functions (memory, self -control) are combined with rapid fatigue of attention on long or repeated tasks. The focus of attention is not reduced due to unwillingness, but due to fatigue.

### 2. Guide for success. Individual approach

#### Peculiarities. Individual profile

*   **Pace.** The energetic start of work with subsequent slowdown during fatigue from monotony. Strong start and support for completion are important.
*   **Perception.** Mostly visual. Schemes, images, short texts, highlighted keywords are better absorbed. Long oral explanations are less effective.
*   **Focus of attention.** High concentration on short, interesting, understandable tasks. Attention is easily switched if the task becomes long, boring or unclear.

#### Support. Effective strategies

*   **Crushing of tasks.** Divide large tasks into 2-3 small, specific steps.
*   **Visual tips.** Use memos, algorithms, diagrams. For example: "1. Read. 2. Emphasize. 3. Write down."
*   **Change of activity.** Alternate 10-15 minutes of mental work with 3-5 minutes of physical activity or another task.
*   **Predictability.** A clear plan of a lesson or homework helps to concentrate and saves a resource of attention.
*   **Involvement.** Active participation (questions, work at the board) supports attention.

#### Calls. Which interferes with the process

*   **Multitasking.** Simultaneous listening, letter and observation quickly deplete the resource.
*   **Long monologues.** Oral instructions for more than 20-30 seconds without visual support.
*   **Monotone.** Large volumes of the same type of tasks.
*   **Unstructure.** A chaotic change of tasks without logic and pauses.
*   **Distracting factors.** Noise, items on the table, the view from the window easily disperses attention.

### 3. Action. Practical solutions

#### At home. Organization of space

*   **Homework.**
    *   Miss tasks into 15–20-minute blocks with 5-minute breaks. Use the timer.
    *   Make a simple plan: "First mathematics (15 minutes), then a break, then reading (15 minutes)."
    *   The workplace should be ordered, without unnecessary items.
*   **Promotion.**
    *   Praise for efforts, and not just for the result: "You have focused perfectly for 15 minutes!", "It's great that you yourself found a mistake!"
    *   Be realistic: productive attention to the educational task lasts about 10-15 minutes. Plan pauses.
*   **Communication.** Speak briefly and clearly. Instead of complex instructions, break it into pieces: "Now we are going to clean the toys. The timer is 10 minutes." Then the next instruction.

#### At school. Educational process

*   **The learning process.**
    *   **Placement.** Optimal-closer to the teacher (1-2 desks), further from the window/door to minimize abstracts.
    *   **The structure of the lesson.** It is advisable to build a lesson with blocks for 10-15 minutes, alternating activities (listen, write, answer, physical education).
*   **Material and tasks.**
    *   **Instructions.** Formulate briefly and clearly. One sentence and a visual example on the board. Ask the student to repeat the instructions.
    *   **Innings.** Use interactive, dynamic style. Ask short questions, involve in the process (erase from the boards, distribute notebooks).
*   **Personalization.**
    *   Offer tasks on separate cards with a smaller volume of text and "air".
    *   Allow me to use a line for reading on lines or an empty sheet to close the completed tasks.
    *   Use non -verbal signals (for example, a colored token) to remind you of the task.

### 4. Development. Recommended training

These games and exercises will help to develop attention and self -control. The recommended duration of classes is 10-15 minutes a day.

*   **Attention.**
    *   **Graphic dictations.** "2 cells to the right, 1 cage up ..." They train auditory attention and retention of the instructions.
    *   **Corrective tests.** "Find and cross out all the letters A." Start with small volumes, gradually increase.
    *   **"Find 10 differences."** A classic game for the development of concentration and visual attention.
*   **Coordination. Control.**
    *   **Games with the ball.** Bowing the ball from the floor or wall, alternating hands.
    *   **"Forbidden movement."** Repetition of movements, except for one "forbidden". Develops braking.
*   **Memory. Rhythm.**
    *   **The game "Memory".** Search for paired cards. Develops visual working memory.
    *   **Rhythmic exercises.** Repeating the sequence of cotton or tributaries. Teaches concentration and memorization of sequences.

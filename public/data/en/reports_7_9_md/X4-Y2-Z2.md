**Assessment of cognitive abilities**

This report reveals the strengths and areas for the development of your student. Recommendations are designed to support his successful training and harmonious development.

### **1. A brief report for each test**

*   **Sustainability of attention (CPT):** Long -term concentration on one task causes difficulties. Fatual fatigue and distraction is possible.
*   **Self-control (Go/No-Go):** There is a tendency to impulsive reactions. The development of the ability to think about action is required.
*   **Visual working memory:** Removing the information is fragmentary. The implementation of multi -step instructions and compliance with strict sequence cause complexity. Lack of a systematic approach.

<!-- page-break -->

### **2. The guide for the success of your student**

#### Key features

*   **Pace:** A measured pace with the possibility of pauses is required. Hurry provokes errors, reducing effectiveness.
*   **Perception format:** Information is best absorbed when it is visually and step -by -step. Long oral instructions are ineffective.
*   **Focus of attention:** Productive attention intervals are 10-15 minutes. After that, a short reboot or change of activity is required.

#### Supporting factors

*   Visual supports: checklists, “first-point” schemes, rules with rules, reminders.
*   Crushing of tasks: Large tasks should be divided into 2-3 small, understandable steps.
*   Pronouncing aloud: helps to keep the instructions and prevent impulsive actions.
*   A clear rhythm and predictability: timers and schedule reduce anxiety, improving concentration.
*   Tactile stimulation: the use of anti-stress objects during explanations can contribute to concentration.

#### Factors that reduce efficiency

*   Multivativity: simultaneously fulfilling several tasks, translates the working memory.
*   Long oral instructions: instructions from three or more consistent actions are difficult to perceive.
*   Noise and distracting factors: distracting noise and visual disorder reduce attention.
*   Monotony: prolonged completion of the same type of tasks leads to rapid fatigue.
*   Time pressure: phrases, rushing a child, provoke impulsiveness and interfere with the thought process.

<!-- page-break -->

### **3. Recommendations**

#### Home environment

*   **Homework organization:**
    *   Use the “tomato” technique: 15 minutes of work, 5 minutes break (drinking, light exercise).
    *   Make a visual plan for the evening: 1. Have dinner. 2. Mathematics (15 min). 3. Rest. 4. Reading (15 min).
    *   Before starting classes, remove all unnecessary items from the table.
*   **Support and encouragement:**
    *   Encourage the process and efforts, and not just the result.
    *   Instead of a direct question about lessons, ask about difficulties. Discuss them together.
*   **Mercing of attention:**
    *   Continuous work is effective within 10-15 minutes. Several short approaches with breaks are preferred.

#### School environment

*   **The wording of tasks:**
    *   Submit the instructions step by step, with pauses to perform each step.
    *   Accompany oral instructions with a schematic recording on the board.
*   **Lesson structuring:**
    *   Sound and write down the lesson plan at the beginning: check d/s, new topic, exercises.
    *   Change the activities regularly: letter, discussion, physical education, work with visual materials.
*   **Class placement:**
    *   The optimal place: the first or second desk, the center of the class, away from distracting factors (windows, doors).
*   **Material feed style:**
    *   Use interactive elements: cards, magnets, the possibility of active participation.
    *   Give short, specific instructions to involve the child in the process.
*   **Individual educational route:**
    *   Consider the introduction of a personal checklist on the desk or the visual schedule of the day.

### **4. Recommended training**

The presented exercises are easy to integrate into everyday life. They develop the necessary skills without special equipment.

*   **For attention:**
    *   "Corrective test": the search for certain symbols or words in the text for a while.
    *   Board games: “Volble”, “Drum”, “Find the differences” - for the development of reaction and concentration.
*   **For memory and strategy:**
    *   “Memory” / “Find a couple”: a classic game for visual working memory.
    *   “I put it in a bag ...”: an oral game for the development of auditory -making memory.
    *   Drawing up instructions: creation of step -by -step instructions for simple actions (for example, how to brew tea).
*   **For bodily control and braking of pulses:**
    *   “The sea is worried once”: trains the ability to stop and freeze.
    *   Rhythmic games: repetition of rhythms (pops, stomping) with gradual complication.
    *   Respiratory exercises: Slow inhale-exhaust (for example, “inflate a ball”) for concentration and reassurance.
    *   The game “Yes, and not say”: develops cognitive control and the ability to follow the rules.

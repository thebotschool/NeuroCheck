# Cognitive test report
## Age: 7–9 years

### 1. Review of testing

**Visual working memory**
High visual memory. The child effectively remembers and reproduces visual information (pictures, diagrams), preserving it even after distraction.

**Go/No-go (braking)**
Expressed self -control. Confidently controls his actions, following clear rules. The ability to concentrate and timely stop is developed.

**Sustainability of attention (TCP)**
The tendency to impulsiveness. In conditions of monotonous or long -term tasks, the child can rush, reacting to complete thinking.

**Key aspects**
There is a strong potential for self -control and excellent visual memory. However, with monotonous tasks or the need for prolonged concentration, internal braking mechanisms can weaken.

### 2. The guide for the success of your student

**Individual characteristics**

*   **Pace.** Uneven. It can work thoughtfully, but with a loss of interest or fatigue, impulsiveness is manifested. Prefers the quick performance of methodical work.
*   **Perception.** Expressed visual. It better assimilates information through the images: diagrams, drawings, checklists, color accents, short videos. Oral instructions without visual support can be less effective.
*   **Focus of attention.** Effective on short, understandable and interesting tasks. With routine or long work, attention can be dissipated. The main task is to maintain concentration throughout the process.

**Supporting factors**

*   **Visual supports.** Check lines with the stages of execution, timers (especially graphic), schemes of action, highlighting keywords.
*   **Crushing of tasks.** Separation of large tasks into small, step -by -step stages reduces pressure and prevents a rush.
*   **Clear rituals.** Repeating actions at the beginning and end of the work help to tune in and complete the process. Example: starting the timer, check on the list.
*   **Short breaks.** Regular short pauses (2-3 minutes) for physical activity or a change in activity help restore concentration.

**Distracting factors**

*   **Multitasking.** Simultaneous listening, observation and recording can lead to overload and errors.
*   **Long oral instructions.** The child assimilates the beginning and the end, losing details in the middle.
*   **Monotone.** Long-term completion of the same type of tasks reduces quality and leads to errors due to rush.
*   **Noise.** Distracting objects, loud conversations or background noise significantly reduce productivity.

### 3. Recommendations

#### For the house

**Organization of homework**

*   **Visual plan.** Use a board or paper to draw up a plan for the evening. Example: "Dinner. Mathematics (15 min). Break (5 min). Reading."
*   **Timer.** Set the timer for 15-20 minutes. This is the optimal time of productive work on one task. After the signal - a mandatory short break.
*   **Priority.** Start homework with the most difficult or least favorite items, when the level of energy and concentration is maximum.

**Support and promotion**

*   **Praise the process.** Mark efforts and following the strategy: “I see how you tried not to rush!”, “Great, you checked your work!”, “Well done, you followed the plan.”
*   **Constructive dialogue.** Avoid negative phrases. Instead of "Why is it dirty again?" Or "You hurried again!" Ask: “Let's see what can be improved?”, “It seems that this step was difficult. How to help you?”

#### For school

**The formulation of tasks**

*   **Short instructions.** Let's clearly, step by step. Duplicate oral instructions on the board or card.
*   **Example.** Divide the command "Open the notebooks, retreat two cells, write down the number, cool work and solve the problem number five from the page twenty" for several short teams with pauses.

**Lesson structure and space organization**

*   **Place in the classroom.** Optimal: the first or second desk, in the center, to minimize distracting factors.
*   **Visual schedule.** The lesson plan on the board helps the child to navigate and reduces anxiety.
*   **Individual support.**
    *   Suggest a checklist to verify the completed task (for example: “He emphasized spelling?”, “Checked the calculations?”).
    *   Allow me to use a simple visual timer on the desk.
    *   Agree on a non -verbal signal if the child loses concentration (for example, a light touch).

### 4. Recommended training

These games and exercises develop self -control, attention and coordination in a fascinating form.

**For attention and self -control**

*   **Games for a stop.** "Fast!" Or "the sea is worried once" - an instant stop at a signal.
*   **"Edible-universal."** He trains the reaction speed and the ability to slow down the action in time.
*   **Graphic dictations.** Develop auditory attention, following instructions and spatial orientation.

**For coordination and bodily control**

*   **Games with the ball.** Catch the ball after a certain number of cotton; Do not catch on a certain word.
*   **"Labyrinth on paper."** Conducting a line with a pencil without touching the walls requires the concentration and control of fine motor skills.

**For memory and rhythm**

*   **"Repeat me."** Repetition of the sequence of cotton, tapping or movements. Start with simple rhythms, gradually complicating.
*   **Board game "Find a couple" (Memory).** It perfectly trains visual memory, which is the strength of the child.

## Cognitive profile report
### Assessment of the ability of a student (age: 7–9 years)

This report is designed to reveal the strengths and development zones of your student. The recommendations presented will help create an optimal environment for its successful training and growth.

---

## 1. Evaluation results

*   **Visual working memory:** The schoolboy effectively remembers information (for example, images), but experiences difficulties with its ordered reproduction. This indicates the difficulties in organizing sequential actions.
*   **Self-control and inhibition (Go/No-Go):** Great ability to follow the rules and stop in time. This strong side manifests itself in structured situations, demonstrating good impulse control.
*   **Sustainability of attention.** With prolonged, monotonous or multicomponent tasks, the concentration and reaction rate decrease.

### General conclusion

The student demonstrates high potential for self -control, realized in conditions of a clear structure. Long or multitask exercises exhaust attention, leading to errors in the organization.

## 2. The guide for the success of your student

### Individual characteristics

*   **Pace:** Needs a measured pace. Rush leads to disorganization; Too slow pace reduces the focus. The optimal short, focused work intervals are optimal.
*   **Information perception:** Better assimilates visual and step -by -step instructions. Abstract or long oral explanations are less effective.
*   **Focus of attention:** Attention is strong, but limited in time (10-15 minutes). It is depleted with prolonged work. Sensitive to external distracting factors and internal multitasking (simultaneous listening, recording, understanding).

### Which helps

*   **Step -by -step instructions:** Tasks divided into simple, consistent steps. Example: "1. Open the notebook. 2. Write down the number. 3. Miss two cells."
*   **Visual supports:** Check lists, algorithms on cards, highlighting keywords with color, timers for monitoring time.
*   **Clear structure:** A predictable lesson plan or a sequence of homework helps a student to tune in and control himself.
*   **Rhythm:** Short working segments (15–20 minutes) with physical breaks (jump, stretch) more effective than long sessions.

### What interferes

*   **Multitasking:** Simultaneous actions (listen, watch, write down) lead to a loss of information.
*   **Long oral instructions:** Only the beginning or end is remembered, the middle is lost.
*   **Unstructured environment:** Chaos at the workplace, excess items, noise reduce productivity.
*   **Fast change of tasks:** Without a clear completion of the previous one.

## 3. Recommendations

### For the house

*   **Organization of work:**
    *   Divide the tasks into parts. Instead of a general request to "make mathematics", offer: "We will decide these two examples, then we will take a break."
    *   Use the timer. Set the agreement: "15 minutes of work, 5 minutes of rest." This helps to visualize time and hold the focus.
    *   Make a visual plan. Write or draw a sequence of affairs: 1. Lunch, 2. One task in mathematics, 3. Game, 4. Read the page.
*   **Support and motivation:**
    *   Mark the process, not just the result. Example: “I see how you followed the plan!”, “You lasted perfectly for 15 minutes without being distracted!”
    *   Help with the organization, but do not work for the student. Ask: "Where do we start? What do we do first, then what? Let's write down."

### For school

*   **The wording of tasks:**
    *   The instructions should be short, cherished, step -by -step. Duplicate oral instructions on a board or card.
    *   Attract the attention of the student (eye contact, light touch) before giving instructions.
*   **Lesson structuring:**
    *   At the beginning of the lesson, briefly voice the plan: "Today we will check your homework, write a dictation, then play the words."
    *   Place the student closer to the teacher and the board, away from the sources of distraction (window, door, rear desks).
*   **Individual approach:**
    *   It is recommended that the use of algorithms cards (for example, "plan to analyze a word in composition").
    *   Allow me to use a line for tracking a line when reading.
    *   In the control work, the tasks can be crushed and issued sequentially, and not all at once.

## 4. Developing activity

The proposed games and exercises contribute to the development of key skills.

*   **Attention and following the instructions:**
    *   **Graphic dictations:** "Two cells to the right, one up ..." - training of concentration and holding the sequence.
    *   **Game "Find 10 differences":** Develops focused, detailed attention.
*   **Coordination and bodily control:**
    *   **Games "Traffic Light" or "The Sea is worried once":** Develop the ability to quickly respond to a signal and stop movement.
    *   **Ball exercises:** Throwing the ball with additional tasks (for example, name the word on the letter, solve an example).
*   **Memory and sequences:**
    *   **Board games "Memory":** Search for paired pictures, games to repetition of sequences of actions or sounds.
    *   **Compilation of stories according to the series of pictures:** Develops building logical and temporary chains.
*   **Rhythm:**
    *   **Cooling/checking rhythms:** The adult sets the rhythm, the student repeats. Gradual complication. Develops the organization of information in time.
